Efficient market, momentum, mean reversion...

No math. High priority knowledge!

In the following post, I’m going to introduce the Efficient Market Hypothesis and the empirical deviations from it. I will explain what momentum is, introduce the concept of mean reversion—all to help you understand a century of observations on how the market behaves.

HISTORY, BACKGROUNDThe relationship between supply and demand and its impact on prices is one of the most fundamental questions in trade, business, and economics.Modern money and capital markets exist to direct capital to parts of the economy where it is needed for production. Households, as the largest savers, invest their unused income through capital markets into companies and governments that lack funds. Like buyers in commodity markets, these investors carefully select their investments and expect comprehensive information on each opportunity. Naturally, they want the highest return for the risk assumed, whereas capital users seek the lowest-cost funding.

For markets to function well, participants must establish systems and rules for providing and using information. Two practical questions arise. First, from the investor’s perspective: how can I predict which investment will generate the highest return? Well, if we knew how an asset’s price would change, it would open the door to substantial profits. Second, from the capital user’s viewpoint: Under what conditions can I issue or sell securities—be they equity or debt—at the highest possible price? Well, this question leads back to the first, since company managers also wonder whether their securities’ prices will rise or fall in the future.

A race began to develop methods for forecasting market price changes.Everyone sought as much information as possible about the instrument, its issuer, and its environment. Those with an informational edge enjoyed higher returns, but their success was short-lived because once others noticed the excess return potential, they followed suit. According to supply and demand, prices rose, and the excess return vanished.

This illustrates how information is incorporated into prices: the more traders monitor each other, the faster profit opportunities disappear. Ultimately, with many market participants, information can be priced in almost immediately. We also see that everyone interpreted the new information in a similar way, leading them all to buy. Thus, the market functioned efficiently in conveying and using the information. The speed of this process is crucial: the winner in the competition for information can earn excess returns. Examining market efficiency, therefore, seeks to determine whether it is possible to find information that yields excess returns, and if so, where that information might come from.

First models”I can calculate the motion of heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people” (Sir Isaac Newton after lost all of his money in a 1720s stock market bubble)

Economics is a social science, yet the belief in the predictability of asset prices (with continously changing probability levels) has driven researchers to develop mathematical models to describe market movements. This effort has been supported by 20th-century advancements in probability theory and stochastic processes, drawing on the work of Kolmogorov, Wiener, and Einstein.

The first known and significant work in this field was written by Louis Bachelier in the early 1900s, utilizing probability theory. He was the first who laid the foundations of option pricing and mapped out the profit functions of futures contracts, options, and complex option positions.His fundamental assumption was that price movements are influenced by an enormous number of factors, some of which may counteract each other. (remember my article about probability: here) He also stated that different investors can interpret the same information in opposite ways, meaning that a particular event or news can simultaneously attract both buyers and sellers to the market.As a result, at any given moment, the collective expectations of investors neither indicate a price increase nor a decrease, provided that there are equal numbers of buyers and sellers. Investors willing to sell at a given price do not anticipate further price appreciation, whereas buyers do. If market sentiment changes, prices adjust accordingly.If we define expected return as the product of the probability of a price increase and the magnitude of the increase, then in such a market, the expected return for investors is zero. Bachelier referred to this as a ‘fair play’.I will explore the concept of a "fair play" in a separate post.

In this post here I’ve already covered the concept of “Martingale process”. What Bachelier essentially said is that the expected return is equivalent to the realized return over the long run. However, it also means that if market prices reflect equilibrium prices, meaning they incorporate all available information, then no trading strategy can generate returns exceeding the risk-adjusted expected return. The definition of a martingale process is more than this: given a set of available information, the expected future return over the next period is equal to the realized return from the previous period. In this framework, the asset price follows a submartingale process.

Brownian motion was named after the Scottish botanist Robert Brown, who observed through a microscope that pollen particles suspended in water exhibited random movement.

I’ve already demonstrated in the linked post how Einstein later modeled this motion mathematically. The key takeaway for now is that, in this model, Brownian motion is a Markov process, meaning that the past values of the variable have no impact on its future evolution; only the present value does. In other words, the past positions of a particle does not provide any information about its future trajectory. Later, the mathematician Norbert Wiener formally proved the existence of a stochastic process that fits Einstein’s description, now known as Wiener process or ‘standard Brownian motion’.

Brownian motion became the foundation for modeling financial market movements, after Maurice Kendall’s research, published in 1953. The paper is titled “The Analysis of Economic Time-Series”. You can access it here.He analyzed twenty-two time series, including stocks from various industries as well as wheat and cotton commodity prices, in an attempt to identify recurring cycles and construct a predictive model based on them.Kendall found that fitting any kind of trend to the time series was highly questionable. Additionally, he provided both theoretical justification and empirical evidence showing that the movement of aggregated indices appeared to be more structured than that of their individual components. This was because the short-term idiosyncratic fluctuations of individual stocks and commodities tended to cancel out at the aggregate level.Perhaps the most influential conclusion of his work was that stock prices exhibit minimal serial correlation, suggesting that, given the information available in the market, price movements are difficult to predict beyond a one-week horizon.This also implies that the underlying assumption is that market returns follow a normal distribution (as also discussed in the linked post). Thus, if we accept that consecutive price changes are independent and identically distributed, then over a longer time horizon, their distribution converges to a normal distribution.

After this publication, the theory faced continuous criticism and scrutiny. Moore and Osborne found that log returns follow a log-normal distribution. Osborne explained this using the Weber–Fechner law, which says that human perception does not register absolute quantities, distances, or differences, but rather perceives them in relative terms (including in the case of stock returns).

In 1962, Paul Cootner introduced the idea of a price range within which professional investors do not interfere with prices. He suggested that prices move freely within this range as long as professionals refrain from trading.His reasoning was that acquiring market information is costly for non-professional investors, and their risk tolerance is generally lower compared to professional investors. However, since each professional investor incurs different costs when acquiring information, a competitive market for information emerges.Cootner argued that as soon as an asset's price reaches a level where the potential excess return exceeds the cost of acquiring information, professional investors immediately enter the market. However, since new information arrives randomly, both in the market and to professional investors, prices exhibit a random walk between trend shifts. As trend changes themselves also occur randomly, the entire process cannot be classified as a pure random walk.Within each trend, as prices approach the edges of the range, we can expect negative autocorrelation, as the likelihood of a counter-movement increases. Additionally, the distribution of price changes is expected to be more peaked between trend shifts, as extreme price swings are less likely within this range.Cootner was able to empirically validate these assumptions, but he could not explain the long-term return distribution (which in his calculations became platykurtic).

In 1963, Benoît Mandelbrot challenged the assumption of normal distribution, which—let us recall—is the foundation of the entire market random walk model. Instead, he applied the Lévy-Pareto distribution.He observed that fat tails occur more frequently than traditionally assumed and that statisticians often discard extreme fluctuations to ensure that returns can be well-described by a normal distribution—a clear example of confirmation bias.

In 1963, Eugene Fama emphasized that under the distribution proposed by Mandelbrot, speculators cannot effectively protect themselves against large losses using stop-loss orders. In a fat-tailed distribution, large negative swings occur less frequently, but their magnitude is significantly greater than that of more frequent, smaller losses. Since price crashes can happen suddenly and with extreme severity, there is little opportunity for defense (paper here).In his 1965 paper, Fama provided further evidence that stock prices do not follow a normal distribution but instead conform to a stable Pareto distribution. One of the key assumptions of the Pareto distribution is independence, which he tested using multiple methods on his dataset. Although he detected minimal serial correlation, he concluded that these correlations were not statistically significant and, more importantly, far too weak to support any viable trading strategy. And with this, we arrive at the Efficient Market Hypothesis…

THE EFFICIENT MARKET HYPOTHESIS You've all probably heard the saying, "there is no free lunch." In financial markets, this means that no investor can consistently outperform the indexes without taking on higher risk.Fama's central idea and starting point were as follows: for a market to be considered efficient, the price of a given asset must fully reflect all available market information. Investors compete for even the most seemingly insignificant pieces of new information, and as soon as someone obtains and acts on it, it immediately gets incorporated into prices.In a market where these conditions hold, the instantaneous adjustment of prices ensures that no investment strategy based on new information can generate excess profits.The possibility of excess returns can arise in the presence of informational inefficiencies, which is the main driving force behind the competition for information mentioned in the introduction.Outside of financial markets, there is hardly any other market where competition is this clearly observable and intense. This pushes prices down and minimizes potential profits.In short: in an efficient market, price movements cannot be predicted if all public data and the expectations of all market participants are already incorporated into prices.

The theory states that market participants trade based on rational decision-making. According to the theory, the assumption of rational expectations holds because the opposing decisions of irrational investors cancel each other out, resulting in a net long-term effect of zero.The theory suggests that market instruments are priced based on some intrinsic value, which is essentially the present value of the asset’s expected future cash flows.Fama acknowledges that market prices do not necessarily match intrinsic value, as in an uncertain world, intrinsic value is not universally known—different investors may assign different values to the same asset. This variation in perceived intrinsic value can be treated as market noise (Muth, 1961). Therefore, Fama does not see this as conflicting with the random walk hypothesis or its assumption of independence.

He categorized empirical tests of efficiency into weak, semi-strong, and strong forms: The weak form says that all past price movements and historical data are already reflected in current prices. This implies that no excess returns can be achieved by analyzing past price patterns. (Excess return should be understood as the return achieved per unit of risk.)The semi-strong form says that all publicly available information is already incorporated into market prices. This includes macroeconomic and microeconomic factors, as well as insights gained from observing a company’s operations and financials. Therefore, neither past price data nor public information can be used to achieve excess returns.The strong form asserts that even fundamental analysis and access to non-public (insider) information do not allow investors to generate excess profits.

Trading strategy based on EMHAs a conclusion of the theory, the best strategy is passive investing, meaning that instead of stock-picking, one should invest in low-cost index funds or ETFs that track broad market indices and offset risks with bonds or commodities.This strategy is based on the "buy and hold" approach, meaning that we build a so-called ‘lazy portfolio’. The core idea is that, since the theory suggests that market returns cannot be consistently outperformed, the best approach for an investor is to minimize costs.Now, the problem with such portfolios is that, in some cases, investors do not properly adjust the proportion of hedging positions or fail to determine correlations accurately. Investment risk can be reduced using low-correlation assets; however, correlation itself fluctuates over time.

Between 2000 and 2022, the average stock-bond correlation was -0.31, meaning it was inversely correlated, which effectively allowed bond positions to reduce equity exposure risk. However, in the previous thirty years, the average stock-bond correlation was 0.35, meaning it was positive. A 2023 study (link here) using simulations demonstrated that, during the 1970–1999 period of positive correlation, investors had to take on an average risk of 10.4% to achieve the same returns that could be attained with 8.5% risk in the 2000–2022 period, using a 60/40 portfolio.

Another issue is that many investors mistakenly project future returns based on solely historical average returns. For example, if we examine a 100-year period, the S&P 500's average return is typically between 9-10%, with a standard deviation of approximately 15%. However, there have been 20-year periods where the average return was significantly lower. The problem lies in the fact that averaging conceals important information. If, in addition to calculating the average return, we also determine what percentage of individual periods had positive or negative returns, we obtain a more nuanced perspective. For instance, if a 10-year period has an average return of 5%, but 57% of the time it had negative returns, that presents a very different risk profile compared to a scenario where the positive return rate was 72%.At the same time, the picture is further refined when comparing average returns to each other. For example, if the average return in a given month was 2%, this carries a very different weight depending on whether the rolling average yearly return was 1%, 1.8%, or 3%. In the first case, the monthly average return was twice the yearly average. In the second case, it was only 1.11 times the yearly average. While in the third case, it was just 0.67 times the yearly average. (This is something I like to use when measuring momentum and weighting different data points in formulas. Also I like to compare the relative AUC of the ROC to each other on different timeframes.)

There are several issues with the buy-and-hold strategy. For example, not all stock markets can be expected to experience continuous growth. A great example of this is the Japanese Nikkei index, which only managed to surpass its 1989 peak in 2024.

Siegel's observation that over the past 200 years, U.S. equities have significantly outperformed the bond market. The Nobel-prize winner economist Robert Shiller raised two key objections to Professor Siegel's findings:Firstly, Siegel's study examines 30-year time horizons, but returns over shorter periods, such as 10–20 years, can differ significantly.Secondly, the 20th century was the most successful era in the relatively short history of the stock market, which does not guarantee that the same trend will continue in the 21st century.

Another issue is that Professor Siegel estimated bond market returns in his book based on Homer (1963) and Macaulay (1938).Macaulay calculated U.S. interest rates, stock prices, and bond yields starting from 1857. Researchers generally test strategies from 1926 onward, as the Ibbotson database contains detailed data only from that year. Any data prior to this relies on Macaulay’s calculations.This was also the case with Sidney Homer in 1963, who calculated bond market returns based on Macaulay’s data instead of deriving them from actual bond price fluctuations. These two studies became the foundation of Professor Siegel’s research.The problem with this approach was that in the second half of the 19th century, bond market returns were 1.5% higher than what Siegel accounted for, and the effect of compound interest further magnified this discrepancy (see below).

This means that in the 19th century, inflation-adjusted real returns of bonds and equities moved together, and stocks did not outperform at all.The chart below shows that bond market returns closely followed stock market returns and, in some periods, even outperformed them.

After 1942, the U.S. stock market indeed began to soar relative to bonds, but this trend only lasted until 1982.

Between 1982 and 2013, stock outperformance fluctuated, and by the end of the period, the real wealth of both portfolios was equal.When considering the Sharpe ratio—which measures excess return per unit of risk—the bond market edge is significant. This was demonstrated by Professor F. McQuarrie in his 2019 study, linked here.

Anomalies When researchers at the University of Chicago created the CRSP database in the 1970s—which includes both price data and financial information on companies—new opportunities emerged for backtesting various relationships. Through these backtests, numerous market anomalies were discovered that the Efficient Market Hypothesis fails to explain. Today, over 150 such anomalies have been identified, but I will only list the most historically significant and basic ones.

Beta anomaly: Beta measures the riskiness of a stock relative to an index, indicating how much more or less volatile an individual stock is compared to the overall market.The Efficient Market Hypothesis states "no pain, no gain," meaning that risk and return should be positively correlated: higher returns can only be achieved by taking on greater risk. This principle is also reflected in the well-known original CAPM, which states that:

Means, the expected return of a given stock (or portfolio) Rᵢ consists of the risk-free rate and the equity risk premium (the stock market return minus the risk-free rate), with the risk premium weighted by beta.However, the CAPM formula was found to inaccurately estimate returns, leading Fama and French to revise the model by incorporating the size and value premia. Later, the profitability premium and the asset growth effect were also introduced. (This acknowledged the size, value, and asset growth effects as significant factors in asset pricing.)In 1972, Fischer Black created a market-neutral portfolio, where he took long positions in low-beta stocks and hedged them with short positions in high-beta stocks. Black’s portfolio was able to generate excess returns, which contradicted both Fama’s hypothesis and the CAPM model. Later, Andrea Frazzini further examined this phenomenon by comparing leveraged 40/60 portfolios with unleveraged 10/90 portfolios.The results showed that investing in a 10/90 portfolio with high-beta assets yielded a lower risk-adjusted return than investing in a leveraged 40/60 portfolio with low-beta stocks. (Research link here)This suggests that the positive correlation assumption between risk and return is not perfect when returns are evaluated on a risk-adjusted basis.

Size anomaly: it refers to the relationship where stocks with lower market capitalization tend to generate higher returns. Between 1927 and 2010, the largest market-cap stocks had an average annual return of 10%, while the smallest market-cap stocks had an average annual return of 21.26%. This pattern can be observed across a spectrum between the largest and smallest stocks—meaning that, the smaller the market capitalization of a stock, the higher the potential return. (Link here)

Value anomaly: This relationship demonstrates that value-based cheap stocks outperform value-based expensive stocks. (This was exploited by Benjamin Graham.) In 1992, Fama and French confirmed through their research (link here) that the P/B ratio (the ratio of price to book value per share), the P/E ratio (the ratio of price to earnings per share), and the size effect were the two most effective factors in achieving outperformance.The essence of the strategy is to invest in value-based cheap stocks (low Graham number = P/E × P/B < ~22.5), low-beta stocks (Buffett exploits the beta anomaly, meaning that stocks with a low beta have a higher return per unit of risk), and profitable stocks (growing earnings, high dividend payout ratio). Using this strategy, Buffett was able to achieve an average annual return of 20% over a 40-year period, compared to the S&P 500’s average annual return of 9-10%.

Asset growth anomaly: in 2009, Cooper, Gulen, and Schill analyzed data from the 1968–2007 period and established an inverse relationship between a company’s asset growth and its future returns (link here ). This means that companies with higher asset growth tend to underperform compared to those with lower asset growth. Assets can include real estate, plants, production lines, inventories, and securities. The stockanalysis.com website allows for tracking changes in company assets at any time.This observation cannot be explained by risk factors, nor by the value or size factors, meaning it represents an abnormal return.In 2022, Cao and colleagues published findings (link here) emphasizing the need to distinguish between financing growth and operating growth. According to their research, a company experiences financial growth when it expands through secondary stock offerings, borrowing, or bond issuance. In contrast, operating growth occurs when a company expands through profits generated from selling its products and services, reflecting its actual performance.Investors tend to overestimate the future prospects of companies characterized by financial growth, leading to overvaluation and subsequently lower future returns. Conversely, the market typically underestimates operating growth, making these companies undervalued from a fundamental perspective.

MOMENTUM EFFECT An analysis of stock returns over various time intervals demonstrated that stocks with high past returns tended to generate high returns in the near future, while stocks with low or negative past returns underperformed in the short term (link here). This is called as ‘momentum anomaly’. Stocks with low momentum had an annualized average return of approximately -1.3%, whereas high-momentum stocks achieved an annualized average return of 15.4%, compared to the 7.5% annual return of the index. Additionally, high-momentum stocks had a Sharpe ratio of 0.76, compared to 0.52 for the stock index, meaning they also outperformed from a risk-adjusted return perspective. This demonstrates that momentum-based strategies can also be used to outperform the market on the short-run, which contradicts the Efficient Market Hypothesis. As a result, Fama and French incorporated the momentum factor into the CAPM model in their study.

Reason behind momentum effect First and foremost, I would like to emphasize that momentum is not yet a precisely defined concept, and therefore, its exact causes remain unknown—even more so than the previously discussed anomalies. However, we can tell that the momentum effect is essentially driven by the fact that when equity markets rise, they create new collateral and expand the overall asset base, effectively increasing the amount of actual dollars in the system. Since the entire financial system is built on leverage, this expansion has a compounding effect. Many institutional investors (hedge funds, banks, asset managers) manage portfolios on a risk-adjusted, notional-neutral basis rather than a fixed capital allocation. As markets rise, they reinvest gains, further fueling the rally.What stops momentum? Well, the stopping force isn’t always obvious and doesn’t have to be directly linked to the initial move. Once the reinvestment cycle runs its course, the artificial boost fades. If underlying economic conditions or market fundamentals are weak, the lack of structural buying can lead to a correction.Alternatively, momentum can break down due to positioning shifts rather than fundamentals. For example, last year, when the dispersion trade was playing out in the S&P 500, index volatility was being aggressively sold, causing a rotation that halted momentum in certain stocks and fueled in others—not due to fundamental reasons but purely as a result of shifts in positioning. As mentioned earlier, positive momentum expands the money supply, supporting economic growth. The opposite is also true—when momentum reverses, it contracts liquidity, negatively impacting economic conditions.

Short-term vs long-termBenjamin Graham stated that in the short term (on a weekly, monthly, or even quarterly basis), markets behave like a "voting machine"—driven by investor sentiment, psychology (like FOMO, herd-mentality, etc.), and short-term capital flows, making prices primarily a function of supply and demand dynamics. This can be manipulated by news and narratives designed to influence market sentiment and volatility bets.This creates volatility cycles, where volatility becomes either over- or underpriced, attracting the herd into one side (momentum) before bagging those overcrowded positions on them (reversion). This is not a conspiracy nor a deliberate attack (though in some cases, it can be—such as when a company faces insider battles over higher ownership stakes through share accumulation). Rather, it is simply a reflexive effect in the markets, as a result of decreased liquidity due to one-sided positioning.However, in the long run (longer than a year, even ten years), markets act as a "weighing machine", where prices eventually converge toward the intrinsic value of companies, determined by factors such as earnings, cash flows, and balance sheet strength.In essence, speculation and emotion can dominate rational valuation in the short term, but intrinsic value tends to prevail over longer periods.

A personal thoughtTo me personally, momentum occurs when I observe an accelerating trend. I always monitor trends across various data points, rather than focusing on individual values, because the trend of changes is what truly matters.For example, let's say a curve moves from a 1% return to 1.2%, then the next data points are 1.46%, 1.82%, and 2.34%.Here, the percentage changes are 0.2%, 0.22%, 0.24%, and 0.29%, indicating a clear upward trend in data point changes.However, if the dataset looked like 1%, 1.2%, 1.46%, 1.80%, 2.16%, then the percentage changes would be 0.2%, 0.22%, 0.23%, and 0.2%, meaning that the rate of change is reversing. We can confirm this by analyzing the percentage change of these rates as well.By comparing this trend to previous behaviors in percentage change, we can assess whether the acceleration is unusual or not. If the acceleration is significantly steeper, or if its mean average change and standard deviation are relatively higher compared to previous datasets, we can conclude that a move is gaining momentum.However, it is crucial to evaluate the broader context—such as whether momentum is already trending at local extremes—and to factor in simulated dealer flows. These two factors can indicate key market levels, signaling whether momentum can persist if those levels break (proving the momentum) or if it is likely to fade (disproving the momentum). To me, both criteria must be met: 1) growth must accelerate relative to previous dataset, and 2) momentum must be strong enough to overcome resistance from distribution effects and potential counter dealer flows.This is just a small aspect of my personal approach. Momentum cannot be measured directly but only through statistical methods, as it is inherently a statistical phenomenon.

MEAN REVERSION”Nowadays people know the price of everything and the value of nothing” (Oscar Wilde)

It all started with Richard Thaler, a name that may be familiar to those who have seen the movie ‘The Big Short’. In 1985, Professor Thaler and Werner De Bondt published a study (link here) in which they rejected the Efficient Market Hypothesis after analyzing the cumulative excess returns of stocks listed on the New York Stock Exchange over 16 three-year periods between 1932 and 1977. Their findings showed that stocks with the worst long-term past returns tended to outperform over the next 36 months, while stocks with the best past returns underperformed in the subsequent period. See the chart below:

The "winner portfolio" indexes stocks that had the best returns over the previous 36 months leading up to the initial (0th) month. The "loser portfolio" indexes stocks that had the worst performance over the same prior 36-month period.By the end of the next 36-month period, the loser portfolio outperformed the market index by 19.6%, while the winner portfolio underperformed by 5%.(It is worth noting the tendency that positive returns generally have stronger momentum, as humanity is inherently long on the economy.)It is also interesting to observe that at the starting month, the difference in returns between the two portfolios is not yet significant. According to the study, the long-term reversal effect is already detectable over a two-year period, but not yet over a one-year period.Professor Thaler explained this by stating that most traders overreact to news and unexpected events affecting stock prices, in both directions. This aligns perfectly with what Benjamin Graham had previously stated (written above).

The mean reversion phenomenon essentially means that an asset's price deviates significantly from its average. This occurs simply because traders overreact to events, creating momentum in both directions. However, it is also observed that these deviations tend to correct themselves, with returns reverting to the mean (it is the returns that average out, not the price curve itself—meaning that the price index can overshoot the rolling mean curve).This contradicts Fama’s Efficient Market Hypothesis because knowing this characteristic of the market allows traders to achieve excess returns.Mean reversion strategies focus on identifying assets whose valuations have deviated significantly from their historical average, and in such cases, traders bet on a reversal in the opposite direction. (Both Niederhoffer and Jim Simons have used mean reversion strategies).

In 1987, James Poterba and Lawrence Summers found that past stock market returns are positively correlated with future returns in the short term, but over the long term, this correlation shifts to negative (link here). These findings align with Thaler’s long-term reversal observations but extend the effect to shorter time horizons as well.

My personal view on this is that today's spending is tomorrow's debt. Over time, a point inevitably comes when this debt burden becomes too great, and repayment becomes necessary.At such times, people convert their securities into cash, revealing the price level at which sellers are unwilling to sell for less and buyers are willing to purchase. This process not only reflects how the market values a given asset but also drives the movement of the mean price.From a mathematical perspective, the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process is a Wiener process that exhibits a tendency to revert toward a central location, with a stronger pull the further it deviates from the center. The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process can also be seen as the continuous-time analogue of the discrete-time AR(1) process. I will cover these concepts in a separate post…

Where are those ‘extremes’?How can we identify extremes? Well, there are two basic methods I would like to briefly introduce. The first, and perhaps more widely known, is to calculate the rolling SMA (Simple Moving Average) or VWMA (Volume-Weighted Moving Average) of the price. EMA (Exponential Moving Average) or HMA (Hull Moving Average) are not ideal choices as they are too sensitive. Based on the selected period, we then compute standard deviations, specifically the 1-sigma and 2-sigma levels. As the price approaches one of these levels, the probability of mean reversion increases.The second method involves using implied volatility. Realized volatility represents the volatility that has already occurred, whereas implied volatility is a market bet, essentially the volatility risk premium, indicating how much volatility traders expect over a given period. If we can extract the implied volatility, we then convert the annualized value to the desired timeframe and multiply it by the closing price.

Applied on a daily basis, this formula will provide the expected range of price fluctuations based on market expectations, assuming a normal distribution. If we account for this value on the previous closing price, we get the range wherein the price is expected to remain with a 68.2% probability. Doubling this calculation gives us the range where the price is expected to stay with a 95.45% probability, and so on.

I will write about this in more detail, but it is crucial to remember that volatility assumes a normal distribution, which, as I explained at the beginning of this post, does not fully reflect reality. Nonetheless, sigma levels offer a reasonable approximation of when a given momentum move is overstretched. And since many quant strategies rely on these levels, a reflexive effect also plays a role, reinforcing their relevance in market behavior.

CLOSING THOUGHTSThis was a basic overview of how our market perspective has evolved over the past century. It is incredibly important to understand this to gain perspective on how we interpret the nature of markets.

In my personal opinion, one should never cling too rigidly to any single theory because, as you have seen, dear readers, every theory is merely a description of statistical observations rather than a true theoretical framework.

The understanding of mean reversion, momentum, or other market effects is inherently limited because they emerge from the interplay of many random elements over different time horizons. One of the most critical components of this is dealer flow, hedging activities, whose importance continues to grow as market structure evolves, algorithmic trading expands, and volatility markets exert an increasingly strong reflexive influence on market cycles.

I do not believe in a future single model that can fully describe the entire market because, as I mentioned, it is the outcome of numerous random variables interacting across different time frames, each of which would require its own distinct model. The market is essentially a multivariate, multidimensional stochastic function.

What I do believe is that market probabilities can be estimated with more or less accuracy, and this accuracy itself fluctuates. By leveraging these probabilities, we can build various risk management and position sizing techniques to exploit market inefficiencies and achieve long-term portfolio profitability. However, an essential part of this process is also recognizing when we have absolutely no clue about what’s happening.
