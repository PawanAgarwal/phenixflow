My trading principles Pt. 2 - The Wall Street Casino and My Story

Turning negative edge into positive edge

Sep 20, 2025
∙ Paid

The market’s system is structured so that most trading strategies are sure to be loss-making in the long run. This is similar to a casino, except that the negative edge comes together in a much more complex way.
What are the components of this negative edge? What can we do to achieve a positive edge? What strategies exist for long-term positive returns? And what is the difference between a speculator and an investor?
These are what I’m going to write about.

First, I’d like to revisit the importance of expected value, long-term edge, and risk management.
A trader does not typically remain unprofitable primarily because of a low directional accuracy rate. Data show that retail activity often anticipates short-term market momentum; yet, broker risk disclosures consistently report that more than 80% of retail accounts in average lose money. The core issues are the lack of a consistent strategy and discipline, and poor position sizing.
That is what I will address now.

The Wall Street Casino
In the 1990s, during the tech boom and the early dot-com era, simple, fast, widely accessible trading opened up through online discount brokerages. This made it possible for people to spend their money more quickly and easily. Through TV commercials and success stories produced purely for marketing purposes, trading was reduced to a simple video game.
People gambling in these at-home ‘virtual casinos’ were then labeled investors. This part of the marketing mattered because it corrupted the meaning of the word ‘investor,’ making it harder for people like Warren Buffett and other investors like him to disabuse the public of the hype and to draw the line between true investing and speculation clearing people’s heads of this deception.

The playbook is the same today; it’s just that Wall Street now has far more platforms (Instagram, YouTube, X, etc.) to recruit influencers and to promote the gambling they call ‘day trading’ through fake stories and unrealistic, false promises.

In a casino, every game is set up so that, because of the probabilities, the expected value is always negative over the long term—which means the gambler loses and the house wins.
Take roulette, for example. On the American roulette wheel, the numbers run from 1 to 36 with red or black colors assigned, and—unlike the European version—there are also two green zeros.

This means that if I walk into the casino and bet on either red or black, then I have a

\(p_{w}= \frac{18}{38} = \frac{9}{19} = 0.4737 \)

47.37% chance of winning. That’s almost purely random—a so-called fair game. When setting the payout, the casino, of course, doesn’t take the two zero pockets into account, so the payout is 1:1, as if the game were a 50/50 fair game.

Let’s say I have infinite money on my account

and I keep betting $100 USD on red in every round:

\(\frac{9}{19} \cdot 100 - \left( 1 - \frac{9}{19}\right) \cdot 100 = -5.2631\)

So the longer I play, the more the house wins.
Some of you might now say: don’t stake $100—set a profit target, say $100, and keep betting $1 until we hit that target. Let’s say the money we allocate to this strategy is $1 million. Intuition says you’re more likely to put together a $100 profit than to lose the entire $1 million.
But the math says otherwise…
Roulette can be modeled as a martingale process, therefore:

\(\begin{gather} \text{If } p \lt \frac{1}{2} \text{, then} \\ p( \text{win }$m \text{ before losing } $n ) \le \left( \frac{p_{w}}{1-p_{w}} \right) ^m \\ \text{}\\ p_{w} = \frac{9}{19} \\ p_{l} = 1 - \frac{9}{19} = \frac{10}{19} \\ \frac{p_{w}}{p_{l}} = \frac{\frac{9}{19} } {\frac{10}{19} } = \frac{9}{10} \end{gather}\)

So the chance that we win $100 before we lose our entire capital is

\(p = \left( \frac{9}{10} \right)^{100} = 0.00265614\%\)

which is, well, rather small. And it still would be impossible to gain with my infinite bank account… put a comment below, why…

A gambler’s only hope for any real profit lies in volatility—that is, in the dispersion of outcomes.
This means that although the probabilities converge toward the expected value, the data scatter around that expected value in different ways. So it can happen that the wheel comes up red twice in a row. But the key is that the longer you play, the more losses you accumulate.
And of course volatility can work against you if you hit many losing spins in a row. This is the so-called risk of ruin.

The situation is similar in the markets, only in a much more complex form.
Here the net negative expected value comes from trading costs and the market’s unpredictability. Any time you open a new position, you are immediately in a loss.
On the one hand because of transaction fees and any currency-conversion rates you may face. Further, the selling price and the buying price of an asset differ, so if you sold the asset right after buying it, you would already be booking a loss. Even if you got the direction right and ended up with either a gross or a net profit, you still have to pay taxes on it.
These costs eat up a significant share of the profit, and only returns above these costs can be called real profit. For long-term positions you also have to factor in the impact of inflation.
A day trader has to overcome all of these negative factors every day, in every single trade, consistently.

With continuous directional trading this is, however, impossible, because costs offset the potential positive expected value of our hit rate. This is so because competition sets the market-maker spread so that it is expected to cover
1) order-processing/clearing costs,
2) inventory risk, and
3) adverse selection—the expected loss to informed buyers.
This is what causes the negative EV.

The other problem is the market’s unpredictability.
Estimating the probabilities of individual outcomes is difficult and not an exact procedure. No to mention volatility… There are anomalies—such as short- and long-term mean reversion and mid-term momentum (I’ve written about these before)—that help with directional trading decisions, but in most cases directional trading is random, that is, similar to a 50/50 coin flip, where the payoff results in a negative expected value over the long run.
And an intelligent speculant doesn’t like to play a fix negative-return game. That’s why I avoid even the lottery as well.
And this is why I keep emphatizing:

The more time you spend in the market, the higher your loss rate will be.

This isn’t an opinion; it’s pure mathematics…

No fortune has ever been made from day trading (my own story)
Building wealth takes patience and years. Getting rich quickly through day trading is a lie that spreads like a virus. The only person who truly doubles their wealth in a few weeks is someone who put their entire life savings on one or two bets and got very lucky. But that is very rare. Most of those ‘success stories’ are lies.
I myself owe some money to luck, but I know it was nothing but luck.

I personally have always worked a lot, starting from my teenage years. In the summers I helped my father split wood at the forestry, and during the school year I taught languages and math to private students; in my twenties I also held some very well-paid jobs alongside all this. Of course, I didn’t spend on unnecessary things, I never bought luxury goods, I rarely traveled, and I didn’t waste my money.
Every month I kept spending statistics and calculated the ratio of my income to my expenses and their standard deviation, and on that basis I determined how much money I could allocate to investments and to trading. I kept most of the required amount in my own home currency. This was the three-month reserve within the one-sigma band. The remainder—intended to cover leaner times—I kept 60% in Swiss francs and 40% in physical gold. That way these funds were immediately convertible to cash and to some extent protected against inflation.
The rest of my savings went into my own investment portfolio and into my trading account.

Investing is not speculation. Investing is putting your money to work while minimizing risks. I won’t write about this—Benjamin Graham already has. But I will say that investing is the key to real wealth building!
I have always been a cautious investor. Of the savings earmarked for this, 70% went into tax-exempt domestic government bonds, 20–25% into equities, and 5–10% into high-risk assets. I rebalance the portfolio monthly to quarterly, and I hedge with relatively low-cost options spreads depending on the risk.
I was lucky here, because the yields on my domestic government bonds, when I started (after the 2008 crisis), were around 9–10%, and they stayed above 5% all the way until 2014. Eight years later I was again able to buy at 9–10%, only that time in much larger size. In equities I mainly bought technology stocks, because I had heard a lot about these companies, and in some cases had worked with them. Among them were like NVDA, GOOGL, and IBM, which by the way, gave me a rough time in 2020/23… This made up 20–25% of my portfolio, depending on how much risk I estimated. The weights within the baskets are beta adjusted mostly.
The remaining 5–10% was BTC and ETH purchases around 2010 and 2016. These were very lucky buys as well, since you could buy them for just a few cents or dollars—the only real worry was the reliability of the sellers, because back then it was difficult to buy crypto. True, in the first few years I had more % in BTC, because some of the people around me—friends and classmates—were mining, and we read studies on blockchain, so I trusted it. However, in 2021 I reduced the share of crypto to 10%, when Elon Musk showed up in the market as a buyer. My next major purchase will be when it falls below 60k again, because, knowing the plans for 2030, Bitcoin’s risk is significantly overestimated.
This money management is what made it possible for me, by age 28, to live a comfortable and modest life purely from the returns of this ultra-cautious portfolio. But a large part of this management’s success comes from work, job and from consistent, deliberate planning.
There is no such thing as passive income. And there is no such thing as becoming an overnight millionaire.
And to be honest, my goal has never been to become a millionaire. I only wanted to help my family and to give myself comfort and time for researching and studying.

Still if you assumes the simpliest cases this portfolio still gives a stable 13.25% annual real return (EU inflation adjusted), and an annual 10-11% alpha (nominal return vs. risk free rate), beside roughly ~0.75-0.98 annual Sharpe-rate.
Not bad, eh?

My day trading rules
Day trading has always been a side activity for me. I used a separate account for it, at Interactive Brokers, because the fees are the lowest there and they’re the most reliable. I recommend it to everyone.
Of the net (that is, after-tax) returns coming in from government bonds and from gains realized during rebalancing, I added 60% to my physical reserves, reinvested 30% into my portfolio, and put 10% into my IBKR trading account. That was the basic idea, but in reality it often happened that I also transferred any leftover spending money at the end of the month to this account, and since I always lived modestly, these could often be larger amounts. I also tried to retain as much of my portfolio return as possible after taxes and reinvest it back into the portfolio.
Here there was one rule I always followed to the letter: the total balance in my IBKR account could never exceed 10% of the value of my investment portfolio. From a risk-management standpoint, this is very important!

At the beginning I traded directionally, mainly with ETFs, then with futures, and later with options and warrants.
My main rule—one I can’t emphasize enough—is that the more time someone spends in the market, the higher their loss rate. This is because of the probabilities, costs, and taxes described above.
So I didn’t trade often. Typically three to four times a week at most, and only one trade per day, usually in the morning session right after the open. Occasionally one more around the power hour, but only if I knew and felt what I was doing. I never forced it.
Profits from day trading almost without exception flowed into my portfolio, usually toward the higher-risk assets.
Before trading I always make a plan. I draw the levels precisely, note what each level signaled, think through how I would use them, and what I would do when the price reached them—and then I try to stick to it. If I’m tired, impatient, or ill etc. I simply don’t trade that day. Nor do I trade if I don’t have a clear idea of what I want to do with the setup.
Most failed trades don’t come from a bad strategy but from a lack of discipline.
For sizing I always use half-Kelly sizing to keep a robust logoptimalization, and I always have a daily loss limit… I’ve already written about these in detail in the first part.
The point is that you have to train yourself to be consistent and disciplined.
Oh, one more rule: I never ever used leverage in my life! To create synthetic leverage I use options.
…and most importantly: keep it simple.

“It’s a pursuit that is profitable…but it’s not for the slow minded or weak hearted. You must be resolute…strength of will is an absolute requirement as is discipline, concentration, study and a calm disposition. May your efforts bear fruit and strengthen your will to persevere.”
(Richard D. Wyckoff)

Myth of hit rate, importance of risk management
Most furus obsessively tout their hit rate. But in reality, that hardly matters.
In trading we’re collecting points. If you’re deliberate about risk management, the hit rate is only secondary. If my hit rate is 40%, but (0.4 × win) is greater than the remaining (0.6 × loss), I end up positive. Here we’re simplifying—treat win and loss amounts as net (i.e., after taxes, costs, bid–ask spread, etc.). This is helped by the ‘cut the losers, size up the winners’ principle and by the planned use of limit sell orders. Because if my hit rate is 80%, but (0.8 × win) is smaller than (0.2 × loss), then it’s all for nothing…

The importance of taxes
Most of the investing and trading edge comes from having a good accountant and a good tax adviser. It’s worth putting the tax-sensitive parts of your investment portfolio into tax-advantaged wrappers (and letting them compound there), while keeping the tax-efficient positions in a taxable account.
The point is to maximize the after-tax (net) return you actually receive, not the gross. For example, in the U.S. short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income (at higher rates), so if you do need a short-term or high-turnover strategy, it’s best to keep it in a tax-advantaged/retirement account.
Everyone should discuss this with their local tax advisers and bank advisers.

My personal example is that 70% of my investment portfolio consists of domestic government bonds with an average 7–8% annual yield. Their yield is tax-exempt. Since yields have started to rise again, I’ve begun putting a larger share of my income into one-year inflation-linked government bonds, which likewise provide tax-exempt, risk-free fixed income.
I only have to pay tax on government bonds if I realize a capital gain on them, but to be honest, even during my monthly/quarterly rebalancings I try not to touch these, and instead adjust the equity and crypto holdings. I don’t hold corporate bonds. Nor do I invest in bond funds. Their returns here in my country are terrible.
(…but their are some good ones in the US, like TIPS, I-Bonds, Vanguard Inflation-Protected Securities or Fidelity Inflation-Protected Bond Fund… I just avoid changing currencies and extra taxes)
Where I live, there is the Long-Term Investment Account, which provides tax-free returns after five years, and the Pension Savings Account, which grants a 20% tax credit on contributions (with annual caps) and tax-free returns if the conditions are met—classic homes for the more tax-sensitive assets. On stocks and BTC I had to pay personal income tax on the realized annual gains. BTC is as exemption, it is still under taxes, but not the stock holding.
With these I’ve been able to save 7–10% in taxes over the past years that went back into the portfolio to compound, which greatly helps in the fight against inflation. (Because this is the first boss you need to defeat… the house is only the second one)

Back to day trading…
Generally speaking, bulls have a better long-term hedge against bears, because the market’s net direction points upward. That’s why a buy-the-dip strategy is suitable for investment portfolios. Day traders often mix this up and think it’s a day-trading strategy.
These work when there’s momentum, but once the momentum ends, the day trader faces ugly losses. And the risk of unpredictable, extreme drawdowns likewise results in a negative edge over the long run.

What do we know for sure?
1) Short-term mean reversion tendency
So if a momentum move is too fast and reaches a certain level to which I had previously assigned a probability based on positioning, then the odds of a quick intraday rebalance increase. I can take advantage of this. I’ve written about it before, and I will write more…
yet there is something more important here…

2) Volatility mean reversion and the time factor
This, by contrast, is relatively well modeled and a proven (not just observed) property. Later I will set out that the market’s main driver is the over- and undersupply of volatility. And that creates an opportunity for the trader to enter the market not against the house, but on the house’s side.
Time keeps passing. And volatility is, more often than not, below its own long-term average.

So if we are in a long-theta and short-vol position, the expected value will be positive over the long run. This is because, over the long term, the world is long delta, long theta and short vega on market growth, and short delta, short theta and long vega on short-term rebalancings. This means it expects the duration of rebalancing momentum and volatility spikes to be short-lived, and their speed to be fast, whereas it sees growth as slower and more drawn out in time.
Short-term, short-vanna environments—the reverse of this, which raise the correlation between price and volatility—always signal a divergence between liquidity and market price, i.e., a severe but merely temporary disbalance.

This property can really be exploited in the options and warrant markets. Fair value is nothing more than the average of the simulated possible intrinsic values. That means that, given a particular volatility, I simulate the stock’s possible prices with a specified average value. That will be the strike. Then I look, one by one, at the differences between the simulated prices and the average. If the result is less than zero, it must be treated as zero, because intrinsic value is non-negative. I then take the average of these intrinsic values.
The rough-volatility method can be used to simulate volatility as well, and to model the entire skew. If we know the vanna exposure, we get a picture of the skewness of the distribution, since volatility fundamentally assumes a Gaussian distribution.
Comparing the value obtained this way with the market price of the option or warrant lets us judge whether the derivative is under- or overpriced, and we short or long the extrinsic value accordingly, while delta-hedging the position.
Note, that for this delta-neutral arbitrage strategy, there is no need to forecast market prices. Monitoring and modeling volatility cycles is the key.

I’ll be writing about options next post. For now, just briefly…
An option’s premium is the sum of intrinsic and extrinsic value. An ATM option’s intrinsic value is zero, and likewise for an OTM option, since the strike is well away from the spot price.
So if a 100C ATM option’s premium is $7, that $7 will all disappear by expiration.
If you buy this call, then by expiration the price of the underlying has to rise at least 7% for you to be at breakeven on your P&L curve—in reality you’re at a loss because of the bid–ask spread and transaction costs.
If I short this ATM call and the market closes at 105 at expiration, then the option expires with $5 of intrinsic value, which means I have a $2 profit on the trade, minus transaction costs. And if I was delta-neutral, then that profit will be $7, because the stock produced the remaining $5. Of course delta hedging only offers protection within a narrower price range, but our basic assumption here is that we’re not expecting volatility.
This strategy is called a covered call.

Similarly, let’s say the underlying’s spot is trading at $100. If I work out—using the volatility market—that for a given day the market is likely to move between 80 and 120, and I see that, relative to that, the 0DTE 80P premium is, say, $5, and the 120C premium is likewise $5, and I judge that this is conspicuously high versus fair value, then I can short both and neutralize my net-delta exposure if the two options haven’t offset each other.
If I was right, I collect $10 premium per unit.
This strategy is the short strangle.

I’ll write separately about the calculations for delta and gamma hedging later.
In both cases the key is, first, that I am directionally neutral, and second, that I have determined the market is overpricing volatility and time—that is, the market’s speed toward the strikes. Compared with simple directional trades, these strategies produce a positive expected value over the long run.
It’s no accident I wrote in the previous post that I like to supplement my directional trades with short option positions. But let me stress again: I only bet on direction when I am truly confident in my forecast. For example, on a short-delta trade I also like to short a call, since I expect it to expire worthless—and vice versa.
This is the only way to run a day-trading account with positive long-term expected value: patience and a mathematically disciplined strategy.
…and avoid negative EV strats…

Another strategy I like is mean-reverting strategies, which, however, require knowing the ideas of cointegration, stationarity, the ADF test, distribution, half-life, etc.—which I’ll cover in a later post. In these cases I generate synthetic price series through portfolios that have mean-reversion properties, and then I trade (long or short) this portfolio.
These strategies are called statistical arbitrage strategies.
For example, after lengthy analysis and testing, I take the prices of GLD and GDX and create a combined portfolio from them in a certain ratio, one that I can determine orbits around a defined mean.

In that case I go long the portfolio below a certain z-score, and go short above a certain z-score. This generates positive expected value. Most hedge funds do this, and Jim Simons did as well.
However, in such cases you have to continuously test the two assets’ prices to make sure the relationship you rely on between them still holds. Because these opportunities appear and disappear.
In my next post I’ll write about this and show code, too.

Play the long-run
We must not forget that expected value is meant over the long term. So EV is a number that our profit converges toward the more trades we make. However, this requires consistent and almost mechanically controlled execution if you trade manually. Our performance has a dispersion around the expected value—and that dispersion often changes. Only through backtests can we get a picture of an average volatility.
This means that even in a sequence of consistently planned and executed trades, there will be periods when our results are negative. In those times you have to review whether the strategy is flawed, whether the market environment has changed and you failed to adapt, or whether it’s simply variance at work. You must understand these and prepare in advance so that risk of ruin doesn’t wipe out your account balance—and so that you run only positive-EV strategies.
For riskier strategies, such as simple directional trading, reduce risk through patient, well-planned execution and by reducing frequency and size. Statistically, these strategies increase the volatility of your performance and, because of costs, push down your long-run EV.
Play it smart to stay in long.
