Swap spread is the fixed rate you receive in an interest‑rate swap minus the yield on the corresponding Treasury (e.g. 30‑year swap rate minus 30‑year UST). When spreads turn negative, it means swap dealers are willing to pay fixed at a rate below what the U.S. government offers—because they desperately want to receive floating SOFR or obtain Treasury collateral.Last print for 30y is –93 bp, well below its long‑run average around –80 bp, and near its worst (–97 bp on April 8). Short tenors (2Y at –24 bp) are “only” modestly negative; the long end (20Y, 30Y) is far more inverted. This tells that stress is concentrated in longer‑dated funding. (see my ‘hidden QE’ call) This signals signals severe funding and liquidity stress: banks would rather pay to receive floating (SOFR) than hold Treasuries, reflecting concerns over collateral scarcity, repo market strains and heightened credit risk.Historically, extreme negative swap spreads have preceded bond‑market meltdowns (2008, 2020…), foreshadowed equity drawdowns, as funding strains propagate to risk‑assets via leveraged hedge funds and delta‑hedged positions and force central banks to adjust regulatory ratios (e.g., SLR waivers) or deploy liquidity backstops (standing repo, QE) to restore market functioning.It is the weighing machine, not the voting one.

The key is again the timing…

Bonds are telling the story.SPX popped late because volatility‐selling by systematic funds and options dealers (especially on zero‐DTE and short‐dated gamma, calls going from OTM to ITM, etc.) and ‘hidden QE’ generated mechanical, buy-the-dip flows into equities. While at the same time, funding and credit strains—continued to build behind the scenes.This disconnect creates a classic bear‐market rally setup: a fleeting spike in stocks, but with rising tail‐risk for a sharper pullback once these mechanical flows unwind. Because the rally is only mechanical and not backed by any fundamental setup, once vol-selling fades, the rally fades fast, resuming the downtrend. Especially knowing that vol market accumulated a lot of zomma and vomma risks during the aggressive vol selling. This is why the drawdown started after VIX expo, and this is why I called topish but supportive May OpEx. You’ve caught the short-lived vol-sell squeeze, but the underlying cracks in credit and funding suggest any further equity upside is likely transient without a genuine policy pivot or major data surprise.
