My basic trading principles Pt. 1 - Strategies, basic risk management

In this post I will examine the psychological aspects of trading. I’ll briefly illustrate—through my own experience—the essence of technical analysis and position analysis, explain how working people can easily determine the amount they can allocate to trading, and share my core practical principles for directional trading.

TECHNICAL ANALYSIS OR POSITIONING ANALYSIS? My personal journey on analysisThis is a very old and unnecessary debate between the two camps about which approach is more valuable. I call it “unnecessary” because it doesn’t matter which one you use, as long as you understand the outputs. In short: technical analysis “guesses” the important liquidity zones and probabilities based on historical data, while quantitatively monitoring positioning data, explaining the meaning behind levels and market environments, and thus gives us much greater confidence in determining probability distributions.So, the micro-cycles are determined by the volatility market, within which technical analysis applies. The volatility market helps establish the probability distribution with a high conviction level, but which trades I close and which I size up is ultimately dictated by the realized chart patterns. Fundamentals, by contrast, merely serve to supply a made-up narrative for the prevailing market dynamics. That’s why I never factor the fundamental itself purely into my calculations; instead, I focus on timing and ask myself, “What story are they trying to sell me?”…

I, like everyone else, began my career with technical analysis when I started trading over ten years ago. For me, technical analysis has always included modelling the RoC—both on historical volatility, moving averages, and support-resistance zones. Personally, my approach has always been probability-based, borrowed from the poker games I’ve loved since childhood. The method’s principle was simple: first, I identified weekly, daily, and monthly historical liquidity levels based on candlestick patterns. Then, using volume indicators and moving averages (and later by monitoring the order book), I determined whether selling or buying interest was dominant, allowing me to weight the various support-resistance zones accordingly. With volume indicators, it’s crucial to read and interpret divergences precisely—especially hidden divergences—and I sometimes even measured their percentage changes. I knew that algos are set up to generate liquidity, so they hunt for larger stop-loss clusters. I observed the correlation between historical volatility and spot, and watched whether a given trend was accelerating or decelerating (RoC, derivatives, etc.). That was vital information for gauging momentum.On this basis, I was able to construct a probability distribution for the support/resistance liquidity zones I’d identified, and decide whether—and to what extent—to participate in a trade. Later, I incorporated historical volatility modeling and made volatility predictions. From there, by weighting the various volatility simulations according to their probabilities, I could establish a distribution for the spot price, from which I derived mean reversion threshold levels. At that time, I wasn’t yet monitoring the options market or the VIX index, but was relying purely on spot flow.Later, I added the measurement of intra-index correlation and the measurement of alpha to see which stocks were leading the momentum. I then tracked those stocks individually and placed them into a macro context, allowing me to infer the underlying theme of the market environment. Macro context has always been crucial, because it gives real meaning to price movements and significantly boosts my hit rate. I read extensively—works like Benjamin Graham’s famous book, for example. Finally, I overlaid Elliott and Gann theory onto the chart simply to see what their frameworks would suggest, as a supplementary check. I weighted my directional trading bets according to the estimated probability distributions, and once momentum got confirmed, I cut the losers and sized up the winners aggressively. Simple, classic momentum trading.

At that time, many models didn’t yet exist, and I was not yet familiar with the branches of specific financial mathematics. I essentially invented, tested, and experimented with these methods and formulas myself. I also recognized that the estimated probabilities weren’t so well suited for intraday trading, so I focused more on swing trading. It felt much clearer and more transparent to me. However—one thing still bothered me…Although the method worked—turning $885 into $16,500 in about a year (granted, it was a post-crisis bull market)—I still felt that those liquidity levels and the projected probability distributions came off more as guessing than as well-grounded definitions. This was especially true for price levels where no historical support and resistance levels exist. I also found it problematic that the significance of support/resistance liquidity zones derived from historical data is context-dependent; in other words, what was an important zone a few weeks, months, or even years ago isn’t necessarily so now, because the market’s context has shifted.I understood the causes of the mid- and long-term cycles from market theories, but I wanted to grasp what shapes the cycles of microstructures, what determines whether a mean-reversion signal actually realizes or not, and when a momentum move becomes over- or understretched, or when a gap is magnetic or not.

A friend of mine—who by then already held a degree and had worked on a dealer desk—told me about monitoring positioning, volatility modelling, and the importance of VIX options, which essentially create a reflexive, self-fulfilling feedback loop within the market machinery and artificially generate well-defined liquidity at various areas of the price chart. I’ll cover implied volatility in more detail in my coming articles. For now, the key point is that studying the options market and the volatility market explained, refined, and honed everything that technical analysis tools had only guessed at about 60–70% of the time for me.Examining positioning not only reveals the true market sentiment—if we track changes in positioning—but also uncovers the clearly identifiable forces acting on volatility and how those forces will influence momentum. It further exposes the artificial liquidity zones generated by dealers’ hedging trades. This not only significantly boosted my swing trading performance but also created serious intraday trading opportunities. So that’s why I say the debate is pointless: positioning explains, refines, and complements what technical analysis merely senses.

MY RISK MANAGEMENT RULES1. Determine the amount available for tradingThe key principle is: only trade with money you can afford to lose. On the one hand, the more capital you risk (I’m speaking of relative risk), the more emotionally attached you’ll become, and thus the less able you are to make rational, strategic decisions. On the other hand, this principle is important because you must guard against being driven to the brink of bankruptcy by unexpected expenses in your private life.By “relative risk” I mean how much you’re risking in relation to your personal income and expenses; this varies for everyone, and each person must define this before opening their broker’s platform. You absolutely must track your income and expenses in Excel or Python, and run the numbers. Thankfully, modern banking apps already give you access to your historical data. If you’re not mindful of your money, you will never grow your wealth. There is no royal road to anything. To illustrate this, I’d now like to show a few practical examples.

Start by analyzing your monthly spending on necessities. List out all essential costs such as housing and utilities (rent/mortgage, electricity, water, heating etc.), food (groceries, dining), transportation, other spendings (healthcare, insurance, basic personal items), occassional spendings (like clothes, gym ticket, books, Alma’s substack monthly fee, CumRocket coin, new phone charger for your wife, etc.). Go through historical spending records (e.g. the last 12-18 months at min!) to find what you typically spend on these items. Now calculate the avg of your spendings, and its variability to determine how much your expenses fluctuated. From here, you should calulate your VaR the way I already showed you, in order to hedge out the fluctuations. This is effectively your non-negotiable budget floor – money that cannot be risked in trading because you’ll need it to pay for food, bills, and other necessities. Trading with rent or grocery money is a recipe for financial trouble.Meanwhile, you need to have an emergency fund of about 3–6 months’ worth of expenses. This buffer protects you if you lose your job, face a medical emergency, or encounter any unexpected big expense. Without this, don’t trade. Save first, and always dedicate a portion of your income to this fund each month, even if it is small. A common budgeting guideline is the 50/30/20 rule, saying: allocate ~50% of income to needs, ~30% to wants, and ~20% to savings/investments. 2. Prepare for the sessionThe basic rule is to trade only a product you know. Whether it’s stock, currency, futures, or options doesn’t matter—just understand how its pricing works. Read the analyses, and calmly reflect on the conclusions you draw from them. Decide whether you want to do swing trading or intraday trading. Then, based on those analyses, determine what you want to trade—delta, vega, or theta—and in which direction: long or short. It’s crucial to choose the side you both “feel” and understand.Finally, determine the daily amount you want to put to work that day. Decide how much loss you can afford to take in a single session, and what level of profit already constitutes a stable passive income. Plot the levels on your chart. Understand what each level or zone signifies/means. Identify the exact price at which you will buy or sell. Be able to explain yourself (and your family) which patterns you must see to pull the trigger, and which market dynamics will force you to close the trade and take the loss. And if you don’t “feel” either side, or you don’t fully grasp the setup, don’t touch the market—wait until the picture clears up for you. You can’t catch every wave, not even an algo can!

3. StrategyThe higher your relative risk, the less able you’ll be to make rational decisions—so the danger grows that you’ll deviate from your well-defined strategy, even if you know exactly what you’re doing and why.Develop a conscious plan.

In my analyses you receive: 1) Daily mean-reversion threshold levels, which outline a normal distribution for that trading day—i.e., what the market is “betting” on that day. These levels/zones tell you the probability with which the market is likely to revert to the mean. The so-called ‘Risk Level’ is a propriotary output. This is a level that, if broken by the price action, confirms and reinforces the existing momentum.2) The descriptions of expected dynamics at various levelsthese are derived from positioning. You gain insight into traders’ sentiment and can simulate dealer flow and its strength across different volatility environments. Based on the levels and their significances, you yourself consider which dynamics to expect in each zone, and you monitor them (using tools you’re familiar with). Pay close attention to divergences from these dynamics, and—before the session begins—define which signals would invalidate your plan and where you need to exit. Volatility and Greeks tutorial posts are coming soon.3) Sentiment environmentEvery day I explain which events are unfolding and what their significance is. Based on this, you can prepare for unexpected twists (e.g., a Trump tweet or a data release), and you’ll be able to place that information within the market context—which helps you decide whether you need to close your position or not.

Based on this, you can prepare a trading plan for the day or for a swing trade. From that point on, your job is simply to wait—patiently and calmly. Train your mind not to seek either confirming or disconfirming patterns in ongoing events, but to remain as emotionless as possible. Always wait for the target levels and dynamics to reveal themselves. If and when they do, then jump in. If they don’t materialize, don’t force an unplanned trade. Trade only when you know exactly what you want to do and why!

+1. Avoid overtradingOnce again, I emphasize: even the best algos can’t trade every wave. They only initiate trades under certain conditions, and some of those trades will still be losers.So anyone trading manually won’t achieve 100% performance or trade every wave of the price curve from top tick to bottom tick.In intraday trading, there are generally one—at most two—momentum moves per day that are truly tradable (I’m talking about delta trading). If you miss one, leave it alone—don’t chase. And there are days simply unsuitable for delta trading. It’s crucial to set a firm daily profit target: once you hit it, you close the position and leave the desk.The problem is this: let’s say you have $1,000 in your account. On Monday you make $500 profit. On Tuesday you lose $300. The next day you gain $400. On Thursday you lose $700, and then on Friday you manage to make $100. After all is said and done, you still have $1,000 in your account (fees not included), and it was all for nothing.

SOME OF MY PRACTICAL RULES FOR MANUAL TRADINGThose who follow me know that I am primarily a momentum trader, trading both futures and options (I also hold forwards, equities, and bonds in my portfolio). My goal is to sustain growth with the lowest possible Sortino ratio, having built up a very substantial nest egg a few years ago. Here, I’ll be focusing chiefly on the principles of momentum trading.

My primary rule is to set a daily profit limit—say 20% per day. If I trade with $500, that amounts to $100 per day (about $3,000 per month and $36,000 per year), and this will grow in proportion to my capital. Not as a straight line, but it will grow. Annual growth is far more important to me than daily or weekly.

You must also rid yourself of the “playing with the house’s money” mentality. Approach each day as if you’re starting from zero. The profit you’ve realized is yours—but if you made $1,000 one day and then lost $300 the next, you don’t have $700 of profit, you have a $300 loss.

Trading demands discipline. If I see that the trend isn’t fulfilling my expectations, I must close the position and accept the loss. This is difficult, because we tend to hope that perhaps the pattern (which, rationally, we would know has invalidated ou position) isn’t really important. Thus we refuse to accept it and compound my losses. I was there too, not only once…It’s important to note here that you must begin cultivating discipline in your personal life. If you can’t keep to a daily routine, maintain your home in order, manage your time, and weight your priorities (family, work, sports, study, etc.) by their importance, then you won’t succeed in trading either—nor, indeed, in anything else…Mental discipline is built, not a gift.

My strategy is simple: first size on probability distribution then cut the losers, size up the winners. You saw it yourselves in real time: I have a target, I wait for it, then I trade. If the market moves into my favor, I size up, roll with the flow, take profit, adjust my stop-loss, and let the position run. From that point on, I’m guaranteed to close the day in the green. Otherwise I take the loss and leave.

Momentum vs reversion strategyIf I have a clear target from which I know the price will bounce with high probability, I wait and trade against the move. This is the mean reversion approach—but beware, because mean reversion trading is the easiest way to lose hard-earned money, as old-school traders say. Mean reversion trading requires well-defined support/resistance levels within a relatively tight zone (or at least within the IV-projected zone), high conviction, and enough room for volatility in between. All of these conditions must be met. If there’s too much gamma in the zone, the move will be slow. And the market is like a minefield: the longer you stay in it, the more likely you are to bleed out!If the gamma is low, the color is net negative and the well-defined support/resistance levels sit outside the risk level or the 68.2% range, then I’ll switch to momentum trading, if I can identify levels within that zone that give me information about the momentum’s state. That simply means: once momentum has been confirmed, I size up on the bet.

For momentum trading, it’s crucial to have reference points within the zone—not just an empty, uncharted range. Simply put, these serve as checkpoints for me. I can see whether the structure is weakening or holding its strength, and decide accordingly how long I want to remain in the position.

Futures or options?My primal go-to instrument in momentum trading is always the futures contract. And once momentum is confirmed, I’ll lever up on the position with slightly ITM options. The reason is simple: if a move gains momentum, I can collect a significant amount of points on the futures trade—which by itself is profitable—without having to factor in the options’ additional risks (time and vol risks). However, futures trading can also foster bad habits, since it the hardest way in the world to earn an easy buck. You can make large sums rapidly—but the stress, volatility, and potential for total loss are extreme.

In mean-reversion trading, however, I prefer options, since here I expect tighter but faster moves that I can catch through the option’s convexity (gamma). These are generally fast, long-gamma hit’n’run trades, executed only in the morning session and during the power hour. It’s still a delta trade—exploiting the option payoff’s non-linearity—but option IV and time bring extra risk. That makes sense, because the option writer charges an additional fee for convexity as well as a vol risk premium.On the short-term horizon, the theta risk is the more significant of these. Theta decay is greatest where gamma is greatest. Thus, given a steep short fly pattern, in the mean-reverting behavior between the wings we can expect strong theta decay—accelerated further by the IV crush generated by the strong central long gamma. In such cases, I don’t touch 0DTE at all, and only trade delta (direction) under very strong conviction. Instead, I use 55/60 delta 1–2DTE, and aim to exit as quickly as possible.If the pattern is a long fly, volatility is likely to be elevated for at least the first few hours. The long options’ veta is negative, so theta decay increases with volatility—however, the shorter central gamma environment compensates for that risk. In such cases I prefer to use 0DTE, but only with an ITM strike. If a fakeout occurs at my entry target level, then when I size up I switch to an ATM strike to maximize gamma on the trade, which accelerates profit growth. If my delta reaches 75–80, I might consider rolling back down toward 50–55 delta strikes before finally closing the position.ITM options offer several advantages. On the one hand, their theta decay is the slowest because they possess intrinsic value, while they have sufficient gamma, and high delta.If I ever DCA, I take at most two or three legs—weighting them 30/30/40 % or 40/60 %, with the heaviest weight on the final ATM leg. I do this only when my conviction is high on that particular trade. But I never exceed my defined capital limit.

For longer-term directional swing positions, I use OTM 20/30-delta options to gain higher speed (gamma convexity), which provides greater leverage into my favor—though I almost always construct them in spreads, then may “open up” the position later by closing the short leg once I have high conviction in the move. In terms of expiries, I target EOM and OpEx dates because they’re the most liquid and favored by hedge funds. For downside bets, I’ll also go for quarterly and EOY expirations when I’m anticipating a larger vol event, since demand for those options increases significantly, driving up their premiums.As I said, for longer-term bets (monthly, quarterly) I mainly think in spreads—but it still depends on the volatility expectation.If I’m expecting a strong vol-crush rally, I might open a 2×1 or 3.5×1 short call spread (with at least a monthly expiry). Under high-conviction, vanna-driven rally expectations (i.e., strong put decay), I might sell a 22-delta put against the call (risk reversal). If I expect no vanna-squeeze, I can sell a 30/20-delta OTM put and a 30/20-delta OTM call against an ATM call. And if I’m anticipating a spot-up/vol-up short-vanna rally, I might instead go long a call-ratio spread.The approach is similar on the downside. If I expect a spot-down/vol-down move, I consider a short-put ratio spread to capture the declining vol. Or, if I foresee heightened vol on the downside—due to a trend change—I simply sell OTM calls against my ATM put.(More on vol trading later…)

Futures plus optionsAs you can see, futures and forwards (1-delta assets) are more suitable for delta trading for me. They offer tight spreads, deep liquidity, also lower transaction costs. For directional bets, I only use options when my conviction is high and I have well-monitored volatility and time estimates. However, you can trade delta-neutral with options as a complement to a longer-term swing futures trade. I can sell a condor or a strangle within my well-defined ranges to collect premium. At the same time, since these delta trades are speculative, I might sell a covered put against my short futures if I see the move slowing, or—more likely—sell an OTM call against a long futures position to collect extra profit from volatility and time decay while hedging some downside.

Stop-lossesFirst, you need to distinguish between a breakout and a fakeout. In a breakout, support turns into resistance, resistance turns into support, and the trend ‘accepts’/absorbs the new dynamics in that zone and merges with them. A fakeout, by contrast, remains foreigner in the new environment and therefore soon returns to the old one. That’s why it’s crucial to map out and understand the mechanical dynamic of the environment.A breakout confirmation for me looks like this: the volume of the candle is at least 28% higher than the rolling 5-period mean, its body is 68% larger than the rolling mean, then it retests the pivot, calms down, and builds a structure beyond that pivot.From an Elliott-wave perspective, for example, it will draw either a 3-wave pattern (fakeout) or a 5-wave pattern (breakout).

Stop-loss is a very important tool that many don’t use. Yet once you open a position, you should set it immediately. Gann generally recommends a 1–2% stop-loss, though it can be 7–8% depending on position size and your estimated probability distribution.I personally never use trailing stops, but I always adjust dynamically to the price action. Personally, all my stops are managed by an algo based on volatility, but here’s a trick to place your stops roughly in the right spot to avoid algo-driven fake-outs* kicking you out too early:Stop-losses belong at pivotal areas—places where momentum shifts and can turn against you. Once you’ve identified that level, take the 21-day ATR, multiply it by 0.674, and then subtract it from or add it to your chosen level, depending on whether you’re covering a short or a long position. This way you leave a little room for potential fakeouts. However, I primarily prefer to set the potential max loss limit—that is, to determine in advance how much I’m willing to lose on a given trade.

*Algos are programmed to go to key technical areas—such as previous highs/lows, stop-loss clusters or round numbers—both to shake out weak hands and to provide counterparties, thereby generating liquidity and funneling large orders into those liquidity pools.This is why you should avoid clustering stops at popular technical lines.

CLOSING THOUGHTSWhen will you be a millionaire?Well, I won’t lie… if you haven’t inherited a lot of money, or you don’t possess knowledge that lets you bring marketable products to the table (business ventures, research, services, etc.), you’re not going to become a millionaire. However, through trading you can generate enough passive income to markedly improve your quality of life and ease your day-to-day burdens. Over many years, you can build a reserve so substantial that even low-risk investments become a significant source of income.But it takes patience.You can occasionally hit it big in the markets, but that requires luck as well as sound analysis—after all, you’re unlikely to risk half your savings on a single trade.That’s the reality. The best investment is always in learning, and in making your life so seamless that you discover what you truly want. Just be careful: while you trade to live, don’t let the years slip away sitting at your computer and staring at your broker’s platform.

As I always say: money exists to serve you, not the other way around!Always know your goals…
