CORE CONCEPTSLet’s start with the fundamental operating models of markets, as well as the concepts of liquidity and the order book.

Auction markets vs. dealer markets, dealersAll traded products can, in broad terms, be grouped under these two operating models. In auction markets, orders are routed (typically via brokerage firms) into a central order book (a central limit order book, CLOB), and price is driven by incoming buy and sell interest. The best bids and offers sit at the top of the book, while less competitive orders sit deeper in the book. An auction market is therefore an order‑driven market.In dealer markets, by contrast, the price level depends on how much inventory individual dealer firms hold and how much risk they are willing to warehouse, because they are the ones posting quotes and competing for order flow by offering better prices. Here, market participants transact with the dealer, who fills clients from its own inventory, and prices are driven by dealers’ quotes. This is why such a market is called quote‑driven.

A major drawback of dealer markets is that they can offer more scope for certain manipulative practices—such as slippage manipulation, stop‑loss hunting, and bid–ask spread manipulation. A frequently cited historical example is Nasdaq’s “odd‑eighths” episode (publicly discussed in the mid‑1990s): when stocks were quoted in fractions, many market makers avoided quoting “odd eighths,” effectively keeping inside spreads at or above $0.25 in many actively traded names. The core point is that dealer firms compete with one another on quote quality, and the quotes they can post are constrained by their inventory and risk limits. This is also one reason why many FX and OTC brokers have historically preferred countries with lighter regulation.That said, liquidity is better in dealer markets—precisely because dealers stand ready to make markets. As a result, spreads can be lower, and it can be cheaper to trade even in lower‑turnover, less liquid stocks.

In an auction market, liquidity can be a real constraint. It can happen that there are too few orders, which causes the bid and ask to drift further apart, widening the spread. In some smaller stocks, it can even happen that there is effectively no buyer in the order book—meaning you cannot execute a trade at all.For this reason, larger exchanges such as the NYSE historically used a specialist model (i.e., a designated market maker). A specialist is a market maker who typically covers a limited set of securities; historically, each stock had a single specialist, whereas in dealer markets a single stock can have many competing market makers. The specialist’s task is to provide liquidity and, when needed, to use its own capital to help decrease volatility when there are not enough natural buyers or sellers—later rebalancing any accumulated or depleted inventory. Specialists are subject to strict rules; for example, they generally may not trade ahead of public orders at the same price.

Auction markets are for example: NYSE, Euronect, TSX, SETS, CME, ICE, Eurex, etc.Dealer markets are for example: Nasdaq, US OTC markets, corporate bond markets (largely OTC, dealer-driven), FX markets, OTC derivatives (like swaps).

What is liquidity? Liquidity is a multi‑component concept that refers to a stock’s tradability and the market’s ability to absorb trading without excessive cost or price impact.In high volume stocks, a large number of transactions take place; many participants trade; the order book is full of resting orders with few or no gaps at all. The bid–ask spread is therefore narrow, and the asset can be converted into cash quickly and cheaply. Investor.gov defines it as follows: “Liquidity generally refers to how easily or quickly a security can be bought or sold in a secondary market.”In Fischer Black’s early framing: “a liquid market is both continuous and efficient: small trades execute very close to the current market price quickly, and even large trades can be executed over time at prices that, on average, remain close to the current market price”. In such a market, the spread is almost infinitesimally small; it is sufficiently resilient to gravitate back toward fundamental value after periods of high volatility; and its depth is not infinite (i.e., the order flow required to move the price by one unit does not grow without bound).

Liquidity, therefore, is essentially the ability to execute sizable trades quickly, at low cost, with limited price impact—when you want to trade. If the order book is thin and the bid–ask spread is wide, price changes can accelerate (prices travel farther in less time), which increases volatility and therefore risk. At the same time, as I have shown earlier, higher risk is often associated with higher expected returns. Yakov Amihud’s work showed that expected stock returns increase with expected illiquidity. This was called as liquidity premium. However, more recent evidence suggests that the characteristic liquidity premium has declined materially over the past decades and, in recent years, appears to be reliably priced mainly among the smallest/common “micro‑cap” type stocks. At the same time, the shift toward electronic and algorithmic trading is associated—on average—with improved liquidity measures such as narrower spreads (especially in large stocks).Therefore, one should be careful with blanket claims that “only illiquid stocks with higher volatility can quickly deliver higher returns,” because U.S. equities have, over time, moved closer to higher‑efficiency conditions in many segments.

I often say that the relationship between liquidity and volatility is inverse.Intuitively: if the order book is rich, buyers and sellers do not need to “search” for one another for long, because there is meaningful resting size at many price levels—enough to satisfy demand. The spread is narrow, and the price will not jump much because there are few or no gaps in the book.In an illiquid market (or in a less liquid market), the opposite holds: bids and offers sit farther apart, and the sell orders available at a given price may not be sufficient to absorb buying interest (or vice versa). And because the book is gappy, the price makes larger jumps.And since volatility measures the standard deviation of returns over a given interval, this dispersion tends to be smaller in liquid markets and larger in illiquid markets.

Order bookThe order book contains the buy and sell orders submitted by traders for a given instrument. On some exchanges it is possible to place hidden orders or iceberg orders (i.e., partially displayed orders), so the order book is not always fully visible or complete. What you can see also depends on the market‑data subscription you have with your broker: that determines the depth of book you can view—whether you only see the best bid and best ask (top of book), or deeper levels as well (for example, the first ten price levels).The best offer on the sell side is always the lowest asking price; on the buy side it is always the highest bid price. This—together with the speed/timestamp of order entry—determines which orders appear first in the book (i.e., price–time priority).

By watching the order book, we can form a picture of the levels at which liquidity concentrates, and how those levels have shifted across different timeframes—allowing us to estimate the areas the market is treating as fair value.In addition, we can identify local sell walls and buy walls—price levels where resting sell or buy interest is confidently large. These typically mark support and resistance; a sustained break through them can indicate a trend change and a shift in market sentiment.

For the trader and the analyst, the key is therefore to monitor where liquidity is migrating. The job is simply to align with that flow—get on the train—and ride the trend.

Statistical edgeI have discussed the casino logic before.A market edge typically means a positive expected, after‑cost real return and a positive excess return relative to the risk taken. In practical terms, that means our portfolio’s return should 1) outpace inflation, and 2) exceed the risk‑free return. It is not only the stand‑alone expected return that matters, but also the quality of the edge—which often becomes visible through diversification and through how risks are combined in the portfolio. I have two rules here.The first is to learn to beat inflation, then beat the risk‑free yield—before you start competing with market returns. Inflation is every portfolio’s silent enemy; that is why the primary objective is a 0% real return, which preserves the purchasing power of invested capital. The second objective is to outperform the risk‑free yield on an annual basis. Because if you execute 3,650 leveraged options trades in a year, but your annual return barely exceeds—or does not exceed at all—the tax‑free, near‑zero‑risk 10‑year government bond yield available in your country, then you took unnecessary risk with your capital. My other rule is one my mentor repeated often hogy “First learn how to survive—only then aim to earn a lot.” For example, even with positive expected value, if you trade with position sizes that are too large, variance and extended losing streaks will eventually knock you out of the game (risk of ruin). Beyond the first moment of the distribution (EV), the edge also depends on the risk profile (tails, drawdowns, volatility, skewness, kurtosis). The optimal growth objective is therefore not to maximize the arithmetic average, but to maximize geometric (compounded) growth. This has very little to do with hit rate and far more to do with risk management—i.e., pushing the expected asymmetries to the positive side. And the only way to do that is patience and consistency.

By this I mean that, in essence, almost any trading system can work. Most speculators lose money in markets not because they receive poor analysis or because they chose a bad strategy, but because they behave erratically: they switch strategies almost daily, and news, chats, and tweets distract them. Even traders with three to four years of experience struggle with being scattered, lacking a coherent style, and allowing the constant stimuli of the modern world (TV news, Twitter, chat forums) to prevent them from seeing the market clearly.That is why two things are essential: 1) define entry and exit plans in advance based on the analysis you are using. It is critical to use methods and tools you genuinely understand; 2) develop your concentration and reduce noise to the lowest possible level.After that, you must run the strategy for at least 38–50 trades. Over that span it should begin to become clear whether the strategy is suitable or not. This depends on the size of the edge, because the number of trades required is inversely proportional to the square of the edge—where the effect size (the average outcome net of costs) must be normalized by the standard deviation to obtain statistically significant results. In parallel, you should keep a journal of daily losses and gains and assess whether the system actually fits you.It is very important to accept that, regardless of the system you follow, there will always be winning and losing days—sometimes several in a row. These must be tested. If possible, test on historical data and on a paper‑trading account as well. Once you find the approach that is most meaningful and best suited to you, you should practice it on a paper‑trading account before going live. This is where you still have room for fine‑tuning—and where you are more likely to encounter multiple market regimes (high volatility, mean reversion, tail events, stable trending regimes).If you understand your chosen trading strategy (your entry and exit rules, the structures you monitor, your stop‑loss and profit‑taking settings), then you will also understand what you need to watch to make decisions. With repetition, it becomes routine. And that is how consistent execution behaviour can form—making long‑term profitability possible.

But I will emphasize again: the essence of a statistical edge is that the net direction of the portfolio’s value is positive. Even so, just like a price chart, there will be green and red days—sometimes several in a row. The point is that the net trend should point upward and risk should remain acceptable. And to achieve that, you do not need to be right every time.I once heard this from a monk a few years ago: “In life, you don’t have to be going well all the time.” And indeed you don’t, and it doesn’t even possible. Whether we are talking about spiritual, personal, intellectual, or financial growth, our development curve will fluctuate—and we must accept that. Doing so frees us from a great deal of unnecessary stress.

PUTTING EVERYTHING INTO PRACTICEConstant auctionThink of the market as a place where a continuous auction is taking place—a constant bargaining process between bears and bulls. The famous statue on Wall Street is also meant to depict this.Every market participant’s interest is to buy cheap and sell expensive. However, based on their view of the market and their forecasts about the future, we divide participants into two groups: bulls and bears.Bulls are those who still consider current price levels cheap relative to the future growth they estimate. Bears are those who already judge the current price to be expensive relative to the future growth potential they estimate. So the bull buys, the bear sells.Market sentiment is determined by whether the emergent collective of market participants believes that price can still go higher—or lower. Where the largest share of interests converges, liquidity forms. This area is called the value area.

(Personally I don’t find these kind of tools useful for trading. It visualizes only what can be already seen through momentum indicators and in the bar charts too.)

In a bullish phase, liquidity continuously moves upward, and during profit-taking it does not break through levels that the majority of market participants have previously negotiated. The reason is that, based on the information available, buyers believe the price level is cheap, because they expect further future price appreciation. Sellers push their offers higher and higher, and test buyers’ “appetite”—to see which price levels they are still willing to buy. As long as this bullish sentiment persists, at current prices there will always be more buyers than sellers, competing with one another for offers they consider cheap.This process results in rising prices and, over the longer term, the holding of key support levels during profit-taking.If price reaches a level that buyers already judge too high relative to their estimated future profit, then the balance tilts toward sellers and price runs into resistance. For price to break through this level, new information is needed—information that reignites bulls’ belief in further upside, i.e., that leads them to continue judging the higher price level as cheap.If that belief is not there, buyers give up their earlier positions and the market returns to a level that both sides again consider appropriate.

In a bearish phase, liquidity moves downward. The reason is that, based on the information available, those participants become the majority who judge current prices to be too expensive.Bulls are not willing to buy even at the previously negotiated price levels, so sellers are forced to place their offers lower and lower, competing for the best offer, in the hope that later they will be able to buy their assets back even cheaper. The most important aspect of sentiment, therefore, is not that the price level is too expensive, but rather: “this will be even cheaper yet.” There is an important difference between these two aspects over the long term. If sentiment were simply “too expensive,” that would imply an economic crisis and a fundamental repricing—which, to our current knowledge, is a rare event occurring roughly every 80–100 years.The market then eventually reaches a point where bears no longer want to push their offers lower, because they judge that price is unlikely to become much cheaper in the future where they could buy back. Here, the supply/demand balance shifts and buyers gain the upper hand. We call this a support level. For price to break this level, new information is required that causes bulls to judge this support level as expensive again, and that also makes sellers willing to sell cheaply. Then price breaks the support level.

VolumeThere is, however, an important aspect to all of this: volume at these levels—whether a breakout or a reversal occurred on low or high volume.Because it matters whether price holds a support level because bulls already judge that price as a good buy, or because bears simply refuse to sell cheaper. In the latter case we are talking about an uncertain phase, where the supply/demand balance does not reflect bullish sentiment—only waiting on the part of bears. On high volume, support is driven by optimistic bullish interest; on low volume, there is uncertainty. The same is true for resistance levels as well, only in reverse—although low volume is rarer there.This is why Wyckoff also said that high volume at a longer-term key resistance signals distribution, while at a long-term key support it signals accumulation. Low volume, by contrast, signals uncertainty, which can easily trap those who judge too early.By monitoring volume, always monitor its rate of change in order to confirm tendencies.

The analyst’s jobis to identify these key support and resistance levels across every timeframe, as well as the dynamics associated with these areas that traders monitor in order to manage their trading strategies optimally.

The relationship between liquidity and volatilityAs I wrote earlier, where there is liquidity, the spread is small and the order book is deep/rich, because the bulls and the bears have agreed on a price that is acceptable to both sides. For this reason, at these levels—or rather, in these areas—sell and buy orders are filled quickly, which compresses returns. And the more liquid the area, the more so, by definition.Furthermore, because in these areas the two sides of the order book are the most balanced, price will spend the most time here.

I have already written about volatility before, showing exactly how it is calculated. Here I would like to emphasize its practical aspect, namely that realized volatility gives a picture of local liquidity.Volatility, as we know, is the standard deviation of logarithmic returns over a given time interval. Return, as we know, is the market’s move expressed relative to its own level. And here we need to notice another aspect of these two key concepts: volatility shows us whether the market moved from A to B at a faster or slower speed than before. Because it makes a difference whether the SPX covers a 100‑point distance in two days, or in one week. Velocity is the distance traveled divided by the time elapsed—and thus the steepness (slope) of the trend.

In the first case, volatility will be higher than in the second, because the average returns also had to be larger. The slower trend, on the other hand, suggests that price spent more time at the intermediate levels. This points to the presence of local liquidity.The greater the liquidity in a given area, the more time price spends there, and the more volatility declines. And the less liquid a given area is, the faster price will pass through it due to gaps in the order book—therefore volatility will also increase.This is why a local, relative examination of volatility provides good insight into how the market’s liquidity profile is evolving.

TPO chart, VA, POCThis property is exploited by the so‑called “Time Price Opportunity” (TPO) chart. This chart essentially measures how much time price spent in which ranges.The trader sets a period (typically 30 minutes) during which the algorithm samples, and it marks the result with a letter. The more letters accumulate in a given area, the more time price spent there.

(The yellow line is a special trend filter; it does not belong to the TPO chart.)On the left side of each profile we find a vertical white line. This is the initial balance (IB) range, which price establishes in the first hour. Backtests have shown that this range is broken in more than 90% of the cases examined, and several strategies are built on this (long if it breaks upward, short if it breaks downward, if volume also confirms).The POC (point of control) is the price level where price spent the most time.The VA, i.e., the “value area,” is located between the VAH (value area high) and the VAL (value area low). This is the range where price spent more than 70% of the time, but this can be adjusted.Therefore, the POC is always located within the value area.This area is the most liquid area of the period under review, and volatility is the lowest here. In approximately 70–80% of cases, price gravitates toward the previous period’s VA.This is because, as the most liquid area, this is the price range where the bulls and the bears reached an agreement regarding value. If price breaks out of the VA upward, the majority of bulls will want to buy cheaper; if price breaks out downward, then the majority of sellers will want to sell more expensively, at the previously negotiated price. So it works like a synthetic long gamma zone.What the trader must watch in such cases is how volume, the number of trades, and volatility change—because this reveals whether the “deal” still stands, whether it has become more certain, or whether the market is already valuing the given instrument differently.The market will sooner or later always retest these levels; that is why they must be defined and monitored on every timeframe.This property—together with consciously planned and disciplined position sizing—represents a significant statistical edge for traders.

(Note: I personally do not use it for intraday trading, because I consider the value‑area boundaries too vague, but conceptually I like it, and it is a good addition for a sentiment check.)

Price levelsA common misconception among beginner speculators is that they try to interpret the different levels of a structure as direct turning points, pivots. SPEED PROFILEThis method is my invention. I did not learn it from anyone; after many years of study, success, and loss, I developed it myself. Studying the speed profile is essentially a “cheat” for tape reading, because in this case we do not need to map the liquidity structure via past price action, patterns, and various volume and momentum calculations—which can lead to methodology‑dependent conclusions. Options positioning, however, reveals this structure to us: it shows it, exposes it, and makes visible the aggregated market expectation. Of course, we can sometimes get a false forecast if positioning and structure from a different time horizon starts to move—but those are rarer events in bull markets. The essence of the speed profile is that it exposes the entire market’s net realized‑volatility expectations across different zones, from which a good quant can draw inferences about expected liquidity.This is because speed is the derivative of the gamma function—that is, it shows how steeply gamma exposure changes at each level. I wrote earlier that traders, by selling gamma (i.e., convexity), are buying theta—i.e., they are betting that the market will spend more time at that level. However, the local amount of gamma sold or bought, in itself, says nothing. It is their relationship to each other that matters, because that is what reveals expectations regarding changes in liquidity. It also has a reflexive effect, because dealer hedging flows, through gamma exposure, increase or decrease realized volatility. We can infer the trend of this from the net speed profile of the given areas. Consequently, the speed profile can be used as a proxy for calculating expected liquidity.

What I primarily watch with the speed profile is how intraday realized volatility changes at the individual segments of the profile I calculate. I watch whether volatility follows the expectations dictated by the speed profile, or whether it diverges.I typically calculate three different levels, which I already discussed in an earlier post. The first is the centroid: the center of mass of the entire profile, which splits the profile at the point where volatility‑expansion and volatility‑suppression bets are in balance. The expected distribution is either left‑ or right‑skewed. Traders hedge the tail‑side areas with long‑vol strategies, while toward the body they use more stable short‑vol momentum strategies, or even mean‑reversion strategies around the median. The net speed profile reveals where the market as a whole sees risk, and spot’s relationship to the centroid reveals the direction in which the market wants to tilt—because at the centroid the mechanical forces acting on spot are close to balanced.In addition, it carries hidden information in the sense that a sustained trend on one side of the centroid—once local dynamics appear (which validate the trend)—results in the closing of positions on the other side, especially if spot is on the short‑vol side, because then traders close their long‑vol hedges. The reverse is not always true, because on the long‑vol side the probability of reversions toward the short‑vol side is also higher—except when realized volatility diverges on the long‑vol side.This is what the upside and downside pivot levels can be used for: at these levels the earlier dynamics may still be in progress; beyond them they may no longer be—and this is where it is tested whether the volatility expectation will materialize or not. Because if, for example, on the local long‑vol side volatility suppression occurs while the net speed profile also indicates longer‑vol bets, then the probability of reversion is higher; but in any case it means that at the level where the market expected less liquidity and higher volatility, the order book nevertheless filled with opposing orders that absorbed the momentum. In other words: did volatility compress in shorter‑gamma areas where momentum should have accelerated, or did volatility expand in longer‑gamma areas where momentum should have decelerated? This provides information that liquidity migrated—it did not appear where the market expected it. In that case, the question is whether subsequent expectations and positioning on other time horizons changed as a result, or not. This provides direct insight into the evolution of market sentiment.

Whether price will stabilize or lean into reversion will be made more likely by local positioning; the speed profile describes the environment itself. For the analyst, it is just as important within positioning to identify the local structures (spreads, iron condors, flies, etc.), which provide additional information about how the market is positioned relative to the given structural levels. The speed profile provides the context itself, and indicates in which direction liquidity is expected to move. And because it has reflexive force and is forward‑looking data—and moreover exact, well‑computable data (rather than guesswork)—it provides a significant predictive edge for the trader.
