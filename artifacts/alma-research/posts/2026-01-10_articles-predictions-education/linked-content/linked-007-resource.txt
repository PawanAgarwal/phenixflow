INTRODUCTIONIn my most recent educational post, I explained how, over the long run, most retail traders end up with a negative expected value. Alongside pricing, costs and taxes, one of the most important drivers of this is the random movement of prices, which materially reduces the probability of accurate forecasting. Matters are complicated further by the fact that the very probability of being able to predict price moves also fluctuates over time.

Market inefficiencies can be exploited by a trader only until they become widely known. Once they are, the edge disappears. A classic example was the January effect: toward year-end, investors and fund managers would sell their smaller-cap stocks, partly to optimise the look of their year-end reports and partly to realise losses to reduce their tax bills. That selling created pressure on small caps, which the big players then bought back after the forced sales. As this anomaly became more widely known, more traders started snapping up the small caps being dumped onto the market; the January effect largely vanished, and brokerage costs could swallow the entire profit.

Price dynamics have several mathematical properties that sharply distinguish them from a pure random walk. These include heavy left tails and negative skewness in returns; volatility clustering; mean reversion and long memory in volatility; long-memory in order flow (stemming from order-splitting, while prices remain near-martingales because liquidity providers offset it); momentum and mean-reversion tendencies, and so on—I have written about these before.Algorithms exploit such anomalies as well, so any edge is highly unstable. Bond-market prices, in particular, are literally unpredictable.As I noted in my last post, there are three things we can say with confidence: 1) time passes; 2) volatility mean reverts; and 3) over the long term, equity indices are very likely to rise (though I have already shown counter-examples). This favours long-term investors and volatility sellers.So how could one build a strategy with a positive expected value even in the long term?

In the chart below you can see the SPX’s deviation relative to its monthly average:

This is not volatility, but it is very similar, since volatility measures the standard deviation of log returns over a given window. Yet, like volatility, it exhibits a well-defined mean-reversion property. Because of this, a misconception has taken hold among retail traders that deviations from the 21-day moving average reliably signal an overextension in the direction of market momentum—something that can be exploited with various ‘fade-the-move’ strategies.At first glance the chart would seem to bear this out; the problem is that the moving average itself is continually moving. And that overlooks the role of time correction: the longer the market spends at a given level, the more the moving average will converge on that level. That’s high-school mathematics. Thus the moving average can move towards spot, and spot can move towards the moving average; on the chart above, the two will look identical. That gives an edge to volatility mean-reversion trades, but for spot traders it poses the same question yet again.The problem is unchanged: the average moves—and it can move either up or down. What we would want is an ‘average’ like volatility has, something that is roughly estimable. The problem is the uncertainty of direction. Now, spot has no such thing… but we are able to generate one ourselves…

MEAN REVERSIONA time series is called stationary if its joint distribution is invariant under time shifts. In other words, its mean, variance and covariance do not show any systematic trend or fluctuation over time. Consequently, it doesn’t wander off to infinity but stays around the mean. This does not mean the series is range-bound; rather, its variance does not grow as fast as under normal diffusion.

It can generally be said of asset prices that they are non-stationary stochastic processes.However, as I showed in an earlier post, from the price movements of two instruments we can, at times, construct an approximately stationary price series that we can use for mean-reversion trading. To do this, we need two or more cointegrated assets.

Once we have found such a pair of two or more instruments, we can scrutinize the mean and meassure the sigma levels at which to place our sell and buy orders.

Classic pairs-trading opportunities are rare and short-lived, because market efficiency allows two closely related instruments to remain meaningfully apart only for a short time. The key to success for the trader is to recognise the divergence quickly and respond with appropriate position sizing and timing, before other arbitrageurs exploit the opportunity and the anomaly disappears.Short-term mean-reversion, range-bound tendencies can also be observed within long-term non-stationary time series—for example, in the SPX index. In such phases, correlation and volatility are strongly suppressed, and the index moves around an apparently pinned, quasi-stationary level.

The strategies I will be writing about are mean-reversion pairs trading and long/short strategies (going long assets that rank higher on various factors and shorting those that rank lower)—together with their risk management.

THE RISK OF MEAN REVERSION STRATEGYMean-reversion strategy is effectively a market-making strategy, because when the price slips sufficiently low relative to the recent past we place a limit buy, and when it is sufficiently high we place a limit sell—so we supply the opposite side to the market aggressors, earn the spread, and in essence bet that the short-term imbalance will even out. In doing so, we effectively provide liquidity to the market.A mean-reversion trader is short realized volatility, since the bet is that the imbalances in realized returns will cancel out as time passes. Therefore, the payoff is concave in realised volatility, and the risk increases in proportion to volatility, with a degree of convexity. Thus the payoff and the risk resemble those of a short straddle/strangle strategy.This strategy is highly sensitive to both left- and right-tail events. Hence the use of leverage is very risky in this strategy even at half-Kelly, because such rules all assume a Gaussian distribution, whereas the risk of mean-reversion strategies stems from heavily skewed distributions. Under homoskedastic models the risk is significantly underestimated.

Kelly sizing does not work here very well, because it assumes a normal distribution. It does not protect against Black Swan events. Risk can be reduced with a stop-loss and with downside hedges (if such can be constructed at an acceptable price), but only if the stop-loss is set far from the current price. Backtests have shown that stop-losses reduce the performance of mean-reversion strategies.A downside hedge provides protection only if such can be constructed for the specific portfolio. However, this requires continuous risk monitoring and ongoing forecasting of market direction, because if we hedge the portfolio with put spreads every time, the costs will slowly consume the profits and turn long-term P&L negative.

We can reduce our portfolio risk by running convex, market-taker strategies (that is, any directional trading strategy) in both directions in line with the cycles of realised volatility. This is why it is particularly important for the analyst to determine when the market starts to enter the gravitational field of a tail of the distribution—whether the right or the left tail. At such times we rebalance the strategy in line with the risk and go long on realised volatility.And since a mean-reversion strategy is a short-realised-volatility strategy, a long-realised-vol strategy reduces risk. You can often see evidence of this in the relationship between index correlation and volatility. When funds are running mean-reversion trades—i.e., short vol/short gamma—they pin the index and suppress correlation, because they short one leg of the pair and go long the other. However, if the index crosses a threshold towards one of the tails, the algos tilt towards momentum trading, and index correlation then starts to rise.

In the next parts of the post we will clarify the concepts of stationarity, the ADF test, cointegration, half-life and the z-score.

THE AUGMENTED DICKEY-FULLER TEST (ADF-test)ADF-test aims to objectively detect mean reversion in time series data.

The ADF test is:

Δy is the change in stock price, α is a constant drift (the long-term trend of the time-series),β is the coefficient of the time trend (showing us if the time-series has a trend in a certain direction over time),γ term describes if the time series has a stable, stationary range where the time-series tends to be pulled back if it goes too far from it. If γ is negative, the time-series produces mean revertin tendency, meaning that spikes and drops eventually revert back to a stationary area. Non-negative γ means that the time-series is wandering without reverting back to a stable area. The δ terms check if the past rate of changes in the time-series affect the coming changes, smoothing out short-term noise to clear out the trends. The p is the lag of the order of the autoregressive process,ε is the random shocks, spikes in the time-series.

What the ADF test essentially does is check whether we can reject the null hypothesis that the time series has a unit root. How do we express all this in the language of mathematics?

Some of the limitations of the ADF-testThe test requires sufficient statistical power to detect whether a time series is stationary. If the sample size is small, it can produce false results (roughly 90–100 days of data are usually required).The basic assumption, by design, is that the input time series follows a linear trend; in any other case the test will give false results. It follows that it cannot account for left- and right-tail events, during which the usual structure breaks. Even if the time series exhibits a stationary, mean-reverting pattern before and after the tail event, the test will give a false result.It is very important to specify whether the time series has a drift or a trend, or both, or neither. Drift is the constant term (the intercept) that shifts the series’ baseline, while trend is the time-varying component (a function of time, often linear) describing the long-term upward or downward movement of the series, such as a gradual increase or decrease in price. If the analyst sets these parameters incorrectly, the test will again yield a false result. (So the ADF test is not useful in the case of volatile or trending time series.The number of lags used in the regression is crucial: too many lags can overfit noise, while too few can overlook significant short-term fluctuations; both can lead to false results.Also, if the drift is very gradual and mean reversion is slow or incomplete, the test might fail to recognise it.Note that the test is designed to detect long-term stationarity, but it still leaves room for short-term cyclical trending behaviours within the time series.(Further infos here)

JOHANSEN-TESTIt is a multivariate approach that can test for cointegration among two or more time series up to twelve time series. Unlike the CADF test, which is a residual-based ADF one-step/unit-root test (the Engle–Granger two-step procedure), the Johansen test can be applied to more than two time series and is order-independent.

The model uses vector error-correction model. You write a multivariate difference equation with a long-run impact matrix and short-run lag matrices. The test forms residual cross-product matrices, solves a generalized eigenvalue problem, and obtains a set of sample eigenvalues between zero and one. Two likelihood-ratio statistics are built from these numbers: a trace statistic that sums over the smallest eigenvalues and a maximum-eigenvalue statistic that focuses on the next candidate relation. By comparing these statistics to non-standard critical values that depend on your deterministic setup, you determine the number of cointegrating relations, that is, the rank of the long-run impact matrix. If the rank is positive, there exist one or more stationary linear combinations; in trading, those combinations are the spreads you try to exploit.

I can use from statsmodels.tsa.vector_ar.vecm import coint_johansen or simply pip install ‘johansen’ and then use from johansen import coint_johansenRunning this on my previous simulated Stock ‘A’ and Stock ‘B’ assets, it gives:

The R ≤ 0 gives a trace statistic of 18.9762, and the magnitude of the critical value is 15.4943, meaning the null-hypothesis can be rejected with 95% confidence. The eigen statistics for the null hypothesis can be rejected with a 95% confidence level. So the mean reverting tendency is relatively strong. However at r ≤ 1 both statistic values are lower than the magnitude of the critical values, thus fail to reject. The estimated rank is r = 1, meaning only one cointegrating relation exists between A and B. The columns of the eigenvector matrix evec are the estimated cointegrating vectors β. For a pair with rank r = 1 that vector gives the weights of a stationary linear combo of your series, and you turn those weights into a hedge ratio by normalizing one side.

Since the result of the Johansen test does not depend on the ordering of the instruments under review, if we reverse the order (Stock ‘A’; Stock ‘B’) to (Stock ‘B’; Stock ‘A’), we obtain the same result. With this test we can also examine the relationship among more than two instruments, but it yields reliable values for at most twelve instruments, because no one was able to compute critical-value tables for larger systems.

HALF LIFE (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Formula)Half-life tells us how long we should expect a mean-reverting series to take to move halfway back toward its typical level after a shock. That timing is critical for risk management, instrument selection, setting up the Bollinger band look-back period, and deciding how long to hold a position. It lets a trader set a time-based stop: if the move has already lasted longer than the half-life, there is a higher risk that the regime has shifted and the mean-reversion premise no longer holds.Alongside stationarity tests, a trader should always check the half-life. If the half-life is too long, it may be better to avoid trading the spread even if it passed the stationarity test.Conceptually, we connect a simple discrete model of the spread to a continuous-time mean-reverting model. We think about tiny changes in the spread over tiny intervals, with random noise, which gives a standard stochastic differential equation known as the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck formula.

In this formula, the ‘mean-reversion speed’ theta θ determines how fast shocks decay, and the half-life is the natural log of two divided by theta:math.log(2)/thetaThe value of theta comes from the error-correction coefficients estimated in the VECM after Johansen.

REGIME AWARENESS, TIME-STOPS, STOP-LOSSESWhen we want to manage risk, we must give up the mistaken notion that we can obsessively avoid ever taking a drawdown. Our P&L curve fluctuates even within a monotonically rising trend, just like the price, as we saw in the earlier simulated chart.This means losses are part of trading. The goal is not to avoid losses altogether, but to minimise drawdowns so that, ultimately, the expected value remains positive.

How to use stop-losses in mean reversion trading?As I wrote in my previous educational post, when setting a stop-loss we must first take into account how much loss our account can bear, factoring in our monthly expenses and unexpected events (I showed you practical formulas). Then we need to identify those pivotal areas that would collapse the structure we are targeting and thus override our plan. Finally, we must incorporate current volatility, since pivotal levels and areas also have a margin-of-error range that is a function of volatility.This is the basis of stop-loss management, but it applies mainly to momentum trading. In the case of mean-reversion trading, the strategy is somewhat more nuanced.

The problem we have to solve is that we cannot know when a mean-reverting regime will switch into a trending regime. Even if we run cointegration tests and half-life tests continuously, we still cannot be sure they will signal the transition in time. Tight per-trade stops are usually bad for mean reversion, because in a mean-reverting regime you want to wait; fixed percentage loss exits lock in losses and cut off the rebound. My approach here is, again, probability-based.

In the earlier backtest I set the Bollinger bands so that the SD calculations closely tracked not only rolling realised volatility, but also rolling realised skewness and kurtosis. I call this a rolling Cornish–Fisher-adjusted Bollinger band. Here we can specify a probability, for example 99.9%, which under a normal distribution corresponds to a 3.3 z-score. Based on the rolling CF, the code can back out which z-score corresponds to that probability under the currently realised distribution, and it sets the stop-loss to that z-score. At that point, theoretically, there is only a 1% chance of further momentum. This is essentially a rolling VaR.

It is important to note that in these tests I did not take any bid/ask spread or transaction costs into account. The testing was illustrative, run on simulated spreads. In reality the regime can change multiple times, to varying degrees. Although a consistent strategy can indeed be profitable, convexity declines. Beyond continuously recalculating the sigma levels and sizing the strategy against them, mean-reversion trading also requires constant half-life recalculation and cointegration checking.

RULES OF THUMB FOR RISK MANAGEMENT IN PAIR TRADINGThe key is to make your pairs pure by neutralizing every unwanted factor you can measure, then size them to a residual-risk target. That’s the institutional path to robust, portable alpha from spreads.View every position through its factor loadings (growth/equity beta, real rates, inflation, credit, EM/commodity, plus style factors like value/size/momentum). Set a desired factor mix, then add hedges (or rebalance legs) to hit targets. Most active returns were actually systematic factor bets. If your pairs carry market, value, momentum, or credit beta, those are not alpha—they’re risk premia.If you pick pairs within an industry or if cross-industry, add/short the relevant sector indices to neutralize sector risk so the bet is truly on the spread relation, not a sector tilt.Check neutrality beyond mean beta: use variance/VaR/tail neutrality tests (Patton’s “depth of neutrality”) and track liquidity exposure so the pair doesn’t secretly load on the liquidity factor in selloffs. Use your measured median half-life as a design input: e.g., time stop at ~2–3× median if the deviation hasn’t halved; price stop if the CF 99% band (proper two-sided ≈2.576σ) is breached.Compute the residual (post-overlay) spread, target a fixed risk (e.g., 8–12% annualized on residuals), and scale exposure inversely to realized volatility.

Where to find pairs to tradeStock, ETF, currency and futures markets.First classify the assets by sector, market capitalisation, liquidity and daily volume. Then take only the highly correlated candidates and run CADF and Johansen tests for cointegration.

The currency market is the most common place to look for pairs (for example, the well-known AUDUSD vs CADUSD combination), mainly because of its high liquidity, low transaction costs and the absence of short-sale constraints on currencies. Simply look for countries with similar economic fundamentals.

Futures markets are also highly liquid, inexpensive to trade and free of short-sale constraints. However, it is less likely that you will find cointegrated pairs, and even if the spot prices are cointegrated, the corresponding futures need not be. In addition, the individual contracts must be back-adjusted into a continuous price series (not daily returns), otherwise you will see gaps.

In the ETF market it is relatively easy to find cointegrating pairs, and they can be more durable than those found in single stocks; but the half-life also tends to be longer, which adds risk. You can look for pairs in markets that share common economic drivers (for example, USO/NORW, as Norway’s economy is largely driven by the oil industry).
