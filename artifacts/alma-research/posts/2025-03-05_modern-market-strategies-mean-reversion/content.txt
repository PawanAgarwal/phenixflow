MODERN TIMES The foundation of quantitative trading lies in searching for market inefficiencies and leveraging the resulting opportunities for excess return (alpha). If you recall, the strong form of the Efficient Market Hypothesis states that prices always reflect all available information, implying no predictable patterns (such as mean reversion) should persist. However, real-world observations contradict this. Why? Well, in my view, it fundamentally comes down to the aggregation of numerous stochastic processes.

The Grossman–Stiglitz Paradox (1980) highlights that if markets were perfectly efficient, no one would have an incentive to gather information and trade, so there must be some inefficiency to compensate arbitrageurs—implying that markets are “efficiently inefficient.” This concept, later expanded by Lasse Pedersen, holds that markets are mostly efficient but have pockets of inefficiency that skilled traders can exploit until further exploitation isn’t profitable. In practice, this means anomalies like mean reversion or momentum can persist, but only to the point where exploiting them barely covers costs and risks.

Over the years, researchers have documented numerous anomalies that violate EMH—such as short-term reversals, momentum, the value premium, small-cap outperformance, and seasonal effects (which we discussed previously). Rather than dismissing these anomalies outright, modern finance has integrated them into new models. The Fama–French multi-factor models were developed after mounting empirical evidence of EMH anomalies made the single-factor CAPM untenable. By adding factors like size, value, momentum, and low volatility, academics acknowledge that these strategies earn excess returns, likely as compensation for risks or behavioral biases. For example, short-term reversal—a form of mean reversion—is now recognized as a “factor”: stocks that drop sharply in one month often bounce back the next, on average, and quant funds capitalize on this effect. The existence of these return premia directly challenges the simplest form of EMH. Top quantitative firms build their portfolios around these anomalies (often rebranded as “styles” or “smart beta”). For instance, AQR Capital has published research on the volatility risk premium (where implied volatility generally exceeds realized volatility, so selling volatility earns a premium)—an inefficiency also linked to mean reversion, given that implied volatility eventually converges toward realized volatility.

Leading hedge funds and prop trading firms employ more advanced market models that depart from classical assumptions. Rather than assuming a random walk, they use data-driven, pattern-recognition approaches. They collect terabytes of data (prices, economic indicators, weather reports, social media sentiment) and let the data guide them. The resulting “model” is essentially a massive statistical engine that identifies predictive signals—some mean-reverting, some trending—and combines them into a multi-strategy ensemble. This approach views the market as an intricately patterned system, not an efficient one. (Notably, Jim Simons pioneered such methods.)

Firms like Citadel and Two Sigma also rely on machine learning models, agent-based simulations, and market microstructure analyses to forecast price movements. For example, order flow models—based on the idea that trades reveal incremental information—can predict short-term reversions. If a burst of selling pushes the SPX down by 1% within minutes, an order-flow imbalance model might forecast a rebound once the selling pressure subsides. Reinforcement learning and AI agents, as discussed elsewhere, are tested to navigate markets in ways that no simple equilibrium model can capture.

At investment banks, strategists have devised complex macro models that integrate both fundamental data and technical signals. These models might not outright reject EMH but rather extend it—for instance, using “adaptive expectations” frameworks, where market participants have varying time horizons and information sets, causing intermittent mispricings. Agent-based models simulate hundreds of diverse traders (trend-followers, market-makers, value investors, etc.) to examine how their interactions create phenomena like fat tails, flash crashes, and periodic mean reversion. Such models—common in academic research (e.g., at the Santa Fe Institute) and increasingly in the industry—offer insight into why we see recurring patterns such as volatility clustering or mean reversion: these arise from feedback loops and structural flows, not from constant rational re-pricing.

The key takeaway is that markets are mostly efficient most of the time, but they contain enough inefficiencies to be exploitable with the right methods. When pricing derivatives and managing options risk, quants use a stochastic volatility model (like Heston or even rough volatility) because it better reflects option pricing behavior. To forecast asset prices, they rely on machine learning models that have no built-in traditional finance theory. For risk management, they still refer to classical theory—assuming markets could behave efficiently in extreme events—to avoid overleveraging any one bet. Essentially, top quantitative researchers treat EMH as one benchmark model—useful for establishing a baseline (null hypothesis), but readily overturned by empirical data.

Modern trading strategies thus incorporate tools such as nonlinear factor models (which account for regime shifts), Bayesian networks that update probabilities for mean reversion versus trends in real time, or high-dimensional deep learning models that develop a near “market intuition.” For example, Jim Simons used deep neural networks to map interactions among thousands of variables and continuously retrain them as markets evolved.

And these same principles form the basis of poker algorithms as well.

SAME AS TRADING? When we first started doing speculative trading, we used a similar approach.I say ‘similar’ because there are some important differences between poker and speculative trading on the stock market.

One difference is that poker is a zero-sum game with fixed rules and a fixed number of players. A poker player’s risk management essentially comes down to optimal strategy against the worst-case scenario (the strongest possible opponent). The aim is to play an exploitable-proof (GTO) strategy that guarantees long-term results. By contrast, financial markets aren’t zero-sum for investors as a whole (though in certain areas, such as options, they can be approximated this way). A good trader is searching for the probabilistic optimum—there isn’t a single fixed opponent, but rather “the market,” which can be friendly or hostile, without any precise set of rules. As a result, poker players rely heavily on game theory and Nash equilibria, while traders tend to rely on statistical edges and risk preferences.

Another difference is that in poker, information is asymmetric and incomplete: cards are hidden, but the rules are known, and the probabilities (the deck composition) are fixed. The main sources of risk are the opponent’s hidden information and the randomness of the draw. In trading, a great deal of information is publicly available (prices, news), yet the future is uncertain and potentially countless factors can affect the market. Hence, the sources of risk here are market uncertainty and unpredictable events (e.g., economic news, geopolitics). This is why risk management in trading often includes elements that don’t exist in poker, such as accounting for portfolio correlations (because markets are interconnected, while two parallel poker tables are essentially independent). Also, in poker there aren’t really “off-table” factors, whereas a trader (and their algorithm) must monitor external, exogenous inputs as well.

Furthermore, poker is structured into discrete hands, after which the player (or AI) resets the strategy—carrying forward only what’s been learned about the opponent (possibly via Bayesian updates). In trading, the process is more continuous: positions can be opened or closed at almost any time (within defined market hours), and the market itself is in near-constant motion. Hence, risk management in trading involves continuous monitoring and intervention (so-called dynamic risk management), whereas in poker, decisions are episodic. Additionally, poker handles variance by playing many independent hands, and your profit is the net result of a long series of them. In trading, a single position might stay open for a very long time, so you can’t simply rely on the law of large numbers to average out risk—you’re more dependent on modeling and hedging.

There is also a significant difference in optimality criteria. A poker player or poker AI is typically risk-neutral with regard to the game’s outcome; its job is to maximize the expected number of chips it wins over an infinitely repeated strategy. By contrast, trading algorithms frequently optimize a risk-averse objective function, because investors aren’t just looking for expected returns—they also care about minimizing the chance of large losses. For instance, a poker player might accept a 0.1% chance of a huge loss if there’s a 99.9% chance of small gains, as long as the expected value is positive. A trading algorithm, however, might exclude a strategy that has a 0.1% chance of a big crash—even if it’s EV-positive—because the investor can’t tolerate that small probability of ruin. Hence concepts like minimizing ruin probability or optimizing a convex risk measure are common in trading, whereas poker strategies (especially AI-driven ones) rarely include such considerations explicitly (an AI isn’t “psychologically” programmed to avoid risk unless you build that in). It’s true that bankroll management in poker aims to minimize the likelihood of ruin as well, but that’s a meta-strategy (choosing which stakes to play, etc.) rather than part of the in-game decision-making. In trading, strategy and risk management are more tightly interwoven.

That’s also why stochastic calculus and differential equations are irrelevant in poker, but crucial at a professional level in trading. Game-theoretic equilibrium computations (e.g., the Lemke–Howson algorithm, linear programming for Nash, or Counterfactual Regret Minimization) are at the heart of poker, but have no direct counterpart in trading (nobody is literally computing a Nash equilibrium in the market—though market-theory Nash models do exist, real-world traders don’t operate that way)… obviously. At the end of the day, both poker and trading seek to quantify and manage uncertainty, then execute a strategy that maximizes long-term results while ensuring survival and stability. The differences stem from the nature of each problem: one is a formally well-defined game with opposing players, while the other is an open, multi-player, continuously evolving system.

DYNAMIC RISK MANAGEMENT Dynamic risk management means that an algorithm adaptively modifies its positions and rules in real time based on market conditions. Unlike a fixed set of rules that handles risk statically (e.g., always using the same stop-loss distance or maintaining a fixed position size), the dynamic approach adjusts its risk parameters according to the context.

For example, a quantitative algorithm might use volatility-based position sizing: if market volatility increases, the algorithm reduces position sizes to keep the portfolio’s variance under control. Mathematically, this could mean the target portfolio variance is held constant, so if the market variance goes up, the algorithm scales the investment with the reciproc of volatility. Similarly, if there are several consecutive losing trades, the algorithm might temporarily switch to a risk-reduction mode—using, for instance, the Kelly formula, where a decline in perceived win probability leads to a smaller fraction being invested.

A concrete quantitative example of dynamic risk management is portfolio insurance strategies, such as CPPI (Constant Proportion Portfolio Insurance). Here, the algorithm continuously rebalances the ratio of risky and risk-free assets in the portfolio according to market movements, in order to protect a predetermined minimum value (the “floor”). MEAN REVERSION MODELING Mean reversion strategies often exhibit negatively skewed profit-and-loss distributions, with many small gains but occasional large losses. This occurs because trades “buy low, sell high” on the assumption of reversion, yielding frequent modest wins, but a sustained trend can cause outsized losses before prices snap back. Such fat left-tail risk means a few bad trades can erase months of steady profits.

One must be extremely precise when attempting to catch tops and bottoms and identify under- or overpriced contracts. Robust risk controls are crucial, because markets do not always revert to the mean. Funds prepare for the possibility that the so-called “uncrossable” deviation will continue to widen rather than snap back. They run thousands of simulations to model worst-case scenarios and estimate VaR. They also use a fractional Kelly approach to mitigate estimation errors and reduce volatility. It is essential to test how the strategy performs against historical stress periods to see how a persistent trend or volatility spike could affect P&L.

Because the market isn’t always mean-reverting, quants may use stationarity tests—for example, the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test—to determine whether a time series is likely to revert to a mean (i.e., whether it is stationary) or follow a random walk (i.e., if it has a unit root). Pairs trading models find two correlated stocks or indices and track their price ratio; when the ratio deviates from its mean by a threshold, the model signals to long the underperformer and short the outperformer, betting the spread will revert. To set these thresholds, firms often use filters like Kalman filters (which dynamically estimate the mean and variance of the spread in real-time).

Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) represent another cutting-edge tool to infer mean reversion probabilities.HMM assumes there are unobserved hidden states influencing the observed data. We can think of market regimes (bull, bear, high-volatility, low-volatility, etc.) as hidden states that drive observable metrics like returns or volatility​. The market switches between these regimes in a Markovian way (the next state depends on the current state, not the full history). The HMM attempts to infer the sequence of states and the state transition probabilities from the data. This is useful for identifying when the market is in a trending regime versus a mean-reverting (sideways) regime. The idea is that in a regime-switching context, a trending regime might be characterized by returns with a non-zero mean (positive or negative drift) and perhaps higher autocorrelation (momentum), whereas a mean-reverting regime could be characterized by mean-zero returns with low volatility or oscillation around a level.

Basically, it provides a probabilistic classification of each time point into a regime.

Top quantitative funds apply machine learning to identify complex nonlinear patterns of mean reversion that traditional models might miss. Deep learning models can ingest a large number of features – not just price and volatility, but macro data, earnings, news sentiment, etc. – and predict the probability of an asset reverting or continuing its trend. For mean reversion, a deep neural network might learn combinations of indicators (say, an oversold technical oscillator and a volatility spike and a credit spread widening, interest rates, inflation, GDP growth, etc.) that together can strongly imply an impending bounce-back. THE ROLE OF VOLATILITY In the last decade, research has uncovered that volatility is “rougher” than previously thought – meaning its path is extremely jagged and displays long-range dependence inconsistent with traditional Brownian motion model. This gave birth to rough volatility models, which use fractional Brownian motion (with Hurst exponent H<0.5) to model volatility. Rough volatility models (like the Gatheral–Rosenbaum Rough Heston model) still exhibit mean reversion in volatility, but with a different temporal structure: volatility changes show a negative power-law decay of correlation, rather than the exponential decay in classical models. The practical upshot is that volatility can revert, but in a highly bursty manner – clusters of volatility appear at multiple scales. Quants following cutting-edge research note that standard models may underestimate how fast and irregular actual volatility can move. By incorporating rough volatility insights, traders can better anticipate volatility clusters and persistence. For example, “volatility-of-volatility” might itself be high, meaning after a volatility spike, instead of a quick reversion to normal, there could be aftershocks (several spikes and drops) before stability. Adapting to this, a mean reversion vol strategy (like selling VIX futures after a spike) might be done in stages or with options spreads that can weather multiple surges.

As said above, volatility directly affects position sizing and timing for mean reversion, as thresholds and sizes are adapted to the current vol regime. In a market with low volatility, mean reversion traders can tighten thresholds and increase size, as moves are smaller and revert more predictably, and vice versa. Some strategies explicitly target a volatility normalization: e.g. using the VIX term structure to decide trades. If short-term implied volatility is far above longer-term implied vol (a sign of stress), it often mean-reverts down; a strategy might short near-term SPX options (taking in rich premium) on the assumption that implied vol will settle.

Volatility is also exploited as a trading instrument for mean reversion in its own right. Variance swaps and VIX futures are popular tools – quants will short VIX futures after extreme spikes (betting on reversion to the mean) and cover or go long VIX when it’s overly low (betting on a volatility uptick). The challenge is that volatility mean-reversion trades carry significant tail risk – as seen in early 2018 when the short-volatility strategy imploded. Thus, stochastic volatility models with jumps are used to understand and hedge these risks. Models like Bates (Heston with jumps) or even Hawkes processes (self-exciting processes for jump clustering) are employed to anticipate that volatility can “jump” to new levels rather than smoothly mean-revert. For example, a jump-diffusion model might show a small but real probability that VIX, once in the 20s, could jump to 50+ in a market crisis before coming down – which informs how a fund sizes its short volatility positions or how it hedges them (perhaps with call options on VIX or SPX tail puts).

MARKET MICROSTRUCTURAL EFFECTS Finally, high-frequency trading firms note that some apparent mean reversion is due to microstructure noise (e.g. bid-ask bounce causes prices to mean-revert at the tick level). Quant funds account for this by filtering out zero-mean microstructure noise or explicitly modeling it. They also consider liquidity and order flow – for instance, volatility clustering can result from cascades of order flow; once those flows abate, prices revert. Modern strategies integrate order book signals and market impact models in their mean reversion frameworks. They ensure that their own trading doesn’t exacerbate volatility: using passive orders, avoiding chasing a falling market too early, and scaling in gradually to let volatility mean-reversion manifest without causing a liquidity crunch. (Or intentionally, they can do the opposite as well…) And they can detect when a stock or index has deviated from its typical relationship with other variables and bet on convergence. And this is the basic idea of statistical arbitrage.
