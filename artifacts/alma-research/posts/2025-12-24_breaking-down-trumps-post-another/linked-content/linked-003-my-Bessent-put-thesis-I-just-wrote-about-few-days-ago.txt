The "Bessent put" - SPX/yields correlation; (Hidden QE)

After calling the April selloff and the downside target in advance, I began writing more and more about a so-called ‘hidden QE’—which, by May, I started referring to as the ‘Bessent put’: a concealed support mechanism designed to sustain artificial growth, primarily via equity–yield correlation dynamics. As someone who both trades tactically in the short term and manages a long-term investment portfolio, this metric is one of my most important gauges of market sentiment.

This is because it tells you whether the market will decode the next data print as a growth shock or as a rates/financial-conditions shock. If Treasury market functioning (e.g., eSLR reform, clearing, buybacks/issuance) reduces rates tail risk, then—through pro-cyclical systematic flows—the system will more frequently settle on a ‘supportive’ interpretation, lifting equities and reinforcing the wealth-effect channel.

That’s what I’m going to write about briefly now…

SIMPLIFIED AD–AS MODELFor the sake of simplicity, let’s distinguish between two types of shocks: demand-side shocks and supply-side shocks.

Demand-side shockIn this case, real GDP and prices move in the same direction, while unemployment moves in the opposite direction. A demand-shock environment is therefore easier to keep under control, because monetary policy has the appropriate tools. Put simply, the demand side shifts relative to the supply side.

Positive demand-side shock: GDP up / inflation up / unemployment downInterest rates are low, the debt-service burden falls, and corporate investment (including capex and development spending) as well as consumption increase. Export demand jumps, and households spend more.Demand therefore rises relative to supply, so prices rise as well—but production keeps pace.This typically leads to an economic expansion if the supply side can keep up with demand and inflation remains goldilocks.

Negativ demand-side shock: GDP down / inflation down / unemployment upFiscal and monetary tightening take place. For previously consumption-driven households, debt repayment becomes the dominant priority, so consumption declines.In other words, demand falls relative to supply and growth “brakes.”This typically results in disinflation, and can lead to recession if production is also affected. With a high debt burden, it can, in rare cases, even produce deflation.

Supply-side shockHere, real GDP and prices move in opposite directions, while unemployment tends to move in the same direction as inflation. This creates a policy trade-off, because interest-rate policy can only bring inflation down by deliberately cooling demand, i.e., accepting an output loss. Over the longer term, fiscal policy is usually better positioned to help.Put simply, the supply side shifts relative to the demand side.

Positive supply-side shock: GDP up / inflation down / unemployment downSupply chains normalize, a technology boom unfolds, and energy prices fall. Supply therefore expands relative to demand. Typically, we see disinflation alongside a regenerative/recovery phase. However, demand must also be stimulated sufficiently to avoid sliding into deflation.

Negative supply-side shock: GDP down / inflation up / unemployment upSupply chains are disrupted, productivity declines, and labor supply shrinks due to wars, natural disasters, or geopolitical conflict.Supply falls relative to demand, causing a sudden rise in inflation. This is a stagflation state, which often culminates in deflation because monetary tools attempt to restrain inflation by further compressing an already-weak demand side.

STOCK-TO-YIELDS CORRELATION

Bond prices and yieldsFirst of all, I would tell those who may not know that bond prices and bond yields always move in opposite directions. There is a simple reason for this.A bond is a debt instrument: the investor (the lender) lends money to the issuer (the borrower—e.g., a government or a company), who in return promises interest and repays the principal at maturity.So if I lend $100 for 10 years at an annual interest rate of 4%, that means the borrower will pay me $4 per year for ten years, and then repay the full $100 to me at maturity. It is important to state that both the interest rate and the amount of the loan are defined in nominal terms, never in real terms. In other words, when the loan is repaid, inflation is not taken into account.

However, there is a bond market where I can sell my bond.If this $4-per-year coupon looks attractive to buyers because they expect a larger decline in interest rates in the future, I may be able to sell the bond above its original face value—for example, for $105. However, the new owner will still receive $4 per year on the original $100 face value, which, relative to their $105 purchase price, implies a 3.8% yield (for simplicity, I am using current yield here). So the bond’s price rose, and the yield fell. But if nobody wants to buy my bond at $100 or above because they expect higher yields in the future, then I have to lower my asking price in order to sell it—below the level implied by the future yield they are speculating on. Say it sells for $95. In that case, again, the new owner receives the $4 annual payment on the original $100 face value, which, relative to their $95 purchase price, implies a 4.2% yield. So the bond’s price fell, and its yield rose.These yields will influence the next bond auctions, because issuers/sellers must offer a competitive yield relative to secondary-market yields—if that is well-reasoned by expectations about the borrower’s future performance.

Duration and convexityDuration measures how long it takes, in years, for an investor to be repaid a bond’s price through its total cash flows, hence it showes the change in a bond’s price when rates fluctuate. If the duration of a bond is high, it means the bond’s price will move to a greater degree in the opposite direction of interest rates, if it is low, the bond’s price will have a smaller change. However, duration is not linear. The longer the maturity, the higher the duration, and the greater the interest rate risk, while a shorter-maturity bond has a lower duration and less risk. However this relationship is convex, that indicates that the larger the change in interest rates, the larger the error in overestimating the price change of the bond, and vice versa.

If convexity is lower, the exposure to market interest rates decreases, and the bond portfolio can be considered hedged. If convexity is higher, the systemic risk increases.

The 2020 pivot On the chart below you can see Vanguard’s chart, showing the performance of global equities and global bonds over more than 20 years:

We can see that over the last 20–30 years, bonds successfully hedged portfolios against equity volatility—at least around 70% of the time, and before 2020 in roughly 90% of cases. Bond prices therefore tended to be negatively correlated with equities (which implies a positive correlation between the SPX and yields).This was simply due to so-called ‘growth scares’, which were the result of negative demand-side shocks, where the central bank could step in by cutting rates. In those episodes, yields fell and bonds rallied, providing a hedge against equity-market losses.

In 2020, however, during the pandemic, the supply side contracted significantly relative to an already-weak demand side. Entire sectors shut down, and inventory stockpiles built up in warehouses. The regime flipped.This negative supply-side shock triggered an ‘inflation scare’, reinforced by an abruptly aggressive ‘Fed put’ via QE—and later when inflation became sticky, in 2022, by QT. Higher rates pushed yields up and depressed bond prices while simultaneously compressing equity valuations, driving a higher stock–bond correlation (i.e., a negative correlation between the SPX and yields).

That environment then began to fluctuate as the Fed, starting in 2022, began playing the ‘short ATM call at the top’ / ‘short OTM put at the bottom’ game along with MBS trades, in response to the economic cold war with the East. It had to deliver strong figures for investors—if nothing else, at least on paper.The bullwhip cycle created a good opportunity for this: accumulated inventories flowed into the demand side, resulting in a shorter, synthetic wave of disinflation.

As we can see, before the 1990s the correlation was net negative, whereas after the 2000s it turned positive.The reason is that prior to the 1990s inflation risk and monetary-policy uncertainty dominated: from 1965 to 1982 the Great Inflation prevailed, which tipped into stagflation during the 1973 oil crisis (creating supply-chain disruptions similar to the 2020 shutdowns), and this simultaneously impaired the discount rates of both bonds and equities. In the early 2000s, the dot-com bubble and the 2008 global financial crisis—followed by the euro crisis—threatened economic growth, and with that, inflation risk declined.

Using the terminology of the earlier four-quadrant framework: in the 20th century, the positive demand shock / overheating-economy backdrop, as well as the sudden negative supply shock in 1973, kept inflation elevated.In the 21st century, by contrast, the growth scare dominated due to repeated negative demand shocks, while inflation was low—so the central bank had room to ease. The 2020 pandemic again produced a negative supply shock, but around the election of the Trump administration it is clearly visible on the annual chart that policy prioritises economic growth over fighting inflation—through long-term support for the supply side and short-term stimulation of the demand side. (Moreover, this also allows them to inflate away a meaningful portion of the public-debt stock…) I wrote about this earlier in March, and several times last year on Twitter as well. This is important for understanding the Bessent put…

SentimentA rolling SPX/yields beta is, for me, a very important short-term narrative proxy.I typically talk about two environments. One is “good news is good news” / “bad news is bad news,” which tends to show up in a positive SPX/yields regime. The other is “good news is bad news” / “bad news is good news,” which is signaled by a negative SPX/yields correlation.Here, the word “good” is shorthand for growth as well as inflation, while “bad” is shorthand for slowdown and disinflation. The question, then, is which risk the market is focusing on.In periods of positive correlation, the emphasis is on growth, whereas in negative-correlation periods it is on inflation. The difference lies in how markets interpret growth and slowdown.

If a growth slowdown is not economically threatening, then “bad news is good news,” because it slows—and therefore makes more durable—the expansion, creating room for additional monetary and fiscal support, so risk appetite remains intact.By contrast, in that setup the market interprets accelerating growth as inflation, which is why “good news is bad news.” This reduces risk appetite and pushes investors to focus on inflation hedges. In this regime, bonds do not provide protection because rising rates undermine bond prices. The focus, therefore, is on continuously pushing back and containing rising inflation—keeping it “in check.” This is the negative SPX/yields correlation regime.If, however, the market judges that the economy has no room for any meaningful slowdown, then “bad news is bad news,” because it increases recession risk. In that case, risk appetite falls, but bonds provide good protection versus equities because rates decline.At the same time, in this environment growth is not automatically equated with inflation, so market participants are willing to lean into an improving economy: there is room to grow, and future expected returns in equities look better than in bonds—hence “good news is good news.” The focus here is on sustaining economic growth. This is the positive SPX/yields correlation regime.

“BESSENT PUT”On the chart below you can see the 10-day (purple) and 21-day (cyan) rolling SPX/yields beta curves:

The vertical dashed yellow lines mark the various high-importance US data releases since April, with a label indicating whether the print was “bad news” or “good news”.The red box indicates that the net return was negative, the green that it was positive, and the grey that it was flat.In the analysis, I took into account the direction of the beta curve, as well as how the “monthly” beta (the 21-day curve) deviates from the 10-day curve. For example, if the 10-day curve is in negative territory but the 21-day curve is persistently positive, then—on a good-news print—that sets up a more supportive sentiment backdrop on longer-horizon for the data. However, I want to stress that for this observation I used daily returns only; I did not account for intraday swings. I also tried to filter out the TACO effect.I found that in only 15% of cases the immediate reaction was the opposite of what the correlation would have implied—this clusters mostly around the late-April data releases, when intraday commentary and newsflow were particularly influential for the narrative. In a few cases, the market produced the “expected” move in the following days after it had digested the data. Of the examined cases, roughly 85% were ones where the immediate reaction matched what the correlation would have suggested; and within that subset, about 70% resulted in a positive return and/or proved supportive for the market, also taking into account the prior trend in correlations. If we also incorporate the late-April positive monthly correlation ahead of good prints, the hit-rate is even higher. In the sample, 51% of the prints were bad news.

Given the limited sample size, the result is of course contestable. Still, it illustrates that the stock–yield beta has a pre-positioning / regime-signalling character: it tends to embed a short-term interpretive frame in which a macro print is more likely to be supportive. If we add that, on a year-over-year basis, inflation risk remains elevated, yet since April the longer-term correlation has flipped sharply positive, that is consistent with the positive trend visible in the market. The correlation moved into positive territory right ahead of the inflationary tariff release. Longer-horizon work has documented analogous effects at monthly and annual frequencies; a key driver is information leakage.

Here, however, the point is a short-term beta fluctuation which, in most cases, directly set the stage for the market to shift its focus away from a net inflation narrative and toward growth—specifically ahead of the data releases.In other words: Treasury/Fed liquidity and market-functioning measures (buybacks, issuance management, the clearing/SLR agenda, reserve management) net reduce rates tail risk; this enables pro-cyclical systematic flows, which makes macro events more likely to be priced through a net “risk-on” lens. And because algos actively monitor these metrics and volatility, this produces a reflexive net supportive effect—pushing the market higher and generating a positive wealth effect, i.e., a kind of engineered growth.

Remember: technicals set the stage for the news. News generates liquidity. Liquidity fuels momentum. When conditions ease, leverage expands. When volatility rises, leverage is cut. Boom-and-bust cycles.

Bessent is explicitly focused on long-term Treasury yields, which decouples the narrative from the Fed’s policy rate and reduces supply pressure at the long end—potentially stabilizing the most long-end-sensitive segments (term premium / auction-indigestion risk), even if it does not solve the entire debt trajectory.Liquidity-support buybacks were increased from a $30bn cap to $38bn, and the frequency of long-end nominal-coupon liquidity-support buybacks was doubled.In addition, Bessent has repeatedly supported reform of bank leverage ratios in public, arguing that the current calibration too often becomes a binding constraint and can restrain Treasury-market intermediation (repo, market-making, inventory). This reform targets bank balance-sheet capacity toward Treasuries/repo, reducing tail risk in yield swings.Moreover, through the GENIUS Act, he is building a new, regulation-driven demand base for T-bills—by steering stablecoin reserve composition toward cash and highly liquid assets such as U.S. Treasuries—which further scales the buyback program.This supportive liquidity is primarily debt management, but it also has a “hidden QE” effect because it stabilizes rates volatility and loosens financial conditions—just as I flagged in April.

In addition, on December 10 the Fed announced that it would begin T-bill purchases for “reserve management” purposes; and the New York Fed Desk published the Reserve Management Purchases operational framework as well as the roughly $40bn size for the first month.

Taken together, this effectively means prioritising the financing of growth and reducing the debt-service burden rather than fighting inflation. And that is the key to the whole situation.

REALITYThe admin has therefore put the emphasis on reducing the debt-service burden and driving economic growth at all costs.The problem is that the growth engine is not diversified; it is concentrated primarily around the AI boom. Hyperscalers’ AI-infrastructure spending in 2025 is exceptionally large, and there is a significant risk that the real-economy payoff will end up being only a fraction of it. A large share of the demand here is coming from the buildout itself—essentially supply-chain and construction demand—rather than genuine end-demand. And this creates a very substantial synthetic long-term downside gap further out.And under the “hidden QE” dynamic, that gap will only continue to widen…
