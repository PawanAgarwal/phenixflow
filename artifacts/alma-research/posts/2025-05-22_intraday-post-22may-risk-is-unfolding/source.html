Risk is unfolding, Bear market rally? + Intraday post (22/May)

May 22, 2025
∙ Paid

tl;dr After the May 21 bond auction sent 20‑year yields above 5%, markets are on edge ahead of a short Memorial Day week that brings both Q1 GDP revisions (May 29) and April PCE inflation (May 30).
With little new “toolbox” left for Trump, beyond the just‑expiring 90‑day tariff truce, a further unwind in bonds could trigger a sharp equity pullback this summer—forcing the Fed into an risky choice between engineering a crash (and eventual QE with “on‑paper” inflation spiking) or pre‑emptively easing right after PCE data.
Economists forecast sluggish growth (Q1 GDP –0.3 %) and sticky core PCE (~+2.3 % y/y), suggesting we’re in for a volatile, “bloodbath” season unless data surprise to the upside.

Let’s talk a little bit about the bonds…
Moody’s downgraded the U.S. credit rating from Aaa to Aa1 on May 16, citing rising deficits and debt levels.
The Treasury’s sale of $16 billion in 20‑year notes on May  21 saw a median yield of 4.974% and a low yield of 4.900% The auction “tailed” by 1.1 basis points, meaning the accepted yield was 1.1 bp above traders’ expectations—well within normal historical ranges. Primary dealers purchased 17% of the issue, compared with a 15% average in recent auctions, indicating dealer support was actually stronger than usual.

10‑year breakeven inflation rates—a gauge of market inflation expectations—were essentially unchanged at 2.35% on May 21. 5‑year breakevens stood at 2.43%, reflecting moderately elevated price expectations over the medium term. The Cleveland Fed’s model pegs 10‑year expected inflation at 2.31%, underscoring that inflation risk remains a key input into long‑term yields.
A fragile U.S.–China tariff truce and warnings of impending tariff‑induced price hikes have heightened cost pressures, contributing to yield volatility. Corporate profits are under strain from persistent tariffs on steel, aluminum, and other imports, compounding macro uncertainty. Also credit spreads widened.
Following the auction, the 20‑year Treasury yield jumped to about 5.0%, its highest level since late 2023, as investors reacted to broader debt concerns.

Japan remains by far the largest foreign holder—adding only $4.9 bn in March. China continues to trim its position (–$18.9 bn). Other major increases came from the Cayman Islands, UK and Canada; notable decreases from Hong Kong, UAE and Ireland. This doesn’t favor for any longer term deal.
A surprisingly weak auction for 30‑ and 40‑year JGBs in late May sent those super‑long yields up 15–20 bp to multi‑decade highs (just above 3%) while 5‑ and 10‑year yields barely budged. That sell‑off came as the BoJ backs away from its ultra‑easy, large‑scale bond‑buying: it’s tapering purchases by ¥400 bn per quarter (aiming to halve its gross buying to ¥3 trn by March 2026), which tightens JGB supply/demand. Domestic life insurers and pension funds, after chasing ultra‑long bonds in anticipation of tighter BoJ policy, have begun cutting duration and dialing back foreign‑bond bets (including Treasuries) as hedging costs climbed.
As JGB yields creep higher from near‑zero (now ~1.3% on 10‑year) while U.S. 10‑year Treasuries sit around 4.4%, the USD/JPY carry trade becomes less attractive. When the incentive to borrow cheap yen and buy higher‑yielding dollar assets fades, flows unwind and the yen strengthens.
Whereas Japanese investors had been large buyers of overseas bonds, the recent yield pickup at home has prompted them to shift capital back into JGBs.

Now, the problem is…
If yields keep rising unchecked, a forced unwind could blow out credit conditions, forcing the Fed into emergency QE—pushing measured inflation skyward on the mechanics of large‑scale asset purchases. Alternatively, the Fed could pre‑announce a dovish pivot right after the May 30 PCE release (before the June 18 meeting), aiming to steady markets—but risks being seen as too reactive.
The White House has signaled tariffs could snap back to 20 %+ on July 9 if no new agreement is reached, keeping uncertainty high. Beyond extending US/CN pause or resorting to full QE (which risks “on‑paper” inflation spiking toward 8–10 %), few options remain to calm markets once bond yields and credit anxiety intensify.

The fact that the U.S. curve is in a bear‑steepener (long yields rising faster than 10s) parallels what we saw in JGB markets—when JGB 30s and 40s spiked 15–20 bp on weak auctions, U.S. long bonds also took a hit as global real‑money funds, basis‑trade desks and portfolio managers reevaluated duration risk across the board. A bear‑steepener signals that term premia are rising (higher compensation demanded for being long duration). That tends to weigh on risk assets (equities) and drives safe‑haven flows into the yen and dollar alike.
Investor concern over fiscal deficits, debt‐ceiling brinkmanship, and tariff‐driven inflation risks is increasing. With tariff uncertainty set to snap back in July and the debt‑ceiling deadline looming in August, policy risk is front‑and‑center—fueling more CDS buying and equity hedging.
The confluence of spiking CDS, inverted yield curves, and widening credit spreads mirrors classic pre‑bear‑market setups seen in 2007–08 and 2018.
If economic data hold up (e.g., resilient Q2 growth), CDS spreads and equity markets can recede from the brink - low probability.

What to look for?
As lapsed policy buffers, seasonal volatility, weaker growth, sticky inflation effects compound, the stage is set for a bloody summer. If data disappoints. We have next week’s GDP & PCE prints (May 29–30). Look for end of tariff pause negotiations (early July), Fed communications post‑PCE (June 18) and credit conditions/yield curves—if long–short spreads blow out, risk assets will likely follow. These events are crucial. Weighing machine is still running in the background, ppl forget.
Also, next Monday is Memorial Day, US markets are closed. Historically, Memorial Day week averaged a small gain (+0.52 %), but since 2010 the pattern has flipped to underperformance—raising the chance of a downside acceleration into the long weekend.

If tariff war keeps going on, US economy will be in big trouble. And Xi knows this.
