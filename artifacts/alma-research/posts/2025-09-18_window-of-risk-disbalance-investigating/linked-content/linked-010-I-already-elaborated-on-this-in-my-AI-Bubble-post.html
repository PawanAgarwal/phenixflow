In modern markets, one can use repurchase agreements (repos) to unlock liquidity from bonds. In a repo transaction, bondholders can effectively convert their bonds to cash temporarily: they sell the bond to a lender and agree to repurchase it later, using the bond as collateral. This practice enables large institutions to unlock money parked in treasuries or other high-quality bonds, freeing cash for other investments without outright selling their bond holdings. But this isn’t enough…

There is a big trend under the hood, I signaled first around the April bottom marking as ‘hidden QE’: the enormous surge into money market funds and short-term Treasuries. Money market fund balances have hit all-time highs – roughly $7 trillion in assets by the first half of 2025, continuing a multi-year wave of inflows. These funds are essentially cash parking vehicles, and their yields jumped to ~4–5% after the Fed’s rate hike, and investors are in ‘wait-and-see’ mode parking their money in MMFs. And with so much money sitting in risk-free instruments, there’s less marginal buying power for stocks – and if anything spooks markets, that cash indicates investors were unconvinced and could further reduce risk.
And Trump wants to unlock this money by cutting rates. Sounds foolish, right? But…

How does a bubble burst?
By flooding markets with cheap money, they can inflate asset bubbles. Investors borrow cheaply and pile into stocks, real estate, etc., driving prices beyond fundamentals. Then, when the unsustainable debt‑fueled fake growth collides with tightening liquidity, the elites decide the time has come, the policy flips: interest rates are raised sharply, liquidity is withdrawn, different policy shocks can be managed, insiders start to distribute into euphoria and the bubble bursts as asset prices collapse.
Trump attempted something like this in April by his aggressive tariff announcement in order to take back the 2024 gains and to force the Fed to cut rates.

One of my mentor often said: you can’t predict exactly when a bubble will pop – but you can read telltale signs and position yourself accordingly. The symptoms are like: widespread belief that prices and valuations are absurdly high yet will keep rising, a surge of new amateur investors, record trading volumes, and heavy equity issuance (companies rushing to sell stock).
Big players are always scaling out when euphoria is building, selling gradually into strength in a way you cannot see it in the order flow (block trades etc.). They never ring a bell at the top, but they create the top by their selling.
The bigger the bubble, the bigger it bursts. So, insiders are interested to inflate the bubble to create huge discount later.

From the insider perspective, a bubble’s burst should be controlled – more like a scheduled demolition than an explosion that harms everyone. For instance, policy-makers might hike rates to cool off an overheating market before it explodes on its own; or they might tighten lending standards to slowly deflate a housing bubble. The benefit for insiders is that they can ensure they have liquidity ready to buy assets after the reset. If done well, a controlled burst leaves the system intact (no total collapse, just a scare) and allows a quick transition to the next growth phase (the digital economy, I’ve been writing about).

Stability breeds complacency, which breeds instability. Systems become fragile when risks are ignored, and insiders use that fragility: they wait until the crowd is over-extended and complacent, then act in a way that exposes the hidden risks all at once. Why?
“Liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate farmers, liquidate real estate… it will purge the rottenness out of the system… [afterward] enterprising people will pick up [assets] from less competent people” as explained by U.S. Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon back in 1929, basically purging the excesses of the preceding boom, cleaning out failed ventures and free up resources for new innovation.

Thus, elites use crises as opportunities to reallocate capital to its “best” (from their perspective) use, which often means into their own hands or into the next big growth sector (tech, etc.).

In our era, by bursting the old bubble (say, in traditional industries or overvalued legacy assets), capital can be rechanneled into new tech and digital assets afterward. This pattern was already seen after the dot-com crash: the bubble burst in 2000 cleared out flimsy startups, but from the wreckage rose the next generation of tech giants. Similarly, a future crash will clear the way for blockchain, AI, and other digital innovations to take center stage, with capital flowing to them once the dust settles.
