Running out of time, US/China war (+intraday post 11/April)

Dear viewers and readers, it looks like we won’t have to wander far for our daily dose of excitement today either! The US/China viagra-supercharged cockfight continues, with China in the left corner getting ready to unleash yet another round of hits, while the US in the right corner does its best to absorb every blow and pray its backers don’t throw in the towel. In the stands, the Russian and Arab delegations—who’ve been rooting for the US as their favorite—keep shouting, “Fuck, get yourself together!” Meanwhile, they’re clearly still undecided about whether to sponsor him for the upcoming season. As for Lady Europe, previously known in the tabloids as US’ sweetheart, is in a hysterical frenzy, being clearly unsure yet whose bankroll she wants to gold-dig from in the future…

Currently…Violent sell-offs in risk assets, surges in gold, cash, and safe currencies, and record volatility…Safe-haven assets have surged. Gold prices rocketed above $3,200/oz to all-time highs as investors sought shelter amid a weaker U.S. dollar and recession fears. In fact, gold is up ~21% year-to-date, buoyed by crisis hedging demand and large inflows into gold ETFs. The Swiss franc hit a 10-year high vs USD and the yen strengthened to multi-month highs. U.S. Treasuries saw chaotic trading: paradoxically, long-term yields spiked in a “dash for cash” even as rate-cut expectations grew. The 10-year Treasury yield jumped to ~4.45% (≈+45 bps in one week, the sharpest rise since 2001)​, and 30-year yields neared 4.9% (largest weekly jump since 1982)​.Japan and China has started to sell as predicted. Meanwhile, short-term Treasuries and cash have been in high demand, with safe-haven inflows of ~$18.8 billion into Treasury funds in a week – the largest ever, according to BofA data​.

Incoming data and policy communications also reflect the immediate fallout. Price pressures are mixed – consumer prices unexpectedly fell in March (a sign that demand may be weakening) even as tariffs raise some costs. Core inflation could still spike later (PIMCO estimates U.S. core CPI may reach ~4.5% if tariffs fully pass through), but near-term, deflationary signals have appeared. The Federal Reserve is pivoting toward easing: traders now bet the Fed will cut rates by ~100 bps by end-2025, with rate reductions possibly restarting as soon as June. Everything I predicted is happening.

This dovish shift is a response to mounting recession odds. Each 1 percentage point increase in the average U.S. tariff rate shaves ~0.1 pp off GDP growth. With effective tariff rates jumping by double digits in this dispute, the drag on 2025 growth could easily be 1–2% or more. Consumers will now face an average effective tariff of ~25% (vs ~2% pre-war), a huge shock. We can expect that the tariff-driven hit will push core inflation up to ~4.5% even as growth stalls, as empirically tariffs tend to reduce output and productivity in the medium term while raising unemployment and inequality.JPMorgan warns a contraction later in 2025 is now “more likely than not” despite the slight tariff reprieve​. Fed officials have hinted at flexibility; for instance, Chicago Fed President Austan Goolsbee noted that rate cuts are still on the table if growth falters significantly​. On the fiscal side, the U.S. Treasury has tried to project confidence. Treasury Secretary (nominee) Scott Bessent asserted that “more than 75 countries” are eager to negotiate new trade deals with Washington, downplaying isolation risks​. President Trump himself expressed hope for a future deal with Beijing even as he doubled down on tariffs​. Nonetheless, business sentiment has soured: in a Richmond Fed survey, over 30% of U.S. CFOs cited trade tariffs as their number-one concern in Q1 2025, up from just 8% in the prior quarter​. In the short term, U.S. firms face enormous uncertainty – prompting many to freeze hiring and capex plans until the trade situation clarifies.

The People’s Bank of China signaled a “moderately loose” monetary stance to support financial markets and ensure stability​. Indeed, we can expect the PBOC to cut bank reserve requirements or interest rates in Q2 to counter tariff-related risks (as Bloomberg surveys indicated)​. The Chinese yuan has been allowed to soften somewhat to help offset higher U.S. tariffs​, although Beijing is managing any currency moves carefully to avoid capital flight. Export-oriented manufacturers face a demand cliff in the U.S. market, and Chinese equities have underperformed their global peers amid the standoff.China’s Commerce Ministry (MOFCOM) has been actively rallying diplomatic and economic support: on April 10, Commerce Minister Wang Wentao held calls with Saudi Arabia and South Africa to discuss responses to U.S. “reciprocal tariffs”. Strengthening trade ties with Gulf states and BRICS partners is one tactic to mitigate U.S. pressure. State media in China emphasize national resilience and have framed U.S. tariffs as “abusive”​, preparing the public for a protracted standoff. In the near term, China has also leveraged administrative controls – for instance, reportedly halting some purchases of U.S. farm goods and critical components – as immediate retaliation beyond formal tariffs. Both Beijing and Washington, while engaging in this brinkmanship, have left a door open to negotiation (e.g. Trump’s 90-day tariff pause for non-retaliators), but no comprehensive truce is expected soon.

EarningsAs the Q1 2025 earnings season begins, companies face a very uncertain environment. Many executives are expected to withhold guidance or strike a cautious tone due to the tariff volatility​. Industries with global supply chains (tech hardware, autos, machinery, retail) are already warning of margin pressures from higher import costs and potential supply disruptions. More than 30% of firms now list tariffs as their top business concern (up from just 8% before)​, and this is likely to translate into delayed investment and hiring.Notably, any corporate earnings disappointments pose extra risk for stocks in this climate. Wall Street analysts have started cutting 2025 profit estimates for tariff-exposed sectors – a Goldman Sachs note projected that higher tariffs and weaker demand could reduce S&P 500 earnings growth by several percentage points over the next year​. However, they also suggest that if a resolution emerges within a year, markets “should recover” after the initial shock​. For now, uncertainty reigns. Firms are reassessing supply chains (seeking alternative suppliers outside China, where possible) and some are even renegotiating contracts to include tariff clauses.

Meanwhile, Russian oligarch capital… and other offshore wealth are finding new havens that align with the shifting geopolitical winds. Hit by Western sanctions since the Ukraine conflict, wealthy Russians have increasingly moved assets to Dubai, Hong Kong, and other offshore networks that are outside U.S./EU jurisdiction. This trend is likely to continue and even deepen as the U.S.–China rift grows: if China and its partners create an alternate financial ecosystem, sanctioned or wary capital (from Russia, Iran, or others) will flow along those channels. We may see the rise of a more robust “East-friendly” offshore system, with hubs like UAE, Singapore, Hong Kong facilitating capital movement for those avoiding the Western-centric system. For example, Russian oil trade has shifted to Hong Kong and Dubai as primary centers after Western bans​, and similar re-routing could happen for other capital flows. Over the long run, such reallocation means the U.S. and allied economies could see reduced foreign capital inflows, potentially raising funding costs. Indeed, data from late March showed foreigners dumping $6.5 billion in U.S. equities in a single week amid the tariff turmoil​. A significant exodus from U.S. assets was noted as global investors lost confidence, contributing to the dollar’s decline​. If major reserve holders like Gulf sovereign funds or China’s SAFE diversify away from Treasuries (to gold, euro or yuan assets), the U.S. could face higher interest rates structurally.This is a huge pressure on Trump.

Agricultural products are a focal point of China’s tactical response. After China’s retaliatory tariffs on U.S. farm goods, soybean prices plummeted (China is the world’s largest soy importer). On April 4, when Beijing imposed an extra 34% duty on all U.S. imports (on top of earlier tariffs), Chicago soybeans fell ~3.4% in one day to around $9.77/bushel – the lowest price of 2025 to date. Effectively, a tariff near 44% on U.S. soy “is like shutting down all U.S. agricultural imports” to China. Chinese buyers immediately halted purchases of U.S. soybeans, and other Asian importers also shied away due to uncertainty and logistical issues. By early April, many importers were “not taking the risk of importing from the U.S.” given sky-high tariffs and a new U.S. rule imposing fees on ships linked to China​. U.S. Gulf export terminals saw order cancellations, and soybean stockpiles began to build in American silos.

China has strategically managed its soybean imports to maximize pressure on the U.S. while securing supply from elsewhere. In the short term, China can “gamble” by cutting U.S. soy purchases because it has alternatives: ample reserves and eager suppliers like Brazil this time. China’s buying from Brazil in Q2 2025 is on track for a record surge, making up for the U.S. shortfall. Chinese firms have also invested in South American logistics and farms in recent years, bolstering non-U.S. supply lines. On the demand side, China has measures to reduce soy needs in the short run (for example, tapping state soy reserves and adjusting feedstock ratios for livestock). All of this means China can withstand a near-total cutoff of U.S. soy for some time – a potent leverage tool.Despite the steep drop to multi-month lows, soybean futures saw sporadic rallies on hopes of a deal. Interestingly, when Trump announced a temporary tariff pause (excluding China) on April 10, soy futures briefly jumped ~20 cents along with other commodities​. The market is effectively handicapping the chance of China resuming U.S. purchases later in the year (for instance, if a deal or at least a buying quota commitment emerges). If no agreement is reached by the U.S. harvest time, the absence of Chinese buying would be severely bearish for U.S. prices.

One of my friends pointed out that this purchase is genuine, and it signals that China is prepared for a long, drawn-out struggle. Because soy is a staple in their diet, they’re buying it now to ensure they have adequate reserves in the future when supply disruptions occur.

Corn and wheat have similarly been pressured – U.S. wheat futures are near lows as Asian importers seek grain from Australia, Canada, and the Black Sea instead of the U.S.

Energy markets initially saw oil prices drop on feared demand loss (Brent crude fell into the low $60s, a four-year low, though OPEC’s actions and other factors also play a role. Saudi Arabia has increased crude supply to Asia at discounted prices indicating that even oil trade flows are adjusting – China is happy to take cheaper Saudi oil while cutting purchases from the U.S. (which had become a minor exporter to China in recent years). But I need to emphasize that behind these processes are other, non–U.S.-related internal agreements, which I’m not allowed go into here. Industrial metals like copper have slumped on global growth worries and specific tariffs (the U.S. imposed new copper and mineral tariffs on April 2, which hit mining equities).

Asymmetric risk/rewardWhat we’re seeing now is precisely the repolarization of the world I’ve been writing about for quite a while. The BRICS economies are stockpiling their reserves and restructuring themselves. China and Russia can achieve near-complete self-sufficiency, and their exports are essential to the global market, so they will always find enough demand for their goods. Tariffs and sanctions can’t seriously weaken them. Not to mention that China, Japan, and the EU hold a significant portion of U.S. debt—if they start selling, the U.S. will be in big trouble.

It’s also important to remember that defeating China is not the goal; rather, the aim is to bring the EU to its knees, and eventually buy China out of the international economy in the future (in coalition with Russia and the Arabians).However, the unfavorable risk/reward for Trump is that, although the U.S. economy and military power remain the strongest and also capable of self-sufficiency (Oceania, etc.), he stands to lose much more and gain relatively little, while China can gain a lot and lose relatively little.

If China succeeds in establishing a promising economic network while the U.S. experiences a significant loss of influence, Arab and Russian investors could back out of the agreement and permanently shift toward China. They’re not driven by Trump’s interests—they care about business, even if, ideologically, they could partner with the right wing in the long run.Meanwhile, it’s crucial for the U.S. to maintain its military superpower status, which currently secures its leading position in the world. However, if alliances and trust are permanently shaken—as I’ve mentioned before—those dormant conflicts will flare up, the U.S. military will become overextended, and it won’t be able to fund its defense budget.The time works against Trump.
