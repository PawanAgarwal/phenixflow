Weekly post (13-17/April), Vol selling coming back

As said on last Tuesday I expect stabilization into April OpEx. Once again, I’m linking my March article on geostrategy for you. At the bottom of that post, I’ve included every relevant update so you can get a clear view of the process. These times, it’s all about geopolitical realignment. It’s crucial to understand these dynamics—take the time to do it.

Where do we stand? Trump wants to prevent the EU from finding a new sugardaddy in Xi, because if the EU became confident about relying on China, it’d be harder to force the globalists to accept a full truce.Restructuring supply chains takes time, but from Trump’s perspective, he can’t let the EU get comfortable. Klaus Schwab’s resignation was a positive sign toward that end so far.On the other hand, as I’ve mentioned, Trump needs a deflation scare, not actual deflation. So the Fed must quietly step in as a buyer, bit by bit—a kind of “hidden QE” to prevent the economy from a crisis. To stay hidden is vital, given the current high volatility. If volatility is elevated, the sentiment is tense. And so if they’d suddenly announce a supportive pre-FOMC QE, the extreme fear could flip to extreme complacency—bad news for the fight against inflation.Instead, the market needs to consolidate at these lower levels and volatility must decline. The negative wealth effect has to run its course. That’s why I maintain a careful short vol, long delta bias into the April OpEx.

“Careful”, because still, watch for the unexpected: a sudden decision could undo progress and reset the cycle. I don’t think that’ll happen, but remember—until you see the EU back away from the idea of new economic deals with China, there’s no green light. Plus, there’s still the ECB meeting ahead plus the bad (potentially deflationary April datas). So after April OpEx, volatility might return, though likely with a lower high. And if we get shorter SPX/LTY correlation (in case if geos goes okay), the bad datas will be good news.My own positioning this week remains short vega, long theta, and modestly long delta—step by step (using only like 2% of the weekly profit for the first leg taken). Never forget, that the final confirmation will come with the full truce, after all, which is the ultimate goal behind this tariff mess.

While I’m writing this entry, Trump’s new post just came out…

It suggests that talks with Xi did not go well. The rare earths markets indicate that Xi is prepared for a prolonged fight for its independence from the US. The problem is that Xi is fully aware of the 'Holy Alliance 2.0' plan, which is not advantageous for him. Therefore, he undermines Trump wherever he can. Meanwhile, Trump, in order to remove the trump cards from Xi’s hand, wants to slowly buy out Chinese interests in the US and become industrially independent, while also wanting China not to offer alternative sources to the EU. This is not in China’s interest. Too much at once…

China is a patient adversary. It owes its great-power status to the hard and well-organized work of its people. After the second world war, that defeated country offered cheap labor for factory relocation. But China turned its weakness into an advantage, and little by little, all the major world powers became dependent on it. China today is no longer a tenant farmer but a superpower—one factor in the geopolitical sphere, second only to America. And the Chinese market’s actions show that it is ready to become independent from America and take away the US’s former partners (like the EU), which Trump is alienating through his impatient moves. As I’ve said, the US is far stronger than China, but the risk/reward ratio is not in Trump’s favor.

What is the priority now? - A ceasefire. That is the most urgent danger, and it must be addressed first. The EU is needed for that. Trump must give up his drive for independence from China. Xi has repeatedly stated that he is open to negotiating with America. Trump must understand he can’t have everything at once. He must offer Xi something that yields a bigger return than the loss from letting go of the EU.It is very important to watch the news. Everything depends on these developments, in this context. Volatility will likely remain at elevated levels but with vomma decreasing a bit, and it’s crucial to monitor the market day by day—especially the bond market and the Fed balance sheet and bond volatility.

I still believe the Fed will support the market because it’s effectively forced to do so in order to avoid actual deflationary spiral. Meanwhile, tax loss harvesting is underway, with hedge funds cover reported losses from these messy price movements. On April 16, we’ll likely see new tariffs announcement. While OpEx flows appear supportive, once VIX expo and monthly expo are done, volatility will very likely return to the market, at least until the ECB meeting, which will be a very important event. The odds favor the first US rate cut in June.The market’s bottom is not confirmed. There’s still a risk of dipping below 4800 before truly bottoming (as mentioned earlier, even 4300 can’t be ruled out). This is a real risk that must be considered. Be very careful in OpEx week—day-by-day caution is best.And once again: final bottom will only be confirmed once the full truce is in place.Trump must be patient.
