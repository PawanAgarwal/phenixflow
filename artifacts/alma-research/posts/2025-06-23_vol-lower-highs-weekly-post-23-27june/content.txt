So while the flip points slide farther, the vol effect on deltas decreases and the moves can slowly accelerate as local gammas will also be eaten up by the zomma. GEO RISKIn a further escalation case, the conflict could broaden – for example, Iran might refuse to back down and continue or increase missile attacks on U.S. bases or Gulf infrastructure, prompting direct U.S. military action. In this worst-case scenario, stagflationary shock would hit: Oil would skyrocket well above $120-130, add roughly 2% points to U.S. CPI in the short run, equity markets would lurch into the bear market territory and safe havens (Treasuries, gold, the U.S. dollar) would surge. Volatility would almost certainly overshoot prior highs – even VIX above 40s if U.S. and Iran enter open conflict. Credit spreads would widen significantly as risk aversion spiked. Essentially, an escalation risks importing a Middle East war premium into all asset prices: higher energy and defense stocks, but broadly lower everything else. Potential triggers for this scenario in coming weeks include: Iran activating Hezbollah or other proxies against Israel (expanding the warfront), a high-casualty event that hardens positions, or a mistake at sea (like an Iranian missile hitting a U.S. warship or commercial tanker).

My base case however is that the conflict will not expand to fully choke off oil. The probability of a deliberate Hormuz closure seems low-risk as Iran would be damaging its own positions especially by irritating its main customer: China. For now, it is all controlled.
