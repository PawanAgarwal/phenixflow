INVESTMENT (big boys mindset) I’ve already written about the market’s fundamental driving principles. At root, these come down to the price levels at which investors are willing to buy or sell. They have various methods for determining those levels, which I’ve also outlined briefly. Liquidity providers balance supply and demand (especially against speculative flows), while investors finance companies’ operations and a country’s debt, thereby supporting growth. But sometimes a particular event will create uncertainty among investors…

When that happens, serious problems arise both in the market and in the broader economy. If investors pull out, no one remains to finance either the government’s spending and economic growth. That leads to a general slowdown—and in some cases a deflationary spiral. The withdrawal of investors’ capital causes a massive liquidity drain in the markets, driving prices sharply lower and triggering a chain reaction of margin calls. That deleveraging event then removes even more money from the economy, producing a negative-wealth effect that further depresses prices (a disinf-/deflationary force).

In such situations, it falls to the government to rescue the economy through both fiscal and monetary measures. First, by deploying various emergency mechanisms (which I’ve also described briefly). And second, by restoring investor confidence. It must pinpoint why investors are withdrawing and address that root problem—offering them a deal that draws them back, convinces them their capital is safe, and makes financing government spending worthwhile. It is important to note that, investors never “short” their finances; they always go long. To invest is to finance a company’s or a government’s operations because you believe in its growth. In return, the company or state rewards you with ‘dividends’ paid from that growth. (They only enter into speculative trades if they’re setting aside money for hedging purposes or if they want to make a little side income. But I can tell you for fact that most of them suck at speculative trading.)Many people simply aren’t familiar with this.

SUPPLY-CHAIN ISSUES (bullwhip cycle on the horizont, hidden inflation)The supply-chain issues that I rang the bell on Friday are something I want to elaborate on a bit…Policy is lagging. A blanket 10% tariff on most imports kicked in April 5, followed by steep country‐specific levies (up to 145% on Chinese goods) on April 9—triggering a data drop in freight volumes. Since mid‐March, U.S. imports from China plunged 36–43% at major ports, and container‐ship departures have fallen from about 70 to 50 ships per day. Booking cancellations reached 50% on China–North America lanes. Retailers typically order holiday and seasonal merchandise 6–8 weeks in advance; with stockpiles run down and no new shipments arriving (amid tariff-driven order freezes), shortages will translate to empty shelves in roughly 3–5 weeks. Now, as tariffs hit on April 5 and April 9, many importers halted new orders. Combined with existing voyage durations, this means no new containers leave China → 3–5 weeks later → no arrivals → empty shelvesBy mid-May to early June, stores and distribution centers will exhaust on-hand stock. U.S. retailers report lower same‐store sales, narrower product assortments, and downgraded guidance. These data points will register instantly on ticker‐based trade‐sales indices and in conference‐call transcripts as sales disappointments and margin compression. Rather than showing up as a spike in CPI immediately, these shortages will first manifest through compressed profit margins, higher wholesale prices, and falling real wages. As stock‐outs proliferate, consumers’ buying patterns shift, intensifying the bullwhip effect—where small upstream shortages cause larger downstream demand volatility—further destabilizing both prices and spending. (In short, bullwhip effect is a phenomenon where small fluctuations in consumer demand lead to larger variations in orders placed upstream in the supply chain.)Goods most at risk include low‐margin items like apparel, toys, footwear, and household electronics, which rely heavily on Chinese manufacturing (70%+ for toys, large shares of footwear and home furnishings). Specialty and small‐format retailers lacking scale to front‐load or absorb higher duties will see the earliest and sharpest impacts.

Retail and consumer‐discretionary stocks will show warning signals: companies posting inventory write‐downs or guidance cuts should see immediate share‐price weakness. Short‐dated corporate credit could widen as cash flows are squeezed; bond‐market volatility may rise as funding costs climb amidst margin stress. The timing aligns with the May 2 de minimis tariff change—another potential catalyst—so investors should brace for renewed volatility around both retail‐sales data and customs‐fee implementations.
