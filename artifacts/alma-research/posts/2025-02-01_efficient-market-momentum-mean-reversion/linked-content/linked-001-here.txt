Game of probabilities

Moderate math warning!

In the following post, I will define the concepts of determinism and stochasticism to clarify and refine the approach. I will also explain what 'probability' actually means from a mathematical perspective, as an introduction to the fundamentals of risk management.

Determinism means that the final output of a process is causally inevitable. It implies that a process is entirely driven by causality, and the outcome depends solely on the variables involved. There is no randomness, and thus the system will always produce the same output from a given starting condition or initial state.For example, imagine a computer running a well-defined algorithm. It will always produce the same output for the same inputs, calculated deterministically. Our classical physical laws have also been viewed as deterministic because they are based on differential equations and function algorithmically.

However, in the real world, no system is truly deterministic—not even a computer. Using deterministic models is a simplification we often employ when analyzing processes at a macro level. This simplification works because it is easier and "good enough" to describe certain systems this way (e.g., the path of a thrown rock).Why is it "good enough"? Simply because complex systems tend to exhibit properties that their individual parts do not possess. When these parts interact with one another, they create a larger unit or system that produces well-observed, repeating patterns, unified behaviors, or properties that can be described deterministically. This phenomenon is known as emergence.

Take the example of a thrown rock: beyond the macro level, countless variables and forces (wind, temperature, air pressure, momentum of small particles, molecules, etc.) influence its path. However, on a macro scale, the rock follows a clear trajectory that can be described reasonably well using deterministic models. If you throw it again with the same force, the path will be almost identical.Similarly, society is composed of thousands of individuals, each with their unique inner world. Yet, as a collective, society exhibits regular behavioral patterns.(This additive effect of random processes is a primary subject of chaos theory.)

On the other hand, when we discuss random processes, we refer to stochasticism. Stochastic systems are entirely random, meaning there is a 50-50% probability for various outcomes, as discussed in my previous article on volatility.

To describe a stochastic system and understand how it operates, we use probability calculus to identify patterns and regularities in its behavior. In a stochastic system, the process is influenced by numerous independent variables. Unlike deterministic systems, where variables interact to create predictable outcomes, the variables in stochastic systems do not necessary interact, and so it is not necessary causality to exists between them.

Consider the stock market, for example: the price action results from decisions made by millions of individuals, with each action carrying a different weight. These weights also change continuously and randomly, and decisions are influenced by sentiments, knowledge, intelligence, etc. In other words, the stock market consists of innumerable random variables determined by other random variables, which in turn are influenced by other randomly changing variables, and so on.

In such a system, deterministic calculus cannot be applied, as there are billions (if not infinite) determinants, and each determinant is itself a multivariable random function. What we can do instead is analyze the system at a macro level (e.g., the overall price action of the market) and search for patterns in its behavior. By identifying these patterns, we can infer possible outcomes and assign probability values to each of them.Probability theory, being tautological—as you will see—can function effectively if likelihoods are estimated with sufficient accuracy. However, the key takeaway here is that we have no benchmark in the present. A benchmark only exists once the outcome has occurred, at which point we can evaluate how accurate or close our estimations were.

This is why strategy testing on historical data is crucial. The closer a model fits historical data, the more reliable it may be in the future, provided events remain within the main body of the distribution and do not fall into the tails.This also explains why no one can truthfully claim, after making an accurate prediction, "I knew it." They merely assumed the outcome with sufficient accuracy but did not know anything with certainty.

PROBABILITY At the very basics, probability is simply the ratio of the set of observed outcomes to the set of all possible outcomes. For example, if you come across a question in your child's math homework like this: "given a square with an area of 20 × 20 m, and a circle with an area of 120 m inside it, what is the probability that, if I throw a pebble onto the square, it will land within the circle?" You should know that the probability is just the ratio of the two areas: the area of the circle divided by the area of the square—nothing more.If you multiply this ratio by 100, you'll get the probability in percentage format. Thus the answer is 30%. This means that 30 out of 100 times, your pebble will hit the circle, but 70 out of 100 times, it won’t.

Is this true? Well, a basic assumption of probability calculus is that, in the long run, this ratio becomes accurate. How does this happen? Let’s say the experiment involves rolling a die, and the event we are interested in is getting a 6. We can start rolling the die continuously and record the number of times the result is a 6. If we divide this number by the total number of rolls, we get what is called the relative frequency—the ratio of the number of outcomes in which the specified event occurs to the total number of trials.Then, we roll again and recalculate it. In probability calculus, we call "elementary events" the different possible outcomes of an experiment.For example, imagine an n-sided die. When we roll the die, the number we get (1, 2, ..., n) will be the outcome of the experiment. Every experiment has only one outcome. We denote these outcomes using ω. The set of all possible outcomes is denoted by Ω, which is called the event space.Events are subsets of the event space.

What is the difference between an elementary event and an event? Well, if I roll the die, the elementary event is getting a specific outcome, like 1, 2, or 3, etc. An event, on the other hand, can be defined as a set of outcomes that satisfy a certain condition. For example, an event could be rolling an odd number, which would include the subset {1, 3, 5}. So I can define any subset A I want.
