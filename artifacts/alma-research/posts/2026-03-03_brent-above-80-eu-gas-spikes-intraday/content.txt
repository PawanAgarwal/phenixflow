West-East war is escalating, Hormuz, Oil, Inflation | Weekly post (2-6/March)

Brent

Brent crude has broken above the $80 per barrel level. But why has it not climbed higher?

The reason is that the attacks and destruction of local oil refineries have left crude oil trapped in storage tanks, thereby reducing local demand. Saudi Aramco has shut down the Ras Tanura refinery. While the company is clearly seeking to avoid panic, smoke-penetrating SAR satellites and NASA’s VIIRS heat maps reveal critical structural damage—scorch marks—and extensive fires at the central cracking and cooling towers. The refinery is operating under Force Majeure and has been brought to a complete standstill.In addition, refined products (diesel, kerosene) are disappearing from the market, creating a negative supply shock. For the time being, the market is pricing in the expectation that the United States and the IEA will release strategic reserves (SPR), which should partially mitigate the shock.

However, the true extent of the risk remains impossible to assess accurately due to the intense local information war. The IRGC has officially announced that it will set fire to every vessel passing through the strait; it is currently relying on China’s BeiDou-3 military navigation network. Insurers have immediately cancelled all war-risk coverage. A Marshall Islands-flagged tanker was struck by a drone off the coast of Oman. According to Kpler data, tanker traffic has collapsed, and navigation has become impossible due to Iranian GPS spoofing.

EU gas price

An Iranian drone attack has also forced QatarEnergy—one of the world’s largest LNG exporters—to completely halt liquefied natural gas production at its Ras Laffan facility. Since vessels cannot sail out of the Strait of Hormuz in any case, Europe (which replaced Russian pipeline gas with Qatari LNG) now faces renewed risks of inflation and industrial shutdowns.

China vs RussiaMore than 80% of Iran’s oil exports reach China via the so-called shadow fleet. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, feedstock supply to China’s independent refiners is shrinking. While Chinese and Russian vessels can still transit the area, passage is slower and far riskier for them as well. Consequently, the Chinese are being forced to strike behind-the-scenes deals with Russia or, at a later stage, to deploy units of the People’s Liberation Army Navy (PLAN) in the Gulf of Oman to escort their own tankers.Because Middle Eastern refined products (especially diesel) and crude have become stuck, African and Asian buyers—led by India and China—are now turning to Russian refineries. The previous sanctions-induced discount on Russian Urals crude has virtually evaporated, allowing Moscow to realise windfall profits from the chaos.

It is also very important to mention that the United States and NATO are reallocating significant quantities of air-defence systems and ammunition to the Middle East theatre. This shift materially reduces Ukraine’s defensive resilience. In light of the developments outlined above, the associated risk is extremely high.
