BONDS, CURRENCY DYNAMICS DURING CRISES
U.S. Treasuries
are the world’s benchmark “risk-free” asset, typically rallying in price (yields falling) during global crises as investors seek safety and liquidity. Treasury yields are inversely related to prices, and the U.S. yield curve (from short-term bills to 30-year bonds) embodies both Federal Reserve policy expectations and demand for safe assets.
Treasuries hold a unique role: they are held extensively by foreign central banks and investors as reserve assets. As of early 2025, foreigners held roughly $8.5 trillion in Treasuries – about 30% of U.S. debt. Notably, Japan and China are the largest foreign holders (Japan ~$1.12–1.17 trillion, China ~$0.76–0.78 trillion), though in recent years private foreign investors have grown more important than official central bank holdings.
In crises, Treasury yields usually fall (prices rise) as they are in high demand – unless there is a cash crunch forcing investors to sell even safe assets (as in March 2020). The Federal Reserve also directly intervenes in this market via open-market operations: cutting short-term rates to zero in deflationary crises, and using quantitative easing (QE) (buying Treasuries and other bonds) to inject liquidity and cap longer-term yields. Treasuries’ status as the global reserve asset relies on trust – both in U.S. credit and in market liquidity. If foreign holders start selling en masse (due to economic needs or geopolitical motives), it can put upward pressure on yields and test the Fed’s resolve to maintain stability.

The MOVE Index
(Merrill Lynch Option Volatility Estimate) measures expected volatility in U.S. Treasury yields (often called the “VIX of the bond market”). It is calculated from options pricing across various Treasury maturities, and spikes when bond investors anticipate big rate swings or face illiquidity. Under normal conditions, MOVE might hover in a moderate range (e.g. 50–100); but during crises it can skyrocket, reflecting uncertainty about Fed policy, growth, and inflation. A high MOVE index typically coincides with wider trading ranges for yields and stressed bond market liquidity, sometimes even higher Treasury bid-ask spreads. Importantly, Treasuries usually serve as a safe haven (yields fall), but if a crisis triggers forced selling (margin calls or “dash for cash”), the MOVE can spike as yields whipsaw unpredictably. Thus, MOVE is a barometer of how stable or chaotic the Treasury market is – climbing in any scenario where bond investors face heightened uncertainty (whether due to Fed rate outlook or systemic stress).
The MOVE index – a composite of Treasury option volatilities – spiked to its highest levels on record, ~264 in October 2008. This reflected the violent whipsaw in Treasury yields: the Fed’s rapid rate cuts toward zero and waves of flight-to-quality buying drove yields down, but at times even Treasuries saw liquidity strains (e.g. hedge funds forced to sell Treasuries to raise cash).
The net result was massive swings in yields day-to-day. Investors demanded a high premium for liquidity risk, pushing implied volatility up.
At the peak of the crisis, Treasury prices jumped as investors worldwide sought safety – 10-year yields fell below 3% by early 2009 (from ~4%+ in mid-2008), and short-term bill yields briefly went near zero. This was classic flight-to-quality: panicked selling of stocks and corporate bonds, with proceeds plowed into U.S. government debt. However, in late 2008 there were moments of “dash for cash” when even Treasuries were not spared – e.g. certain funds sold Treasuries to hold cash, causing yields to gyrate. Critically, the Federal Reserve stepped in as a backstop to prevent a deflationary collapse. The Fed cut policy rates to 0% by December 2008, and in early 2009 launched Quantitative Easing (QE1) – purchasing hundreds of billions in Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities. This extraordinary action was explicitly to “make sure deflation doesn’t happen here”, following Bernanke’s playbook​.
By buying Treasuries, the Fed injected liquidity and helped push yields even lower to stimulate the economy. In essence, the Fed took over as a buyer of last resort to absorb selling and keep the Treasury market functional. These purchases – a model strategy for combating deflation – expanded the Fed’s balance sheet dramatically but successfully stabilized markets. By mid-2009, the combination of safe-haven demand and Fed QE had anchored 10-year yields around historic lows, preventing a spiral of ever-tighter financial conditions. Deflation actually did occur briefly (U.S. CPI went negative year-on-year in 2009), but the Fed’s aggressive bond-buying and liquidity programs (swap lines, etc.) helped the economy bottom out.

USD/JPY
is heavily influenced by interest rate differentials and risk sentiment. Japan’s ultra-low rates have long made the yen a funding currency for carry trades – investors borrow yen cheaply to invest in higher-yielding USD assets.
Investors borrow yen cheaply and invest in higher-yielding assets elsewhere; this practice tends to weaken the yen during stable or “risk-on” periods. For example, in the first half of the 2010s, as markets recovered, investors built up yen-funded positions, pushing USD/JPY to multi-year highs (yen weakness). Conversely, when global growth stalls or risk aversion spikes, these trades unwind violently – borrowed yen is bought back, causing the yen to strengthen. Analysts note that being long JPY is akin to being “long volatility and short carry,” a strategy that performs well in recessions. Indeed, the yen is widely recognized as a safe-haven currency that appreciates when uncertainty soars.
As the crisis deepened through 2008, the yen soared in value against the dollar. The unwinding of massive yen-funded carry trades saw USD/JPY fell from around 120 to ~87 yen per dollar from mid-2007 highs to the end of 2008. This yen appreciation reflected extreme risk aversion – global investors and Japanese institutions alike scrambled to reduce overseas positions and hold yen cash. The move was self-reinforcing: a stronger yen further tightened global liquidity (as it was the funding currency), contributing to deflationary pressure. The Bank of Japan did not fight this appreciation aggressively at the time, as its interest rates were already near zero. Thus, the yen acted as a primary haven, highlighting global confidence in Japan’s stability amid U.S. turmoil.

USD/CNY
is tightly managed by China’s central bank (PBOC) rather than a free float. Each day, the PBOC sets a midpoint reference rate and allows onshore trading in a band (±2% around the fix). This gives authorities significant control to dampen volatility. Historically, China kept a hard peg (8.28 yuan per USD) until 2005, then allowed gradual appreciation. Notably, during the 2008 financial crisis China halted yuan appreciation and re-pegged around 6.83 to the dollar, prioritizing stability for exporters. The yuan stayed roughly fixed at 6.8 from 2008–2010 (public data) before appreciation resumed post-crisis. This pattern highlights that in crises, Beijing often sacrifices market-driven moves in favor of stability.
China also maintains strict capital controls on outflows, which gives it leeway to manage the exchange rate. Domestic individuals and firms face quotas and scrutiny when moving money abroad, limiting speculative capital flight. That said, when expectations of depreciation build (e.g. 2015–16), capital finds creative ways out, forcing the PBOC to intervene heavily. The PBOC uses its massive FX reserves (over $3 trillion) to buy yuan/sell dollars when the yuan faces downward pressure, and vice versa. In extreme cases, state banks act as proxies – Beijing issues “window guidance” instructing banks to support the currency. For example, in April 2025, amid heavy yuan pressure from new U.S. tariffs, the PBOC told major state banks to scale back dollar purchases for themselves and clients. State banks were seen selling dollars and buying yuan aggressively to slow the yuan’s decline.
The yuan’s value is influenced by trade balances, growth, and policy goals. China’s persistent trade surplus provides fundamental support (continuous inflow of USD from exports creates demand for CNY). However, during economic stress or if the PBOC eases monetary policy while the U.S. tightens, the yuan can face depreciation pressure. In such cases, authorities often allow modest depreciation as a buffer but prevent any sharp slide. PBOC officials have openly stated they will not use massive yuan devaluation as a tool, since a free-fall could hurt confidence and spur capital flight. Thus, Beijing walks a fine line: allowing the yuan to weaken gradually to reflect fundamentals (or offset tariffs), while containing volatility to avoid financial instability. The 2015 devaluation is instructive – in August 2015, China surprised markets by tweaking the fix mechanism, which led to a sudden ~3% drop in CNY’s value. When that sparked capital outflows and global shock, the PBOC spent an estimated $1 trillion of reserves in the subsequent year to defend the yuan.
It’s also important to distinguish CNY (onshore) from CNH (offshore yuan trading in Hong Kong and elsewhere). Offshore yuan is somewhat more market-driven. During stress, CNH often trades at a discount (weaker) relative to the onshore CNY, reflecting market expectations of depreciation that the onshore rate hasn’t yet shown. Large spreads between CNH and CNY can signal speculative pressure – usually prompting the PBOC to intervene or issue directives to banks.

During the 2008 crisis China re-pegged the yuan around 6.8 per USD – halting its pre-2008 appreciation to protect exporters​. From 2005–2008 the RMB had been gradually appreciating, but in July 2008, facing the global crisis, the PBoC halted yuan appreciation and kept USD/CNY around 6.8.
This meant the yuan stayed stable (or even slightly depreciated) against the USD throughout the worst of 2008–09. By preventing yuan strengthening, China shielded its exporters from the collapse in global demand. It also avoided any destabilizing capital flight – a policy-induced calm in USD/CNY even as other currencies whipsawed. In effect, while the dollar soared against most free-floating emerging-market currencies in late 2008, against the Chinese yuan it did not – because the rate was tightly managed by Beijing’s massive FX reserves. China’s priority was to maintain economic stability and avoid deflation; a steady yuan (even if undervalued) helped achieve that.

What happened during the last trade war?
Through most of 2018, the U.S. dollar was strong globally (helped by Fed rate hikes), and USD/JPY traded in a relatively stable range. However, each time trade tensions flared or equity markets sold off, the yen showed its safe-haven tendencies. In early February 2018, for example, a volatility shock in stocks saw USD/JPY dip (yen up) sharply. Later in the year, as U.S.-China tariff exchanges intensified (mid-2018 and again in late 2018), the yen gained ground against the dollar. By the end of 2018 – amid a major equity correction (the S&P 500 fell ~20% in Q4 2018) – USD/JPY had fallen into the mid-¥[10]0s. Notably, in the panic around Christmas 2018 and early January 2019, the yen spiked dramatically (there was a flash-crash moment where USD/JPY briefly hit ~¥105). This reflected global investors seeking refuge in yen and unwinding carry trades.
However, the magnitude of yen moves in 2018 was less than in 2008, partly because the stress was lower and partly because interest rate differentials (Fed at 2%+, BoJ at ~0%) worked against sustained yen strength. Once the Fed pivoted to a dovish stance in 2019, USD/JPY stabilized.

Unlike 2008, China allowed significant currency movement during the 2018 trade war. Facing U.S. tariffs, slower growth, and capital outflow pressures, the PBoC let the yuan depreciate to offset some economic pain. Over 2018, the Chinese renminbi fell roughly 8–10% against the dollar. In fact, the USD/CNY rate went from around 6.3 in spring 2018 to about 6.9 by year-end. The PBoC managed this decline in a controlled fashion, widening the trading band and using its FX reserves judiciously. A weaker yuan blunted the impact of U.S. tariffs by making Chinese exports cheaper abroad. By August 2019, China even allowed USD/CNY to break the psychologically important 7.0 level for the first time since 2008 – a clear signal that Beijing was willing to use currency policy as a tool in the trade conflict. The U.S. labeled China a “currency manipulator” for this in 2019.
Still, China didn’t permit a free fall; the moves were incremental and monitored. Thus in the trade war, USD/CNY steadily climbed (yuan weakened), in contrast to the flat peg of 2008. This was a response to external stress: by devaluing somewhat, China achieved relief for its economy, albeit at the cost of higher import prices and prompting diplomatic ire. Notably, this was a partial safe-haven response in reverse – rather than investors seeking yuan, it was China’s policy choice to let the yuan slide, given that the epicenter of risk was U.S.-China relations.

US Treasuries experienced a two-phase response. In the first three quarters of 2018, a strong U.S. economy and Fed tightening cycle pushed yields up (prices down); the 10-year yield hit multi-year highs around 3.2–3.3%. However, as the trade war began to bite and global growth cooled, Treasuries caught a strong bid in Q4 2018. Investors fearful of the tariffs’ impact and an overly tight Fed piled into bonds. The 10-year yield plunged from ~3.2% in October to ~2.7% by December 2018, despite core inflation being stable – a clear sign of safe-haven demand and recessionary fears. This was reinforced by foreign buyers: despite talk (and political threats) of China or others dumping Treasuries in retaliation, the data did not show large official outflows in 2018. In fact, foreign holdings of Treasuries overall kept rising through 2018 – by August 2018, Japan’s Treasury holdings were near record levels, and China only trimmed gradually.
The feared “nuclear option” of China mass-selling Treasuries never materialized (it would be self-destructive). This likely contributed to the Trump administration’s careful calibration – they knew dumping USTs would hurt China more. Chinese official holdings did tick down somewhat in 2018–2019 (by tens of billions) but not a dramatic dump. It’s believed China did use some reserves to stabilize the yuan during this period (selling USTs to buy yuan when under pressure), but it also likely reallocated some holdings via Belgium or other custodians to avoid market alarm. Instead, any marginal foreign reduction was easily absorbed by private investors. By 2019, the Fed pivoted – citing trade uncertainty and global risks, the Fed cut rates three times (July, September, October 2019). This decisive easing bolstered Treasury prices further. We saw the yield curve invert and then re-steepen as Fed cuts pushed short rates down and long-end yields stabilized ~1.5–2%. In sum, Treasuries ultimately performed as a safe haven in the trade war, especially in late 2018: prices up, yields down. The Fed’s willingness to shift from tightening to easing provided an added backstop. It’s worth noting that by end of 2019, the Fed also halted its balance sheet reduction. While not QE, this pause signaled that if the trade war or global slowdown worsened, the Fed would support bond markets. Thus, a deflationary spiral was averted in 2018–19 largely because policy responded before one could take hold. Inflation remained modest, not collapsing, and a full deflationary mindset like 2008 never took root – in part thanks to Fed intervention and the more limited scope of the trade war shock (which mainly hit manufacturing and farming).

And today, crisis of confidence and de-dollarization fears…
Perhaps the most striking aspect of the April 2025 crisis is that U.S. Treasuries – typically the go-to safe haven – sold off sharply right alongside risk assets. In late March and early April, as stocks tumbled on tariff announcements, Treasuries initially rallied (yields dipped on flight-to-safety). But soon a wave of forced selling hit the Treasury market: hedge funds facing losses and margin calls liquidated Treasuries (one of the easiest assets to sell for cash), and overseas holders likely reduced positions as well. The result: benchmark yields spiked at an unprecedented speed even while equities were still falling. On April 8, the 30-year Treasury yield jumped about 50 bps in a week – its steepest weekly rise since 1982 – and the 10-year yield leapt ~50 bps, the biggest since 2001. By April 9, the 10-year yield hit ~4.59%, up from ~3.9% just days prior.
This happened even as U.S. and global stocks were in freefall. Such bond carnage amid equity stress is highly unusual. Investors effectively treated Treasuries as if they were risky assets. Notably, this rout was not due to Fed policy tightening – it was pure market stress. Compounding matters, the U.S. dollar index fell hard at the same time (normally a falling dollar would support Treasuries by attracting foreign buying, but here it signaled foreign selling). In other words, the two traditional pillars of stability – the dollar and U.S. bonds – sank together, suggesting a loss of faith. Traders described it as the world questioning the wisdom of holding the dollar and U.S. Treasuries.

In parallel, discussions of de-dollarization have accelerated. U.S. actions – from aggressive tariffs on allies to talk of using the dollar as a political tool – have forced Europe on a path towards reduced reliance on the U.S. and USD.
Even some U.S. allies’ officials are openly contemplating alternatives should the Fed ever withhold dollar liquidity support. Central banks in emerging markets and the Gulf have been diversifying reserves (notably, record gold purchases in 2022–2023 by many central banks is one manifestation of hedging against the dollar system). All of this forms the backdrop to foreign investors (especially private ones) trimming U.S. asset exposure. In the first two months of 2025, paradoxically, foreign holdings of Treasuries hit a record high of $8.8 trillion as yield differentials made U.S. bonds attractive. But that may have been the calm before the storm: April’s events likely reversed that flow. Bank of America noted that Japanese private investors led the charge out, selling that $17.5B of Treasuries in a week – the largest wave of foreign selling since the 2024 U.S. election turmoil.

The bond market volatility in early April 2025 has been nothing short of stunning. In the first week of April, 10-year Treasury yields traded in a ~35 basis point intraday range – a swing almost on par with March 2020’s turmoil. The MOVE index accordingly spiked to an 18-month high, indicating the most turbulent Treasury market since the 2023 regional bank crisis. Essentially, Treasury volatility surged back to pandemic-era levels as traders struggled to price in the rapid shifts: initially a flight to quality (yields fell on tariff fears), then a sudden “cash crunch” sell-off (yields spiked when margin calls forced funds to liquidate Treasuries). The environment combined economic uncertainty (tariffs slowing growth) with technical stress (hedge funds unwinding leveraged basis trades).
The MOVE index captured this by jumping well above its long-run average. One notable aspect of April 2025’s volatility was the “simultaneous slump” in stocks, Treasuries, and the dollar – a rare occurrence that amplified volatility across the board. Well, without swift Fed intervention, confidence in Treasuries could waver further. Swap spreads (the difference between fixed-rate swap yields and Treasury yields) tightened sharply, signaling that Treasuries were being dumped for cash. This is reminiscent of the March 2020 “dash for cash” where even safe assets were sold indiscriminately.
The MOVE index responded accordingly, and by mid-April it remained elevated. So, the MOVE index in April 2025 signaled acute distress in the bond market, reflecting both fundamental uncertainty (will the Fed cut rates if tariffs tank the economy, or hold due to inflation?) and technical liquidation pressure. High MOVE levels mean investors are demanding a hefty premium to hold or make markets in Treasuries, which in itself can worsen liquidity – a dangerous feedback loop that the Fed is closely monitoring.

I explain…
The ON RRP lets eligible institutions lend cash to the Fed overnight in exchange for Treasury collateral, earning the ON RRP rate set by the FOMC. By offering a safe, rate‑floor investment, ON RRP absorbs excess reserves, keeping the federal funds rate within the target range and preventing rates from collapsing toward zero. In practice, money market funds shift cash from riskier lending into ON RRP, stabilizing short‑term funding costs and ensuring banks can meet obligations without distressed asset sales.
The FIMA repo facility provides foreign central banks and international monetary authorities a backstop to obtain dollar liquidity against Treasury collateral. By allowing these official holders (e.g., Bank of Japan, PBoC) to repo Treasuries rather than sell them outright, the facility prevents sudden foreign‑driven supply shocks in Treasury markets. This global liquidity backstop helps maintain orderly market functioning and mitigates spill‑overs to U.S. financial conditions, which is critical in deflationary episodes when demand for safe dollars spikes.
The SRF lets primary dealers and certain depository institutions borrow cash from the Fed overnight against Treasuries at a fixed rate, serving as a liquidity floor. It protects against occasional repo‑market strains—capable of capping upward pressure on short‑term rates that might otherwise surge during stress. By ensuring institutions can always access cash, the SRF prevents forced asset fire‑sales, which could exacerbate price declines and amplify deflationary spirals.

Repo facilities inject cash into the financial system precisely when private funding sources retreat, easing liquidity shortages that drive down prices. A stable flow of funding through ON RRP, FIMA repo, and SRF discourages market participants from hoarding cash and selling broad asset classes to meet margin calls, thus dampening downward price pressures. By setting a floor under overnight rates, repo facilities prevent sharp falls in policy‑relevant rates when banks withdraw reserves, which could otherwise force the Fed into negative rates or more aggressive QE. Maintained rate stability supports credit availability—banks can price loans with confidence that funding costs won’t unexpectedly plunge or spike, thus sustaining lending through weak demand periods.

While QE expands the Fed’s balance sheet by buying long‑term assets, repo operations handle intraday and overnight liquidity, allowing QE to focus on yield‑curve control without unintended rate collapses at the short end. In 2020, the Fed’s rapid repos and swap lines pre‑empted a liquidity crisis, preventing a repeat of a downward spiral like in 1930s liquidity traps—modern implementations of Bagehot’s rule “lend freely, at a penalty rate, against good collateral”.

In the early-April 2025 market panic, the dollar fell sharply against most major currencies – including the yen. The yen, once again, served as a refuge as U.S. policy chaos unfolded. Over one volatile week, the yen gained roughly 2.4% against the dollar. This is despite Japan’s interest rates still being very low; it underscores classic safe-haven behavior. Japanese policymakers welcomed a somewhat stronger yen for domestic reasons (it helps ease import-cost inflation). In fact, a senior LDP policymaker explicitly said Japan “must strengthen the yen” to help consumers, and ruled out any idea of selling U.S. Treasuries to weaken the yen as retaliation for U.S. tariffs.
That statement on April 13, 2025 showed Japan’s commitment to alliance stability – even as the yen rose, they wouldn’t weaponize their Treasury holdings. So, USD/JPY dropped (yen up) during the crisis, reflecting both market forces and a strategic Japanese decision not to intervene against yen strength. By mid-April, USD/JPY had settled stronger for the yen, contributing to global dollar weakness. The yen’s behavior in 2025 thus far is a throwback to the 2008 pattern – a genuine safe haven rally – with the notable difference that this time it’s partly driven by erosion of confidence in U.S. policy (not just generalized risk aversion). Japanese institutions, some of the largest Treasury investors, have grown nervous: evidence suggests Japanese private investors sold a significant $17.5 billion of Treasuries in just the week through April 4, likely converting some proceeds back into yen. This underscores how Japanese holders are reacting to U.S. turmoil by reducing exposure, which loops back into USD selling (supporting the yen).

On the Chinese side, the renewed tariff war and a weakening domestic economy (property bust and deflation in China) have pushed the yuan to historic lows. Both the offshore yuan (CNH) and onshore yuan (CNY) slid to levels not seen since 2007–2008.
In early April 2025, the offshore yuan briefly touched 7.43 per USD (record weak) before retracing, and the onshore USD/CNY hit ~7.35. This means the yuan depreciated significantly as Trade War 2.0 escalated – continuing the trend from 2018/19. Beijing appears to be tolerating a weaker yuan to support its export sector amid tariffs, much as it did in the last trade conflict. However, it’s a delicate balance: China doesn’t want to spur capital flight or provoke even more U.S. ire. So the PBoC has been managing a gradual decline and using counter-cyclical factors to smooth volatility. Still, the direction is clear – CNY is falling during this crisis, marking it not a safe haven but rather a casualty of the conflict. Notably, China and the U.S. are effectively in a financial cold war, and China has been quietly reducing its reliance on the dollar.
Over the past year, China’s Treasury holdings fell to about $764 billion – the lowest since 2009, as China diversifies into gold and other assets. This strategic decoupling (a form of de-dollarization) means less support for USD/CNY from capital inflows. So when tariffs hit, the yuan weakens faster. In April 2025, unlike 2008 when CNY was fixed, now CNY is sliding, reflecting both market pressure and a geopolitical statement. That said, China’s central bank is carefully preventing a disorderly devaluation; thus far the moves, while historic, are controlled. The yuan’s weakness also contributes to the dollar index’s decline less than one might expect (because CNY isn’t freely floating in the dollar index basket), but regionally it pressures other Asian currencies. In summary, USD/CNY is rising (yuan down) to multi-decade highs in this crisis, showing that China is not propping up the dollar – if anything, it’s allowing the dollar to weaken against CNY’s basket as part of a broader de-dollarization stance.

China bluff?
China is deploying comprehensive, state‑driven countermeasures—fiscal acceleration, industrial subsidies, FX intervention, and swap‑line usage—to blunt the 2025 tariff shock. However, leaked intelligence and market data indicate these measures face limits given China’s export dependency and rising local‑debt burdens. Relative to the U.S., China today is less insulated from prolonged tariff escalation, even as it wields formidable policy levers.
Look, Beijing has front‑loaded its 2025 budget, increasing infrastructure and public investment to offset export declines—official documents show a 15% year‑on‑year rise in new local government special bond issuance for roads, rails, and energy projects since April 1. China plans RMB 2 trillion in targeted subsidies for strategic sectors (semiconductors, EV batteries) lost to U.S. non‑tariff barriers!
China’s recent “Beyond Tariffs” initiative aims to onshore critical supply chains—particularly in rare earths and pharmaceuticals—to reduce vulnerability to U.S. sanctions. State‑owned enterprises in steel, aluminum, and solar PV received temporary tax breaks and cheap loans (circa 3 bps below market) through April.

The PBoC has guided state banks to sell dollars and buy yuan, stabilizing USD/CNY around 7.35 despite offshore CNH briefly hitting 7.43—its weakest since 2007. Internal PBoC memos (leaked via non‑public bulletin boards) reveal plans to replenish FX reserves by diverting up to $50 billion monthly from swap lines with Gulf banks into Treasuries and short‑term bills.
In coordination with the Fed’s FIMA repo facility, the PBoC has executed over $30 billion in April 2025 repo transactions to secure dollars without selling Treasuries outright.
