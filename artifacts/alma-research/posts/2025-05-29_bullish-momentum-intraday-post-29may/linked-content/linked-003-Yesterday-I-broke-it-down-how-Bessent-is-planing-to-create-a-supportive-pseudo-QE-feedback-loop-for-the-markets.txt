Artificial QE?Markets in inflation swaps (e.g. 5y5y forwards) are only pricing in QE once the uncertainty around U.S.–China tariffs clears. Tariffs are the key blocker: until we know if they stay at 10%, snap back to 25–30%, or roll off entirely, neither the Fed nor swap markets believe cuts are safe.For now, the SPX/LTY beta tanked, signaling that the market is effectively rooting for “calmer growth” (i.e. neither strong inflation nor deep recession) so the Fed can pivot to cuts without being boxed in by too-hot or too-cold data.

Bessent is pushing regulators to manually unleash QE‐style liquidity by exempting Treasuries from the SLR this summer—effectively to force the loosening cycle, rather than waiting for the Fed’s data‐driven cuts.And this is where Soros comes into play…

Bessent cut his teeth at Soros Fund Management, mastering the reflexivity framework and policy-perception dynamics. He sees Treasury yields and bank behavior as prime targets for reflexive intervention—where regulatory tweaks can reshape market realities. Reflexivity posits that investor perceptions and economic fundamentals interact in a two-way feedback loop, so that market prices both reflect and shape reality—creating boom-bust cycles.With 10-year yields near 4.5%, swap spreads deeply negative, and CP issuance at record highs, the bond market is fragile. The Supplementary Leverage Ratio (SLR) forces banks to hold expensive capital against low-risk Treasuries, straining liquidity in the $29 trn U.S. bond market. Bessent’s plan is to announce SLR relief, that would make markets anticipate massive bank demand for Treasuries, it would free up to $2 trn of bank balance-sheet capacity. As a result yield spike expectations recede; long-bond yields fall, improving the equity discount rate (stocks rally). Lower yields and equity gains bolster bank capital ratios, validating further Treasury buying. Stronger bank balance sheets lead to more lending, fueling growth expectations and credit expansion. The cycle accelerates unless and until inflationary pressures force policy reversal.

Now, the problem is…Price-level targeting (PLT) would commit the Fed to make up past inflation misses, anchoring expectations even if actual rates rise temporarily—in effect “calming” inflation on paper. Also yield-curve control (YCC) could place caps on 10- or 30-year yields, forcing them lower via Fed operation-twist or/and outright purchases, achieving an “on-paper” drop in yields that supports stocks. If growth doesn’t slows too much. Because if it doesn, SPX/LTY beta will spike.However, even if Bessent’s plan succeeds, the tariff-induced cost shock will reignite inflation pressures and unravel the fragile equilibrium.

What does this mean for the market? Sustained positive drift for now, but the value of the drift is decreasing, the bullish trend flattens/cools. Expecting further reversion into tomorrow-Friday window, when rebalancing comes. For now, it is vol selling as said, and negative intraday speed to cap downside.
