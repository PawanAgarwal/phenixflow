Venezuela, US-CN, AI bubble, Geography, Inflation, Stagflation, Crisis

Overall, there is little scope for additional excess return from equity risk premia, credit spreads, and liquidity premia.And if Bessent fails to keep bond yields low and demand continues to weaken, yields may rise—which, with today’s overvalued asset prices, could trigger a very deep correction, potentially even a bear market.

AI boom, the ticking time bomb - the hidden left-tailDespite the macro backdrop and elevated geopolitical risk premia (driven primarily by Iran and Taiwan), risk appetite remains at record highs. The volume of leveraged positioning is at an all-time high, and market momentum has become heavily concentrated in the technology sector (currently around 40%). Meanwhile, stagflationary pressures have intensified, resulting that consumers are paying higher prices for less value. In the short run, the government is trying to mask this in order to preserve optimistic sentiment by manipulating the economic data and reducing the frequency of their publication. However, this effect is only temporary, while it further increases inflation risk via the wealth effect.

As I wrote, the S&P 500’s annual return in U.S. dollar terms was about 18% in 2025, driven mainly by strong earnings growth (around 12% EPS growth in 2025) and an expansion in valuation multiples of roughly 5%, complemented by about 1% dividend yield. The Mag 7 tech giants increased profits by an average of 22% in 2025, but the other 493 SPX constituents also delivered roughly 9% earnings growth, so in aggregate index profits expanded by around 12%—a broad-based improvement. Companies retained most of the incremental revenues as profit, while wage growth was more muted. This is reflected in a meaningful rise in profit margins alongside lagging wage growth. This will generate serious political controversies in the future. While listed, liquid equities and gold repriced quickly to financial easing, valuations in illiquid investments (such as venture capital, private equity, and real estate) have remained sticky. The liquidity premium—i.e., the higher expected return demanded for holding illiquid assets relative to liquid assets—is currently exceptionally low, which is not sustainable over the long term. As the debt used to finance these illiquid investments has to be refinanced at progressively higher interest rates, and as more investors are forced to raise liquidity (for example, by executing sales), the liquidity premium is likely to rise sharply—leading to a relative decline in illiquid asset values versus liquid assets.

The market’s heavy concentration in technology is problematic because companies are overspending and overbuilding AI infrastructure. The same pattern played out in the 1920s with radio and aviation, and during the dot-com bubble with the infrastructure built around the internet. In both cases, the core issue was excess capacity and excessive credit creation. Just because an innovation is useful does not mean it cannot be overvalued: demand grows slowly, and it does not grow without limit. In addition, supply chains have always had to adapt to natural constraints, social and geographic realities, and broader economic disruptions. Over the long run, only those technologies survive that solve more problems than they create.That is why flying cars, urban air taxis, supersonic passenger travel, pneumatic-tube mail systems, the AT&T Picturephone, Google Glass, nuclear-powered cars, 3D televisions, Wankel engines, or the Dvorak keyboard layout—and countless other inventions—never achieved broad adoption. And the same fate will await self-driving cars, human memory-chip implants, humanoid robots, and general-purpose AIs as well, along with who knows what else. AI is essentially a data-organizing, scoring, and pattern-recognition system that, with the help of LLMs, can present its outputs as polished, well-formed prose. It is not intended to replace or upgrade human intelligence, but to serve it. People tend not to like the current trend toward overly humanoid characteristics over the long run; it is off-putting—see the uncanny valley effect. The other popular developments currently in vogue also raise serious concerns among cybersecurity professionals, including psychological harms, privacy and personal-rights/data-protection issues, and environmental impacts. As a result, this field will fall under much stricter regulation going forward, and most of today’s flashy, overhyped AI developments will ultimately be thrown into the trash can.AI will matter primarily in domain-specific, optimized forms—especially for military R&D, data organization, profiling, and healthcare—and, for the average person, it will help build a more interactive internet (conception of Web4). Everything else is largely hype, used by companies as marketing to attract even more lenders and investors and to keep GDP growth pumping.

The tech sector can also be viewed as the Trump administration’s highly leveraged options bet. Consider the rollback of regulations, the easing of production constraints, the raising of protective tariffs, and targeted government support—all aimed at ensuring that capital can flow abundantly into the economy.At present, company valuations exceed annual revenues by many tens of times, and their investment outlays are unrealistic relative to any rationally expected return. Over the long run, most of these build-outs will prove unnecessary or will become obsolete, so a large share of the investment will not pay back. The problem is that this spending is being financed with investor capital and with debt. The shortfall becomes liabilities that revenues won’t cover.Three factors are masking the overcapacity. First, transactions by speculators operating in a state of euphoria and FOMO, which do not generate genuine long-term payoffs and merely push equity prices higher in the short run. Second, much of the demand is coming from the infrastructure build-out itself—orders from companies building the supply chain—rather than from end-user demand. Third, the companies’ own accounting, which is more sophisticated, because it is used to improve investor and lender sentiment. A few former colleagues drew my attention to these points, and some recent Wall Street Journal article covered them as well under “accounting black box” framing…On the one hand, if a company pauses a half-finished data center for a period of time, it can remain on the balance sheet as construction-in-progress (CIP), meaning it does not generate depreciation expense even though its economic value may be deteriorating. On the other hand, hyperscalers extend assumed useful lives for depreciation, which reduces annual depreciation expense and mechanically lifts reported profit. In doing so, the company can present overstated asset values on the balance sheet and inflated earnings on the income statement. In reality, the economic life cycle of AI servers is continually shortening (they are less energy-efficient and underperform newer generations), so their market value is, in fact, declining. The same applies to data centers built around AI hardware that is optimized for specific chip generations: they lose economic relevance and devalue as those generations change. Yet by raising residual (salvage) values and lengthening depreciation periods, tech giants can book billions of dollars of additional reported profit—profit that does not truly exist. Those accumulated value impairments will later have to be recognized through a large, one-off write-down.A third trick involves stock-based compensation. Warren Buffett drew attention to this relationship in 2018, and a Morgan Stanley analysis had already warned again about this issue back in May. This is a well-known financial process: SBC causes dilution, which large companies often offset on paper via buybacks. But buybacks are an economic cost, and they do not automatically increase per-share value; if repurchases occur at elevated prices, it is akin to making a poor investment decision, which reduces the company’s overall value—so SBC is an economic cost. However, because SBC is not an immediate cash outflow, under the indirect method it is typically added back as a non-cash item when reconciling net income to cash flow from operations (CFO/OCF), making the cost less visible. When borrowing cost declines, these buybacks also will be funded by debt.Using these methods, tech giants can attract additional investors and lenders who likewise do not want to miss “the deal of the century.” Do they know that much of the development is loss-making? I believe they do. They simply do not care. Most will receive a bailout anyway, as in 2008/09; otherwise the economy would stall.They know exactly that much of the development is fake, useless, or unnecessary—and that, in the future, it will be constrained by regulation due to climate risk, energy efficiency and other social issues. The objective is less about viability and more about keeping GDP growth pumping and accumulating profit.Europe has moved earlier in this regard, because it is already trying to align technological development with future environmental and social constraints through strict regulation—accepting that it may miss out on the profitability of the AI bubble, hedging the crisis risk.

The regulatory backdrop for the green sector became more polarized in 2025. The U.S. and European political establishments are now moving on increasingly divergent paths: while the EU’s competitiveness agenda (the Omnibus proposal) aims to streamline sustainability reporting obligations, the United States is seeing a strengthening anti-ESG lobby (lawsuits and rule changes). The risk is that green projects mostly depend on policy support mechanisms, and political reversals or the withdrawal of subsidies can represent material downside risk. At the end of 2025, for example, the EU introduced new budgetary and investment packages to support the Energy Union and a greener economy (TAG 4.0), but their continuity is not fully assured. In addition, transparency and verified environmental impact are still not consistently clear in every case. Meanwhile the market has rallied by roughly 100% over the past seven years.I believe the next major bubble will form here once the AI boom has run its course and the U.S. also pivots into the space as an investor. This will eventually happen because everyone understands that the Earth’s resources are finite. For now, market participants simply ride the prevailing trends for as long as possible before rotating into the next opportunity. The overbuild in AI, in turn, lays the groundwork for the green narrative next.

As I mentioned earlier, when a central bank chair says something along the lines of what Powell said in October—“this time is different,” on the grounds that these companies are genuinely profitable, unlike most participants in the dot-com bubble—you can assume the market top is close. But no one knows exactly when it will arrive. What we do know is that investors start cutting risk and scaling back positions before capital spending begins to slow and corporate outlays reach their peak. That process has already started.Powell’s line is a lie because, as I wrote, the dot-com bubble did not burst because of bianco companies and worthless startups, but precisely because of the overbuild of infrastructure and the fact that most of that investment ultimately failed to earn an economic return. This lie can, however, distract from the tech giants’ excessive leverage and overdevelopments in the short run, keeping optimistic market sentiment and faith in growth alive.

Problems around financing the AI-bubble (TSMC and NVDIA)One of the root causes of the 2008 crisis was that the state not only empowered but also incentivized the government-sponsored enterprises to accumulate mortgages broadly in the name of growth. Chief executives were given aggressive earnings growth targets and were heavily incentivized through stock-based compensation tied to short-term results. After the crisis, the state assumed control through the Federal Housing Finance Agency by placing the enterprises into conservatorship. This created the conditions for the net worth sweep mechanism, under which the enterprises were required to transfer essentially all quarterly profits to the United States Treasury, while retaining only a small capital buffer.That sweep was later modified by Trump, allowing the enterprises to retain earnings and rebuild capital, laying the groundwork for recapitalization and exit from conservatorship. However, the enterprises still face a meaningful capital shortfall. As a result, they cannot materially expand their mortgage exposure, launch new product structures at scale, or reduce guarantee fees in a meaningful way. For that reason, the government is seeking to accelerate the recapitalization process through an IPO. Such a transaction could take place in the first half of the year, potentially at a valuation above book value. Market participants remain more cautious, but the government has a clear policy motive to pursue it.

GSEs therefore function as a quasi-sovereign credit channel. They can fund themselves at exceptionally low cost, and when affordability becomes an explicit policy objective, their balance sheets can be used as part of the policy toolkit in a manner that resembles a synthetic form of QE. The policy goal is to offset the reduction in central bank holdings of MBSs by directing official-sector demand back into that market. The new Fed Chairman preferred by Trump is expected to cut rates during the second half of the year. Lower policy rates would support more plentiful and cheaper credit, particularly in government-supported mortgage channels, which can provide fuel for an asset price boom. If the FHFA directs the enterprises to purchase MBSs, policy-driven demand can compress mortgage spreads. As spreads compress, mortgage-backed security yields decline and mortgage rates fall. This frees cash flow. A portion of that freed liquidity may not go into housing-related spending, but rather into equities.This configuration may be supportive in the near term, but it materially increases downside tail risk as well as inflation risk. Inflation risk matters because the supply-side response in housing is slow, while fiscal policy remains expansionary through persistent deficits. Under those conditions, the policy channel can become self-limiting. In parallel, as the AI infrastructure is overbuilt, and due to future regulation, real-world end-user demand uncertainty, accelerating technological obsolescence, warranty and reliability constraints, and environmental and energy limitations, a meaningful portion of capital spending will fail to generate adequate returns, and debt repayment risk will rise sharply.

The quarterly results of NVIDIA and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company are critical in this context. NVIDIA represents the most concentrated exposure to the artificial intelligence boom. It monetizes a large share of marginal infrastructure spending, and the market prices much of the narrative through the company’s performance. For NVIDIA, delays in data center grid connection and commissioning are most likely to show up first as delivery timing and cash collection timing issues, and later as pricing pressure and inventory risk.TSM sits upstream in the value chain and therefore serves as an early indicator of real order flow. It can reveal how quickly its customers (chip designers) and their customers (cloud providers and data center operators) are actually absorbing production in the real world.There are two major risk factors that can threaten the financing scheme in a stealth, invisible way: 1) delays in electrical grid connection for data centers; and 2) the possibility that advanced packaging capacity and high-bandwidth memory supply expand faster than end-market growth expectations justify.

When grid connection is delayed, demand does not vanish overnight, instead, it is rescheduled, and the investment cadence shifts. In such cases, customers may choose to delay acceptance, or spread deliveries across multiple quarters, creating pricing and inventory risk. In this environment, it becomes critical whether management continues to emphasize “supply constraints” or shifts toward language that stresses “customer scheduling,” “installation cadence,” or “deployment timing.”The delivery of full artificial intelligence accelerator systems is often constrained not only by the compute chip itself but also by advanced packaging and high-bandwidth memory. While these remain binding constraints, scarcity supports strong pricing power and customers tolerate unfavorable terms. However, if packaging and memory capacity expands faster than end-market plans, the market can flip from shortage to surplus. When shortage conditions persist, management emphasizes constraints. When constraints ease, communication shifts toward a more “balanced” market. Capacity is not only a question of installed equipment, but also of continuous utilization. If demand does not keep pace with expansion, utilization declines. In that scenario, companies often become more cautious about the next wave of capacity additions, because they seek to avoid idle assets. As scarcity fades, customers’ bargaining power improves, and this can show up indirectly in profitability commentary.For NVIDIA, scarcity has supported premium pricing and exceptionally strong gross margins. If bottlenecks ease while customers’ forward plans do not grow at the same pace, customers become less fearful of supply shortages and more willing to demand concessions. Over time, that pressure becomes visible in gross margin. Inventory can accumulate at some point in the chain, which then pushes prices lower and slows the ordering cadence. Communication often shifts from “supply constraint” language to “demand normalization.” Bubbles frequently turn when the narrative is no longer about scarcity but about the sustainability of demand.

It is important to emphasize that as new hardware generations improve rapidly in energy efficiency and performance, the relative competitiveness of previously purchased equipment can deteriorate quickly. When economic usefulness collapses faster than accounting schedules imply, this ultimately forces write-downs and impairment recognition. In this current case, at once.

The bubble-burst does not necessarily begin with a sudden cessation of financing or an abrupt collapse in demand (absent a major exogenous shock such as a war or natural disaster). More commonly, the sequence starts with physical deployment delays, which push cash generation further out in time and reduce realized returns. Investment decision makers then become more cautious. As supply chain tightness eases, pricing power declines. Inventory and cash collection begin to deteriorate. Finally, that deterioration feeds back into the next ordering cycle.For TSMC the earliest signals tend to appear in scheduling and the pace of investment planning. For NVIDIA, the earliest signals tend to appear in pricing, gross margin, inventory behavior, and cash collection dynamics. In that way, projects fail to generate the expected cash flows, returns on investment deteriorate, and debt servicing becomes more difficult, particularly where funding relies on short maturities while the underlying projects are long-duration and risky. Even if a project could retain long-term economic value, it may still require repeated refinancing in the short term, which introduces a critical rollover risk.Domino-effect…

CrisisThe overbuild of AI infrastructure therefore accumulates a substantial amount of debt, which will almost inevitably trigger a credit crisis when the bubble bursts. In response to that crisis, monetary and fiscal policy will react with stimulus and further easing, which will generate additional inflation—until the system is forced to respond more aggressively to stop the process. In doing so, the entire economic system as we know it is put at risk.The risk is that if the economy stagnates for an extended period while inflation keeps rising (and I am not referring to “on-paper” inflation, but to the inflation people actually experience), then markets could move sideways—similar to the 1970s—while inflation steadily erodes real wealth.Moreover, if current economic trends continue, the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) projects that U.S. federal debt will rise to $50.7 trillion by 2034, up from roughly $35 trillion today. In 2023, the debt-to-GDP ratio had already climbed above 120%, and the fiscal deficit is once again running at roughly 6–7% of GDP per year—an unsustainable trajectory. As a result, in the 2024 U.S. federal budget, the government’s interest burden on the national debt exceeds defense spending—meaning more is spent on interest than on the U.S. military. This can lead to a crowding-out effect: the government is forced to devote an ever-larger share of tax revenues to debt service, less remains for productive spending, and elevated borrowing needs push market interest rates higher as well.The Trump administration is effectively choosing inflation and aiming to deal with the debt burden that way. The plan is to allow inflation to remain persistently a bit higher than nominal GDP growth, so that the debt-to-GDP ratio declines—alongside low central-bank policy rates. The Fed’s room for manoeuvre will be sharply constrained: if markets fall, it will need to cut rates (to prevent a deflationary spiral), but doing so reignites inflation; if it keeps rates high to fight inflation, debt dynamics can spiral, ultimately forcing monetary financing. Either path ends in inflation, which erodes prosperity.For these reasons, the outlook for bond investments—especially long-duration securities—is unfavorable, and further steepening of the yield curve appears likely.I like tail puts on TLT to hedge reflation.

Will reflation happen for sure? Over the past 20–30 years, globalization—cheaper labor, an optimized global supplier network, economies of scale, and expanding trade—acted as a deflationary, price‑suppressing force in manufacturing and in the electronics supply chain. If they now step back from that model (through higher tariffs, export controls, relocating production to friendly jurisdictions, building redundant capacity, and adding a geopolitical risk premium), the outcome will be a negative supply shock: higher costs and lower potential output.That matters because the financing tricks discussed earlier—lower policy rates, lower mortgage rates, higher asset prices, stronger sentiment, and more buybacks—implicitly assumes a world in which cost inflation is manageable, long‑term bond yields do not run away, and real‑economy supply can adjust fast enough.If, however, deglobalization and geopolitical bloc formation keep the cost level structurally higher, then policy easing will not be enough: inflation risk returns, and eventually long‑term yields, risk premia, or the exchange‑rate channel can blunt the impact of that easing.The core problem, therefore, is that the real‑economy cost base is deteriorating while the growth narrative is often still being priced as if the old margin trajectory remains intact.In the short run, this transition can bring subsidies, an investment wave, greater order visibility, and even pricing power for certain players. The core problem, therefore, is that the real‑economy cost base is deteriorating while the growth narrative is often still being priced as if the old margin trajectory remains intact.In the short run, this transition can bring subsidies, an investment wave, greater order visibility, and even pricing power for certain players. But in the long run, the system as a whole pays for it through higher unit costs, higher energy and materials costs, greater capital intensity, and ultimately weaker returns on investment.So the answer is: yes.

In addition, wealth inequality has increased significantly, creating latent tensions over the long term that policymakers will be forced to address. The “affluent”—primarily the top 10% wealth cohort that holds equities and other capital ownership—have benefited the most: they captured the equity-market rally and recorded stronger income gains, while the bottom 60%—with less savings and greater exposure to price increases—experienced the impact of inflation as severe. This can translate into voter dissatisfaction and could even lead to Republicans losing their majority in the House of Representatives, along with a loss of institutional influence.Furthermore, in the labor market the share of the working-age population is declining, which directly constrains labor supply and output growth. If governments do nothing, demographic drag could reduce global GDP growth by roughly 1.1 percentage points per year over 2025–2050 versus the pre-pandemic pace, with nearly three-quarters of that slowdown attributable to population aging. Based on historical relationships, each 1 percentage point increase in the share of the population aged 65+ is associated with roughly a 0.92 percentage point decline in annual equity-market returns over a 10-year horizon.A slowing or shrinking population reduces aggregate-demand growth, because fewer new households are formed and fewer young buyers purchase housing or durable goods. As the number of working-age people declines, labor shortages can emerge in certain sectors, creating upward pressure on wages. As the baby-boom generation retires, the tightening labor supply can produce stronger wage-inflation pressure in the economy, providing further stagflationary pressures: low growth alongside inflation driven by higher labor costs. In the United States, tighter immigration policy adds to this pressure, because the working-age share has been materially supported by younger immigrants.Social Security and Medicare outlays are rising rapidly as the baby-boom generation moves into retirement. According to projections cited by the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), Social Security trust fund reserves could be depleted in the 2030s unless revenues are increased or payments are reduced—an approach that is politically difficult. The combined OASI and DI reserves are projected to be sufficient to pay full scheduled benefits until 2034. Rising age-related spending is paired with rising sovereign debt in many advanced economies. This dynamic also pushes governments toward the strategy of inflating away the real value of debt.However, the most important aspect of aging is the reallocation of demand. Older households save more and do not have the same needs as a young parent or a single person buying a home, a car, new clothing, and other more discretionary consumer goods. As a result, overproduction can develop in certain sectors; this can be temporarily “offset” by supply-chain-driven demand, but that is not genuine end consumption—surplus remains surplus. Moreover, in today’s consumer society, young people’s spending tends to gravitate toward market-leading products with strong popular branding and hype, which can pull money out of domestic economies and, over time, push many firms toward bankruptcy. This slows the economic performance and self-sufficiency of many countries, with knock-on effects for larger economies’ (for example, the United States’) export and import flows as well.

If we add to this the geopolitical realignment and the fact that the Eastern bloc is challenging the unipolar world order, it becomes clear that we are heading into a serious crisis—one for which I expect a “dress rehearsal” already this year. Perhaps after the spring to June period.I do not know when the market peak will come, and there will not be a single, specific signal. But I do know that even if Trump does everything he can to win the midterm elections, that event in itself does not guarantee that the market will not reach a bull-market peak—just as happened in 2000, 2008, 2018, and 2022. An election year, or a midterm year, does not automatically imply a rally, and the clouds are thickening.In any case, the United States must confront challenges to its long-term strategic buffers so that, after the crisis, it can continue to secure prosperity for its population while preserving its economic hegemonic position. The western world and the Gulf coutries will support these efforts.

A bear market will be necessary because, as I wrote, the disinflationary impact of oil and rare metals/mineral resources materializes only over the long term and does not fully offset today’s stagflationary pressures—it merely moderates them.A market-wide liquidity drain, however, reduces inflation both “on paper” and in practice, at a time when policy rates are already low. The capital that flows out will then move—via precious metals—into a restructured, tokenized, blockchain-based marketplace whose center is being built in the Middle East, and through which global trade can be monitored and controlled more tightly than it is today. I have written about this previously.

World economic renewal? I believe that geopolitical realignments, overpopulation and population aging, widening social disparities, climate change, the extraction of value from nation-state economies by global capitalist networks, the technology boom, and post-crisis disillusionment will destabilize Western market economies more than the 2008/09 crisis did. At times I think this could even mark the end of the economic system we have known so far, because it is not capable of addressing all of these problems simultaneously. Instead, it may gradually push the world toward the slow integration of “third-way” economic models—such as distributism—which can accommodate national economic priorities, higher-quality production, reduced extremes of social inequality, a fairer distribution of goods, the overproduction trap, and the destabilizing effects of large-capital speculation, as well as the alternative risk of state monopoly over goods (like in the East), and so on.Distributism first emerged in the social encyclicals of Pope Leo XIII and Pope Pius XI, and it is compatible with both religious-nationalist and democratic worldviews. I think we are standing in the antechamber of that kind of renewal. The key, however—as I said—is a slow transition.

The defining tone of the year will be set in the spring by the SCOTUS case, when it takes up the legality of the protective tariffs. This will be a very important event. Public dissatisfaction with the government is accumulating, while a new opposition alliance is forming on the left that openly places large concentrations of wealth and social inequality at the center of its platform—which is, in fact, a clearly observable problem for the mass.

I believe we are heading into a larger pullback, during which large institutional investors reload and rebuild their positions, and then we get a blow-off top, well above 7,000. In a previous post, I wrote that I expect the market to remain supported throughout Trump’s presidency. However, given the increasingly convex pace of events, I think a crisis could arrive even before 2028, as the risk of financing the bubble will overgrow too fast. I am not trying to time it; I am simply managing the risk, and watching 10-years and 30-years MAs for targets, and the rate of change in yearly returns, P/E and divident yields for momentum.

The period ahead therefore brings a number of fundamental structural and global challenges for which there are not many good answers. These risks continue to accumulate, and the market is strongly underpricing them…
