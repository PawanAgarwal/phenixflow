Fed is in trouble
Tariffs are expected to weaken hiring and spending, pushing the economy closer to recession. However, these same tariffs are likely to raise prices on many imported goods and materials, which feeds into overall inflation and gives central bankers pause about loosening policy too early.
Because of these conflicting pressures, rate cuts (which could help with growth) might worsen inflation, while maintaining higher rates to curb inflation could deepen a slowdown in employment and spending.
Economists, business owners, and investors are already betting that the combined effects of tariffs will push the economy toward recession. Many are expecting that when the economy begins to slow sharply, the Fed will eventually need to cut rates. However, there’s considerable uncertainty—investors are not convinced that the Fed can swing the policy needle quickly or decisively enough to avoid negative fallout.
If business sentiment deteriorates due to the tariff shock, and if the Fed is seen as too slow to counteract it, the credibility of U.S. monetary policy could be undermined.

Powell’s standpoint is to fully understand the economic impact before making a major policy change. He’s waiting to see a significant weakening in the labor market or a clear signal from inflation data before taking action. But delaying a rate cut too long might allow the slowdown to deepen, while an early move could ignite persistent inflation.

From this, you can see exactly what Xi is playing at.
He knows there are still those who would bail out and finance the U.S. economy. He also knows that if China and the EU (plus the BRICS as a whole) buckle, Trump will roll back most of the tariffs. This is crucial.
But Xi is trying to drag this out until the loss of confidence in the U.S. is too great and the U.S. backs down from the economic war.
