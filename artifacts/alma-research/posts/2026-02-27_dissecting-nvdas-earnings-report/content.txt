Dissecting Nvidia’s ER

TSMC is only willing to build out the required manufacturing capacity for this if Nvidia locks it in years in advance with non-cancelable commitments and cash, therefore Nvidia must blindly commit many billions to its suppliers even before knowing the actual future demand. The company’s total supply chain exposure (future payable obligations + inventory already on hand) has grown to the point where it has reached the operating cash flow generated by the company over a full year.

Additionally, receivable days stood at only 43.20 days in Q1 2025, but have climbed to 51.38 days by Q4 2026 (and even reached 54 days in Q2). This clearly shows that customers are now paying, on average, more than a week slower than before. For now, it’s not much, but if the trend continues to go this way, this will become a problem.

By the end of Q4 2026, accounts receivable had reached approximately 56.5% of the current quarter’s revenue (compared to ~47.4% in Q1 2025). This indicates that, with growing sales, the company is required to extend an ever-larger amount of capital to its customers.

And the ‘allowance for doubtful accounts’ balance is $4 million against the $38,466 million in outstanding receivables, which is a growing hidden complacency, if you consider what I wrote above. Especially, if we add that the sales return allowance - i.e., sales return allowance doubled from last year’s $82 million to $170 million this year.

Below in the 10-K document they write that „...these commitments were $95.2 billion, of which substantially all will be paid through fiscal year 2027.” However, Nvidia’s financial calendar is offset from the calendar year. Thus the 2027 fiscal year has just begun. Substantially all is expected to be paid during the next 11-12 months to supply‑chain vendors.
