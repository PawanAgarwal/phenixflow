I used to feel the same way about AI until very recently. I had lunch with a current Chief Scientist at Meta AI, and the conversation completely shifted my perspective.
According to him, the AI tools available to the public today represent only about 1% of the capabilities of the models they are currently developing. Internally at Meta, there is growing concern that these advancements will radically disrupt if not outright dismantle our current world as we know it.
He described how some companies are already reaching out to Meta and deploying AI software on employees’ computers to track every mouse click, task, and email. After just two weeks of monitoring, the AI can fully replicate that employee’s job. Not 50%, not 95%, 100% replacement.
Just two years ago, training a state-of-the-art AI model cost around $500 million. Today, it’s down to $500,000 and he expects it to drop below $100,000 by the end of the year.
His concerns were serious and deeply felt. In his view, virtually all office jobs, the majority of legal and medical professions, up to 90% of IT roles, etc will disappear within the next five years.
It was a fascinating and frankly unsettling discussion. I only wish I had understood more about this earlier. We are heading into profoundly transformative times...

2025-07-19T19:38:57.458Z | Geoff5891
What's interesting is to start to imagine what is going to happen if he is right. In the short term, shareholders are the one likely to see massive gains when companies cut costs and replace human labour with AI. The immediate effect will be rising profit margins, boosted stock prices, and acceleration of capital accumulation for investors.
But this shift will quickly become destructive. As more people lose their jobs and sources of income, their ability to consume the very goods and services now produced, mostly or entirely, by AI will diminish. Reduced employment leads to reduced consumption, which ultimately undermines the demand side of the economy. The growing gap between capital and labour will probably become even more unsustainable.
Now place this in the context of Western social welfare systems, which are built on the assumption that people will work and contribute to the tax base until the age of 65, 67, or even 70. Systems which are already under pressure from ageing populations... If a significant portion of the workforce becomes economically obsolete, tax revenues will shrink just as demand for public support increases. Systemic collapse in 3, 2, 1 :)
