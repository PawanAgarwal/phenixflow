Short-term vs long-term
Benjamin Graham stated that in the short term (on a weekly, monthly, or even quarterly basis), markets behave like a "voting machine"—driven by investor sentiment, psychology (like FOMO, herd-mentality, etc.), and short-term capital flows, making prices primarily a function of supply and demand dynamics. This can be manipulated by news and narratives designed to influence market sentiment and volatility bets.
This creates volatility cycles, where volatility becomes either over- or underpriced, attracting the herd into one side (momentum) before bagging those overcrowded positions on them (reversion). This is not a conspiracy nor a deliberate attack (though in some cases, it can be—such as when a company faces insider battles over higher ownership stakes through share accumulation). Rather, it is simply a reflexive effect in the markets, as a result of decreased liquidity due to one-sided positioning.
However, in the long run (longer than a year, even ten years), markets act as a "weighing machine", where prices eventually converge toward the intrinsic value of companies, determined by factors such as earnings, cash flows, and balance sheet strength.
In essence, speculation and emotion can dominate rational valuation in the short term, but intrinsic value tends to prevail over longer periods.
