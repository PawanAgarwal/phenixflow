2025-03-01T14:15:02.912Z | Chris
Think about it visually. Let’s look at a call delta curve. The slope of the delta curve decreases on both sides of the ATM price. That means the further you are away from ATM, the less sensitive to changes in spot these options become (which of course is just gamma).

In laymans terms: The price of the call is nothing else than the probability weighted (=expected) payoff from the option. So the further you are below the current ATM price, the lower the probability that your call option will have a payoff.

So far so good. Now let‘s factor in time and use a concrete example. Stock ABC closed at 100 yesterday. Would you place a bet that ABC is at 200 a year from now? And would you place the same bet for tmw? Taking it to the extremes you get the intuition why the time value of an option is higher, the further out it goes. Now fill the spots in between. Would you place a bet that ABC is at at 150 tmw? At 110? . At 102? Your willingness to take the bet and therefore its fair value certainly increases the closer the strike gets to spot. Visually that means that the curve gets steeper and steeper the closer to expiry you get. With a long time to expiry it‘s a nice and easy upward slope. One second before expiry it‘s vertical because it‘s either in the money or not.

So to put this together: with the passage of time, the slope get‘s steeper. Therefore your delta OTM will decrease until 0, ATM stays the same and ITM increases to 1. The wings move more and more to the center.

Now put delta is just call delta times -1. So basically flip the curve below the horizontal axis. Below spot (ITM) delta will decrease until -1 with every day passing, ATM again stays the same, above spot will increase until 0 with every day passing. Therefore you are correct. With the passage of time, below spot delta will decrease in both scenarios and above spot it will increase. Or in one sentence: charm increases with the strike price
