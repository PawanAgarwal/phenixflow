Based on my search, here's what it would take for Congress to override presidential tariffs:

For Congress to override presidential tariffs, they would need to pass specific legislation through both chambers that would either require a joint congressional resolution which would need to pass both chambers of Congress (currently Republican-controlled) and either get the president's signature or override his veto with a two-thirds majority in both chambers.

Congress could pass new laws like the recently introduced "Stopping Tariffs on Allies and Bolstering Legislative Exercise of (STABLE) Trade Policy Act" which would require congressional approval before a president could impose new tariffs on U.S. allies and free trade agreement partners.

The challenge is significant because over decades, Congress has gradually delegated extensive tariff authority to the executive branch. The president currently has multiple legal pathways to impose tariffs including Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 (previously used for China tariffs), Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 (used for steel and aluminum tariffs), the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), and Section 122 Balance of Payments Authority.

In the current political environment, it would be extremely difficult to pass such legislation given the need for bipartisan cooperation and potentially overcoming a presidential veto.

Based on my search, I can explain whether the 50% figure derived from the exports-to-imports ratio would have real effects on trade or be largely cosmetic:

### The Real vs. Cosmetic Impact

The actual tariff situation between the US and South Korea is very different from what might be suggested by the 50% exports-to-imports ratio. South Korea's Industry Ministry has clarified that "under the Korea-US Free Trade Agreement, both Korea and the US have eliminated tariffs on most goods. As of 2024, Korea's effective tariff rate for goods imported from the US is approximately 0.79 percent."

South Korea's finance minister (acting as president) has stated that "the impact of the reciprocal tariff measures may not be huge on our economy, as tariff rates are low due to the Korea-U.S. Free Trade Agreement." However, he noted that monitoring is necessary since the US indicated it would also assess non-tariff barriers.

The newly announced reciprocal tariffs include a 10% minimum tariff on most goods imported to the United States, with higher duties for certain countries. Trading partners are expected to respond with countermeasures, potentially leading to higher prices for many goods.

### What This Means in Practice

The 50% figure derived from dividing exports by imports appears to be more of a rhetorical or political calculation rather than an actual tariff rate that would be applied in practice. Under the KORUS FTA, which entered into force in 2012, Korea and the US have already eliminated duties on nearly 80% of bilateral trade in industrial and consumer goods, with most other duties phased out over 10 years and eliminated as of January 1, 2021.

However, despite the low effective tariff rates under the FTA, South Korea is taking the threat seriously. South Korea's acting President has already ordered emergency support measures for industries and businesses that will be impacted by the US's sweeping tariffs and urged the ministry to closely analyze the details and actively negotiate with Washington to minimize the impact.

South Korea has been actively working to secure exemptions from these new tariffs. The South Korean trade minister has already met with the US Trade Representative and asked for the country to be exempted from reciprocal tariffs.

The real economic impact would depend on whether the US actually imposes new tariffs beyond what's already established in the KORUS FTA, particularly focusing on non-tariff barriers. The 50% figure itself appears to be more of a talking point than a policy that would be directly implemented as a tariff rate.
