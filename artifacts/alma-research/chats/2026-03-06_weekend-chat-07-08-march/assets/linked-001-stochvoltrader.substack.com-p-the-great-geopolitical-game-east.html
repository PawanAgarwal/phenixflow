Foreword

The following analysis does not serve as an ideological or moral compass, nor does it necessarily reflect my own personal judgment. Its purpose is to present the viewpoints of the respective parties.
Note that it is ultimately irrelevant what you or I personally think about the mindset of one side or the other; it does not constitute a decisive factor in the dynamics of the situation.

Where should I even begin…
Mike Tyson, the legendary world boxing champion, once said in an interview: “Everybody thinks this is a tough man’s sport. This is not a tough man’s sport. This is a thinking man’s sport. A tough man is gonna get hurt real bad in this sport.” (Although I have only boxed recreationally since childhood, I have experienced this myself too…)
The “tough,” aggressive opponent may seem fanatical and intimidating, which either paralyzes the other party or it doesn’t. Psychologically, the aggressive, tough opponent wants to make the other believe that they can destroy them, so that the latter—if they buy into it—surrenders. However, the smart fighter who sees through this psychological trick and fights the intimidating opponent dispassionately, with a cool head and tactical mindset, will emerge victorious. Consider the legendary Ali vs. Foreman match...

The exact same dynamic applies between East and West.
The East is the tough, aggressive opponent that builds Tsar Bombas, fanaticizes its army along ideological lines, trains them barbarically (where only the tough and viable can survive), while its autocratic leaders communicate aggressively and hold terrifying demonstrations of power.
The West, on the other hand, is the party that builds precision weapons, develops its intelligence capabilities, instills patriotism in its military, and strives to build a global republic of souvereign countries.
The East knows exactly that militarily it stands no chance against the Western alliance system. More on this later. That is precisely why it seeks to conquer the Western economy by employing the tactic of making itself an indispensable economic player.

The Problem: The East Got Too Close

Thanks to the fine global capitalist magnates who cared only about profit, the East got too close to the West.
This means that, out of a sheer desire for economic profit, they literally opened the gates themselves and sold the keys to their own critical infrastructure and security. China did not enter Europe with tanks, but with state-subsidized mega-corporations and the credit lines of the "Silk Road" (Belt and Road) project. In the name of the free market, capitalists allowed the Chinese Communist Party to lay its hands on the continent's vital arteries.
Let me mention a few recent historical examples, aside from the relocation of important factories and plants to China:

  • After the 2008 economic crisis, the EU forced Greece into privatization. At that time, the Chinese state-owned enterprise COSCO acquired a 67% majority stake in the Port of Piraeus (Europe’s most important southern gateway). Later, Chinese companies also acquired stakes in Belgian (Zeebrugge), Spanish (Valencia), and Dutch (Rotterdam) ports. The most shocking case was Hamburg, where in 2022/23, the German Chancellor pushed through a deal—despite protests from his own intelligence agency (BND) and the US—allowing COSCO to acquire a stake in one of the Port of Hamburg’s terminals.

  • Until the late 2010s, the majority of EU countries would have thoughtlessly entrusted their future 5G mobile networks to China’s Huawei and ZTE, simply because they were cheaper than the Western competition. It took years of diplomatic blackmail by US intelligence (CIA, NSA) to make Europe understand that if Beijing controls the backbone of European data traffic, the West becomes significantly vulnerable to China. (This is why the rumor that 5G causes cancer was spread—to ensure the technology faced public rejection).

  • In 2016, the Chinese Midea Group easily bought out KUKA, one of the world’s most advanced, strategically important German industrial robot manufacturers. Accumulated European cutting-edge technology and know-how migrated completely legally to China. The German government did not veto the deal.

  • Although the Russians annexed the Crimean Peninsula by military force in 2014, Germany and the EU, instead of decoupling from them, signed off on the construction of the Nord Stream 2 gas pipeline in 2015, completely ignoring the desperate warnings of Poland and the US. By 2021, 40% of the EU’s gas supply (and more than half of Germany’s) depended on Moscow. Before the war, Germany and Austria allowed their largest strategic gas storage facilities (such as Rehden in Germany) to fall under Gazprom’s ownership. In the winter of 2021, prior to the invasion of Ukraine, the Russians deliberately left these storage facilities nearly empty, meaning Europe entered the energy crisis completely defenseless.

  • Additionally, in the early 2020s, Russian mercenaries (the Wagner Group) pushed French and EU soldiers out of the Sahel region (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger). In doing so, they seized the Nigerien uranium mines that supply French nuclear power plants and gained direct control over the migration routes heading to Europe.

The root of the problem was not blindness, but ideological differences.
Western leaders genuinely believed that by binding China and Russia to themselves economically, these dictatorships would eventually transform into peaceful democracies, as war would no longer be profitable for them. Consequently, they were unconcerned by their own vulnerability, and through cheap Chinese labor and cheap Russian energy, they reaped massive profits, while the US provided them with protection and the East provided the energy.

The East's Strategy and Trump's Fight

Brinkmanship

Russia and China are building up their war capacities at full steam, but their primary goal is not direct military confrontation. Rather, it is the greatest economic and geopolitical blackmail and disruption in history, alongside inducing self-deterrence within the West. Western intelligence agencies call this reflexive control, and Sun Tzu calls it winning without fighting; it primarily targets the psychological endurance and sensitivity of a demographic they view as softened by Western democracy, while secondarily aiming to shake trust within the alliance. When Putin threatens the use of nuclear weapons, his goal is not to press the button, but to cognitively break Western societies. They want to create a situation where comfortable European and American voters, fearing for their lives, become so terrified of a world war that they force their own governments’ hands like “to hell with Taiwan and Eastern Europe, just leave us alone!” This is textbook intimidation.
(If we consider that certain liberals actually protested for Khamenei’s rights, then perhaps my point is clear…)

Xi has also ordered his military to be ready for an invasion of Taiwan by 2027.
However, according to their own analysis, China’s true hope is that through a horrific naval buildup, blockades, and economic blackmail (rare-earth metal embargoes), they can coerce Taiwan into voluntary surrender and the US into backing down—without firing a single shot.
Furthermore, Chinese military cyber units have been embedded in American and European water infrastructure, power grids, and ports for years. The CIA director stated it plainly: the Chinese are not merely spying; they are planting “sleeper agents / logistics bombs” to be used for blackmail during a potential Taiwan offensive and to potentially spark civil unrest (which is already a sensitive vulnerability due to Western social inequalities, and particularly risky during a crisis and a potential future First Island Chain blockade).
Meanwhile, the Russian GRU is developing weapons explicitly designed to remotely and physically paralyze Western railway systems, airports, and energy grids. Simultaneously, they conduct arsons, bombings, and drone tests (airspace violations) at arms factories, warehouses, and train stations to test NATO and intimidate the public, all while keeping the diplomatic weight of these actions sub-threshold so as not to trigger NATO’s Article 5.
(NATO is, of course, prepared for all this; they just don’t broadcast it. Information is currency, never forget this.)

This is exactly why the East’s primary weapon is, paradoxically, the promise of peace.
A willingness to engage in peace talks signals psychological weakness, and thus, following Gromyko’s strategy, they ask, demand, and extort more and more, while maintaining economic and psychological pressure on society. Consequently, Western parties agree to deals that are strategically disadvantageous to them in the long run. Just consider Putin’s demand to restore NATO’s 1997 borders—which would require fourteen (now sixteen) countries to leave the NATO (which is not a modest request by any means…)—or the Ukrainian peace offer, in which the Tsar demanded the complete disarmament of the Ukrainian army. Simply because the Tsar regarded NATO expansion as a Western provocation (even tho he also wanted to be a prt of the NATO). However, the psychological refutation of this lies in post-World War II European history itself (I am referring here to the eastern and western sides of the Iron Curtain, which clearly illustrate the differences between the Eastern and Western worlds).
Russia does not believe in conventional military force as much as it believes in deception and psychological manipulation. The core of this tactic is to create an artificial information environment and threat level (e.g., constant nuclear saber-rattling) that forces Western decision-makers to voluntarily back down of their own accord—driven purely by fear.
For this very reason, Eastern peace offers are actually demands for self-surrender. And if the other party rejects the offer, victim-blaming instantly appears in their communication, claiming the West is rejecting peace and wants to prolong the war. Anyone who has traveled beyond Eastern Europe knows exactly what I am talking about…

Trump’s Fight

Trump’s foreign policy actions must be examined within this context. It is a very dangerous poker game.
As I wrote last March, Trump’s plan is the so-called Reverse-Nixon maneuver mediated by the Arabs. In 1972, President Richard Nixon and Henry Kissinger went to Beijing, struck a pact with China to detach them from the Soviet Union, and thereby won the Cold War for the West. Today, the setup is reversed: China is the true, systemic superpower enemy, so the goal is to detach Russia from it. The UAE and Saudi Arabia play the role of backchannels. They are pragmatic actors who can reason with Trump, but at the same time, due to the oil market (OPEC+) and BRICS, they maintain excellent, daily contact with Putin as well. (This is what I refer to as ‘Holy Alliance 2.0’, what they laid down in 2017)
Behind the scenes, the Russian elite (the oligarchs and the FSB) are literally afraid of an overly strong China. Outwardly, they proclaim a “no-limits friendship,” but in practice, Russia has become China’s raw material colony in recent years. China sucks up Siberian gas and timber for pennies, has forced the yuan onto the Russian economy, and ruthlessly steals Russian military technology, all while keeping the Far East under demographic pressure (Chinese settlers are increasingly populating Siberia, and Vladivostok is already labeled by its old Chinese name, “Haishenwai”, on official Chinese maps).
Putin knows that if China wins the 21st century, Russia will be nothing more than an obedient “gas station” on Beijing’s map. He wants an empire, not the role of an Asian little brother. Therefore, the Russians desperately need an exit toward the West to regain their geopolitical balance.
America has something to offer Russia (e.g., technology to exploit the Shtokman field), and Russia also holds cards in its hand (e.g., sea routes instead of China’s). However, there is a minor problem with Russia that breeds mistrust in Trump…

Since the early 1990s, Russian foreign policy has used the term “Near Abroad” as an official political and legal designation for the 14 former Soviet republics.
The essence of this doctrine is that Russia considers these states its own privileged sphere of influence and recognizes only limited sovereignty for them in terms of security policy. In 2005, Putin called the collapse of the Soviet Union the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the 20th century. On December 17, 2021, two months before the outbreak of the war in Ukraine, the Russian Foreign Ministry submitted official security treaty drafts to the US and NATO. They demanded in writing that NATO roll back all its military infrastructure to its 1997 borders (i.e., evacuate the former Eastern Bloc, including Hungary, Poland, and the Baltics) and provide a legal guarantee that Ukraine and Georgia would never become members.
For this reason, in 2017, the first Trump administration explicitly defined Russia as a “revisionist power” seeking to undermine the international order in its published NSS. And in 2019, he pulled the US out of the Cold War-era INF Treaty. The official American justification was that intelligence proved Moscow had secretly violated the treaty for years by deploying SSC-8 missiles. The Trump administration simply did not trust Russian compliance with agreements.
In 2025, Trump pressured Zelensky into initiating a 30-day ceasefire, thereby testing the Russian side’s intentions. However Putin indicated that a simple 30-day truce was unacceptable to them because it would only give the Ukrainians a chance to rearm. He demands total capitulation: the removal of Zelensky’s government, the complete demilitarization of Ukraine, the renunciation of NATO membership, and the retention of captured territories. This revealed his true intentions.

Trump therefore sees and understands that Putin envisions peace in the manner of a new Yalta Conference.
Back in the day in Munich, Churchill warned that there were severe ideological divides between Hitler and other non-fascist European nations that would never allow for long-term alliances. Furthermore, he knew that European nations were sitting down to negotiate with Hitler and Mussolini out of fear (Hitler later spoke disparagingly of his European opponents). The situation is similar now due to the fundamental difference between the Russian and American visions. Russia has thought in terms of an empire throughout its entire history; this is embedded in their culture, literature, and politics. The United States, on the other hand, thinks in terms of a global republic of souvereign countries, which is well reflected by its 250-year history when contrasted with Eurasia.
Trump’s goal, therefore, is to negotiate with Putin, but strictly from a position of strength. To achieve this, Moscow must be pushed back from the West to the point where the Chinese alliance becomes painful for them, forcing them to give up their positions. Meanwhile, they are severing China’s strategic “tentacles” to weaken both its military and economic positions. This is the rationale behind the embargoes, tariffs, CHIPS acts, and military operations in places like Venezuela, Iran, Cuba, etc. It is also why he exerted pressure on Greenland (where the Chinese had placed numerous listening stations while their fleets freely operated in the sea; another prime example of European corruption), and why he created a new UN under the name of “Board of Peace”.
This is a dangerous but necessary poker game on Trump’s part to prevent the rise of a communist superpower. However, it is not impossible to win; in fact, the West’s odds are far more favorable.

Military Capabilities

Let’s compare the military capabilities of the West and the East only in light of known events…
By ship count, China’s navy is already larger than the US Navy, but it is in geopolitical captivity. To break out into the Pacific Ocean, they must pierce the wall formed by Japan, Taiwan, Vietnam, and the Philippines, which is defended by Australia (with its AUKUS nuclear submarines) and the US.
China relies heavily on energy imports; if the US and its allies blockade the maritime straits, the Chinese economy will suffocate. Even individually, these nations are sufficiently strong opponents for China.
(I should note here that Japan is one of the strongest, if not the strongest, military powers in the region. In the Battle of Tsushima in 1904-1905, the modern Japanese fleet, backed by British logistics, sent the globe-circling Russian fleet to the bottom of the sea in a single afternoon. Later, during WWII, they proved victorious over China. Since then, Japan, in cooperation with its Western allies, has modernized its military on a massive scale.)

India is not a member of the CSTO (the “Eastern NATO”). In fact, despite its BRICS membership, it is China’s greatest Asian geopolitical rival and a silent partner of the Quad alliance. Despite some diplomatic frictions, India will not fight against the West.

Russia would have to face the entirety of Europe, while their Middle Eastern allies would be up against the region’s great powers (e.g., Israel) and the “shadow-superpower” itself: the Arabs.

With the NATO accession of Sweden and Finland, the Baltic Sea has practically become a “NATO lake.” Russia’s northwestern fleet (St. Petersburg/Kaliningrad) is completely boxed in. Europe is capable of seizing Russian tankers that the Russians cannot defend.
Four years ago, Putin invaded a country directly bordering his own, accessible by land, yet the operation degenerated into a slow, WWI-style trench war. The elite North Korean troops (the “Storm Corps”) sent to Kursk starting in late 2024, along with Chinese mercenaries, have suffered dramatic 30-40% casualty rates on the modern battlefield. The dreaded Wagner mercenaries and Chechen fighters failed similarly.
The 60-kilometer Russian armored column heading toward Kyiv stalled and was destroyed because they ran out of fuel barely a few hundred kilometers from their own border. If Russian logistics collapsed on peaceful terrain right next door, it is physically impossible for them to supply an invasion force in Europe while NATO air forces systematically bomb bridges and railways behind their lines.

Perhaps few know this, but in 2018, a handful of American special forces faced a 500-strong armored column of Russian Wagner mercenaries and Syrians. American artillery and air power annihilated the Russian attackers in a matter of hours (100-200 dead). American casualties were zero.
Similarly, in 1991, the Iraqi army was the 4th largest in the world, packed with Russian and Chinese tanks and battle-hardened soldiers. The Western coalition bombed them into submission in just 100 hours using GPS-guided weapons, vividly demonstrating the decades-wide gap between Eastern and Western technology.

The greatest chasm lies in C4ISR (Command, Control, Communications, Computers, Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance). The US dominates network-centric warfare. Satellites, stealth aircraft (F-35s), and special operations forces share data in real time (the Kill Chain). Thanks to this, they were able to strike with surgical precision on the other side of the world within hours, even under Russian/Chinese air defense umbrellas in Venezuela, or decapitate Iran’s military leadership in a single day.
Let that sink in for a moment…

So, last year, US bombers were able to fly into Iranian airspace and execute precision strikes on their most heavily guarded military facilities without even being detected.
This year, on January 3, US forces crossed the sea, breached Venezuela—which is protected by Russian/Chinese air defense systems (S-300)—and extracted Nicolás Maduro from his own palace in a single night, within 2.5 hours, without triggering weeks-long frontlines. China and Russia watched helplessly.
Iran’s general staff and its proxies (Hezbollah, Hamas) were decapitated by the Israeli-American coalition in days; Russian and Chinese air defenses proved completely blind, and their allies didn’t lift a finger. Israel is a technological superpower, and the Sunni Arab states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Jordan) are armed to the teeth with American weapons and lean pragmatically toward the West.

In stark contrast, the Russians have been unable to catch President Zelensky for four years, even though he was completely unprepared for the invasion in 2022. It is equally true for both Iran and the Russians that during their offensives, they are capable of detecting and liquidating only very few military targets—especially strategically significant ones—unlike Western forces.

However, by attacking civilian targets and terrorizing the population, the East adheres to a Clausewitzian strategy aimed at breaking the civilian population’s combat morale.
Eastern dictatorships bet on the fact that they can sacrifice 1 million men without blinking, while the West will demand peace after 10,000 casualties, triggering protests and possibly civil unrest to topple the leadership. A similar dynamic occurred during the Korean War (1950-53), as well as in Vietnam and Afghanistan, where the US held crushing technological superiority and won every major engagement on the battlefield. But the domestic public, upon seeing the returning coffins, forced a withdrawal. This is due to the enfeeblement of liberalism and the ideology of patriotism, a phenomenon continuously monitored here in Europe as well. This is precisely why a right-wing, conservative shift is necessary now.
At the same time, the East would only stand a chance in a protracted war, given that according to the US Office of Naval Intelligence (ONI), China’s shipbuilding capacity is currently more than 200 times that of the United States. A shorter war would undeniably be won by the West. However, in a prolonged, multi-year war of attrition, the West’s prohibitively expensive precision missiles would run out, while the East (Russia, North Korea) churns out millions of “dumb” but highly destructive artillery shells and cheap drones.
(Consider WWII: Nazi Tiger tanks were technologically miles ahead of Soviet T-34s. But the Soviets mass-produced weapons in such horrendous quantities that sheer volume eventually crushed quality.)

Here, however, I will let you in on a small secret: the Western world is fully prepared for the latter problem, and addressing the former is currently a work in progress.

The East's True Leverage

So what, then, is the East’s true leverage?
If the West holds such immense superiority, why hasn’t it already overrun China and Russia, silencing the threats and forcing deals?
- Well, the issue lies in the sheer size of these nations. The West learned its bloodiest lesson in the Middle East (Iraq 2003, Libya 2011): overthrowing a regime takes 3 weeks, but the ensuing power vacuum results in civil wars lasting decades.
In the case of Russia and China, such a defeat would have exponentially more catastrophic consequences for the global order.

Russia possesses roughly 5,580 nuclear warheads. The Pentagon-affiliated RAND Corporation’s official 2023 report, “Avoiding a Long War,” clearly states: it is not in the US’s interest to have a highly destabilized, disintegrating Russia, because that would lead to nuclear chaos and weapons falling into the hands of warlords (like Kadyrov).
If the central authority in Beijing collapses, 1.4 billion people would plunge into chaos. A Chinese civil war would send tens of millions fleeing across the Pacific as refugees. Furthermore, China is the factory of the world: if a power vacuum emerges there, global supply chains (raw materials for pharmaceuticals, electronics, chips, batteries) would collapse within 48 hours and fall into the crosshairs of local terror groups. This would trigger an immediate, prolonged global economic depression, alongside new regional wars, the rise and fall of terror syndicates, and the toppling of regimes. Paradoxically, the West actually needs strong, centralized leadership in Moscow and Beijing with whom they can negotiate. Hence, they must necessarily keep Putin in power.
(It is no coincidence that Western intelligence warned Putin about Prigozhin’s coup attempt via Turkey. By doing so, they covered their own backs while simultaneously executing an intelligence power move—proving to Moscow that the West sees what is happening inside Russia’s borders better than the Kremlin itself does).

Both China and Russia know this perfectly well and weaponize it as leverage at the negotiating table. It is akin to a shrewd, cunning negotiator who, after being soundly defeated at the table, plays his final card by revealing a bomb strapped to his chest. This is exactly why Trump is applying a strategy of slow economic bleeding against China, while maneuvering Putin into a position that is disadvantageous for him in the long run.
He wants to systematically dismantle their alliance networks step by step and pulling them to his side, ensuring that in the event of a frontal conflict, the fallout from a humanitarian crisis and a power vacuum is mitigated and kept under control.

The Real Risk

The only problem is that the Eastern methodology is, unfortunately, affecting Western societies. Under the weight of disinformation and war psychosis, a significant portion of Western society finds it unacceptable for the West to stand up for itself against the East, and they want to oust the leaders undertaking this effort. There is no grand conspiracy behind this—it is simple risk aversion and willful blindness.
Among other things, this is why there is such a desperate push to remove Trump from power at all costs, to which Trump responds by bringing institutions under his direct control. This move is deeply misunderstood by many, even in Europe, due to our historical traumas involving autocrats and dictators, causing people to distance themselves from him to some extent.

However, I strongly think, that the reality is that Trump is not an autocrat; he is a highly impatient and aggressive politician who possesses a razor-sharp understanding of foreign policy games. Regardless of personal sympathy, this point must be acknowledged. Autocracy is not part of American culture anyway, unlike the Eurasian world.
If the West were to back down now, the consequences would be dire, driving the Western world straight into vassalage. And had the West naively done nothing—as it did in the past—by the time it woke up, the snake would have already coiled around it, leaving it in an abysmal negotiating position. So, there was no other choice. Trump chose the lesser evil.
The crisis outlined in January would not merely reboot the economy; it would also wrest control from the mega-capitalists who allowed this state of vulnerability to fester. Both the American and European economies possess ample reserves to weather the risks of stagflation and deflation, and moreover, Europe is highly diversified.

Could World War III Break Out?

Well, there are two answers to this: one is yes, and the other is no.
Yesterday, I wrote an article about normalcy bias, in which I noted that escalation into a massive war is never the result of conscious decision-making, but rather a chain of irrational processes that slip out of the control of decision-makers. If one side—especially the blackmailing aggressor—crosses a red line at a certain point (e.g., attacking a NATO member) from which the West can no longer retreat, an unstoppable escalation could trigger, inevitably culminating in a great power war.

It is a strange paradox that a world war can only be avoided if the West calls the bluff, refuses to be scared of its own shadow, and opts for ironclad deterrence.
Autocratic regimes like today’s Russia and China have, according to the lessons of history, only ever understood raw power. If they see that their blackmail simply bounces off the West, and that NATO is fully willing to unleash its devastating conventional economic and military superiority, the Eastern dictatorships will slowly back down and be forced to strike a deal, because they too want to survive. If it still happens, God grant that the Garabandal promise be true.
The only question is whether the West will wait for that specific red line to be crossed or not.
Trump will not.
And in this regard, in my view, he is right…
