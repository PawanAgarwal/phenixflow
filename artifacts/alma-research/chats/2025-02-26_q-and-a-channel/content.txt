2025-02-26T22:11:07.323Z | Alma
RV should be up when spot goes down.
Okay, so ... the key takeaway from my post about volatility here is that volatility is proportional to liquidity. Bcs vol is the standard deviation of the price returns.
RV usually goes down when spot goes up, 1) bcs returns are log scaled, 2) bcs plp are usually selling the right wing of the skew and buying the left wing, means buying downside puts, shorting upside calls... or in other term: shorting risk reversal. This is key bcs risk reversal is a synthetic stock trade, mainly bcs its PnL diagram is the same as having a stock position.
In this case, customers are SHORT synthetic stocks. On a net basis of course. Why? - Bcs they hedge their LONG stock position in the options market.

2025-02-26T22:15:00.741Z | Alma
Now this have a reflexive effect, bcs market makers who take the other side of the trade, short those OTM (downside) puts, and long the upside (OTM) calls.
so they are longer gamma up and shorter gamma down (positive speed profile).
Now, what this causes is that whenever the spot grinds higher, it faces increased liquidity as a result of MM hedging activity. They sell the rips and buy the dips when they are net long gamma.
This means, that whenever a plp are buying the stocks pushing the price higher, there will be increased amount of seller at those levels, as MMs have to sell.
So the demand side must balance the supply side to the upside in order to push the price higher.

2025-02-26T22:15:54.732Z | Alma
And this compresses the RETURNS of the price action (I showed on a chart whats the diff between return and price action in my Volatility post, check it).
So the volatility formula will provide a lower value

2025-02-26T22:21:13.506Z | Alma
However to the downside, this is the opposite here, bcs to the downside there is lower long gamma exposure (shorter gamma), and at some point MM exposure even switches to negative gamma.
What this means in practice, that: if the demand side is not enough on the stockmarket to rebalance the supply side from MMs (not solely them, but now lets keep it simple), plp stop-losses, margin lvls will be triggered and need to unwind their positions.
And as they sell, MMs have less and less long gamma, meaning, they have to buy those sell orders, but in a declining volume. So the liquidity decreases.
And when spot reaches MM firms net zero gamma line, they switch to negative gamma, where they have to hedge via selling into dips and buying into rips.

What this causes is, that MMs will be on the SAME SIDE that where the stocktraders are, so the counter-side, the opposite side who is needed to fill a sell or buy order is decreased... in otherwords the liquiditiy decreased significantly, and price needs to move a lot to find where sellers can sell their exposures.

2025-02-26T22:21:49.524Z | Alma
And this expands the RETURNS of the price action, so the volatility formula will give you a higher number.

2025-02-26T22:24:14.151Z | Alma
Implied volatility is a different thing. Implied volatility is a bet basically on the ranges of returns into the future. This determines the risk premium on the options. Higher IV = higher premium, lower IV = lower premium.
IV is basically the odds that the contract finishes ITM.
Higher demand on a certain contract = higher premium = higher IV, and vice versa.

2025-02-26T22:29:43.389Z | Alma
And look here: if customers sell upside calls, they lower those call IVs (selling vega / selling vol),
and as they buy downside puts, they bid those put IVs (long vega / long vol).
And this makes the vol smile steeper on the left wing, and flatter on the right wing.
So when the spot "slides" (on the x-axis) from the middle (ATM) to the left, meaning spot falls, the IV will increase, bcs we have bid IV values to the left.
But if the spot slides from the middle to the right side, the IV will decrease, bcs call vols are sold.

2025-02-26T23:03:34.652Z | Alma

2025-02-26T23:08:50.034Z | Alma
Lets say, market opens and starts to go down towards the downside sigma lvl. You see that there is local negative speed, meaning gamma is increasing. Momentum slows down, vol goes down.

Now, after the open, when traders start to take positions, vol is high, gamma is juiced. Plp are rushing into long puts, long downside vol, and the more the market goes down and down, they fomoing in even more.
There is no real edge here anymore, bcs the IV is bid, odds are high on that downside trade.
You have to wait.
You wait until it hits the threshold where you expect mean reversion first bcs it is an extreme, second, bcs the positioning says so. Vol starts to go down, traders start to sell their puts.
But nobody is buying calls. Their IV is very low.
You buy it, and market bounces. Your premium spikes.

It is not always this simple, but this is the main idea of mean reversion trading. You buy, when the odds are low on a trade, not when everybody and their mom is buying that bet, bcs not good risk/reward. I showed it mathematically what it means.

2025-02-27T22:36:16.595Z | Alma
It comes from the delta hedging activity.
If MM is long vanna = vol up/delta up, vol down/delta down for him.
You can have vanna flip around the middle of a local long fly pattern, or short fly pattern.
The difference is that in a long fly pattern MM has positive vanna convexity,
while in a short fly patter, MM has negative vanna convexity.

In positive vanna convexity, you have to see vol down in order to break through the zero vanna,
while in negative vanna convexity, you need vol up.

The reason is bcs in a long fly pattern, the speed is shorter to the middle, so is the vanna (to keep it simple).
So mechanically you will naturally see vol up towards the center from any direction. This is what the vanna also dictates.
Bcs if you move towards the center, MM becomes shorter gamma. It is below the center, MM has short vanna = vol up/delta down,
but above the center MM has long vanna = vol up/delta up.
So whenever the spot moves towards the center, vanna forces MMs to buy into the direction of the momentum, and this increases the vol.

2025-02-27T22:38:14.059Z | Alma
However on the otherside of the flip, everything changes.
Bcs if you had long vanna above, if spot moves down towards the center of the fly (assuming long fly), vol increases - delta increases, MMs need to sell more. But below the vanna flip, they become short vanna = vol up /delta down, so suddenly they start to face delta crush, so they buyback hedges.

INTRADAY REALIZED VOL SCRIPT:

//@version=5
indicator("GKYZ Volatility", shorttitle="GKYZ Vol. Est.", overlay=false)

lookback = input.int(5, title="Lookback Period", minval=1)  

f_calcDailyRV() =>

    window          = lookback
    trading_periods = 390

    log_hoD = math.log(high / open)
    log_loD = math.log(low  / open)
    log_coD = math.log(close / open)

    log_ocD    = math.log(open / nz(close[1]))
    log_oc_sqD = log_ocD * log_ocD

    log_ccD    = math.log(close / nz(close[1]))
    log_cc_sqD = log_ccD * log_ccD

    rsD = log_hoD*(log_hoD - log_coD) + log_loD*(log_loD - log_coD)

    close_volD = math.sum(log_cc_sqD, window) / (window - 1)
    open_volD  = math.sum(log_oc_sqD, window) / (window - 1)
    window_rsD = math.sum(rsD, window)        / (window - 1)
    kD         = 0.34 / (1.34 + (window + 1.0)/(window - 1.0))
    rvD        = math.sqrt((open_volD + kD*close_volD + (1 - kD)*window_rsD) * trading_periods)

dailyRV = request.security(syminfo.tickerid, "1", f_calcDailyRV())

plot(dailyRV, title="GKYZ Volatility", color=#970ef3, linewidth=2)

so it estimates that your returns come from one of two hidden regimes.
defined by their own Gaussian distribution. I guess you have read my post about them.
The means and variances tell you what the typical return and volatility are in each regime. For example, if state 1 has a mean of –0.00025, this means that when the model is in state 1, the typical avg return is –0.025% per observation.
Then we have the variance of returns within that state. A very low variance means the returns in that state are very consistent, while a high variance would mean they fluctuate a lot.
The state sequence is all in state 1 means that the model finds no evidence of switching regimes during the analysed (1week, 1hourly candles) period.
And also we have a transition probability matrix, that tells me the probability of staying in the same state or switching to the other state at the next time step.

What is IV? (in short)
It is the volatility risk premium on the contract. Indirectly reflecting the supply/demand of that contract.
Bcs the higher the demand for a certain contract, the more people are betting on that contract going ITM into its expo.
And if the demand grows, the price increases. Why? bcs the seller will have loss on that contract if the buyer wins. So he increases the price.
And bcs volatility is the SD of the logreturns, the higher the vol, the higher range of returns are expected on that contract.
So the higher risk the sellers has that the contract will expire ITM. So he will ask for higher premia.

What we traders are interested in in terms of skew, is its relative changes.
You chose the tenor, or date you are interested in, and check how the skew looked like yesterday, 1 weekago, after the last OpEx, vs today etc.
And you can check where the sentiment goes.
We usually meassure the slopeness of the skew by simply taking the 22 delta put vs ATM segment, or 5 delta put vs 22 delta put, and the 22 delta call vs ATM, and the ATM itself...
of course, some plp use the 25 deltas, others the 20 deltas instead of 22 delta... again instead of 5 deltas one might use the 10 deltas...
(I prefer the 22 delta, bcs thats where vanna peaks in avg, and 5 delta, bcs that is used many times for vega strats when hedging tails)

And so by comparing how the slopeness of these segments are changed over the time, you will get an idea about the supply/demand of the customer flow.

If you chose multi expiry chart, thats basically the 3D chart
