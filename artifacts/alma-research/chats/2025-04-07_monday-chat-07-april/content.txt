Grok:

In the context of options trading, "0DTE" refers to options that expire on the same day they are traded (0 Days To Expiration). Convexity, in this case, relates to how the option's price (or its sensitivity to the underlying asset's price) changes as the underlying price moves. Specifically, it’s tied to the concept of "gamma," which measures the rate of change of an option’s delta (the sensitivity of the option’s price to the underlying asset’s price).
What Does "0DTE Has More Convexity" Mean?
High Gamma: 0DTE options have extremely high gamma because they are so close to expiration. Gamma is highest when an option is at-the-money (ATM) and near expiration. This means that the delta of a 0DTE option can change very rapidly with even small movements in the underlying asset’s price.
More Convexity: Convexity here refers to the curvature of the option’s price function. High gamma implies greater convexity, meaning the option’s price reacts more dramatically (non-linearly) to price changes in the underlying asset. For 0DTE options, this effect is amplified because there’s no time left for the option to "smooth out" its behavior—every tick in the underlying price has an outsized impact on the option’s value.
In simpler terms: 0DTE options are hypersensitive to price movements in the underlying asset, especially when near the strike price. This hypersensitivity is what people mean by "more convexity."
When Is This Useful?
0DTE options’ high convexity can be advantageous (or risky) depending on your trading strategy. Here are some scenarios where it might be useful:
Speculating on Short-Term Moves:
If you expect a significant price swing in the underlying asset (e.g., due to an earnings report, economic data release, or market event) within the day, 0DTE options can offer massive leverage. A small move in the underlying can lead to a disproportionately large gain due to the high gamma/convexity.
Example: Buying a 0DTE call option on a stock right before a positive news event could yield a huge return if the stock jumps.
Day Trading:
Traders who thrive on volatility and quick in-and-out trades love 0DTE options. The high convexity means they can capture big profits (or losses) in a short time frame without needing to hold a position overnight.
Hedging Intraday Risk:
If you have a portfolio or position exposed to sudden intraday price swings, 0DTE options can act as a precise hedge. Their sensitivity allows you to offset losses quickly, though this comes with the cost of rapid time decay (theta).
Exploiting Mispricing:
In highly liquid markets (like SPX or SPY options), 0DTE options are heavily traded, and their prices can sometimes deviate from theoretical values due to supply/demand imbalances. A trader with a keen eye for these inefficiencies can use the convexity to amplify profits on small corrections.
The Flip Side: Risks
Time Decay (Theta): 0DTE options lose value extremely fast as expiration approaches. If the underlying doesn’t move enough, the option can expire worthless.
High Volatility: The same convexity that makes them profitable also makes them dangerous. A small adverse move can wipe out the position just as quickly.
Execution Risk: With such rapid price changes, getting in and out at the right price can be tricky, especially in fast-moving markets.
Practical Example
Imagine you buy a 0DTE at-the-money call option on SPY (S&P 500 ETF) with a strike of $500 when SPY is at $500. If SPY moves to $501 in the next hour, the option’s price might jump from, say, $1 to $3 due to the high gamma—far more than a longer-dated option would move. That’s convexity in action. But if SPY stays flat or drops, that $1 could evaporate to $0 by day’s end.
In summary, 0DTE’s high convexity is useful when you’re confident in a sharp, immediate move in the underlying asset and can stomach the risk. It’s a tool for precision and aggression, often favored by experienced traders in volatile, short-term setups.
