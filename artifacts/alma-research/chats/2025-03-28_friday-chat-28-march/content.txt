2025-03-28T18:41:23.236Z | Doc McGraw
You're right about the repelling effect. Let me explain it correctly:

For SPX at 5580 with 40,000 customer long/dealer short puts at 5565 expiring in 3 days, the strike acts as a repellent rather than a pin.

When dealers are short puts at 5565, their hedging behavior creates a repelling force:

- If SPX moves down toward 5565, dealers must sell more futures/stocks to hedge (becoming shorter delta as puts gain delta), pushing the price even lower and away from the strike
- If SPX moves up away from 5565, dealers buy back their hedges (reducing short exposure as puts lose delta), accelerating the upward move

This creates a "repelling" effect around large put positions, where price movement tends to accelerate away from the strike rather than pin to it. The large gamma exposure at 5565 acts as an "anti-pin" or repellent point.

The market can appear to "bounce off" these levels rather than cluster around them, especially with very large positions that create significant dealer gamma imbalances.

Here's a strat you all may like in high vol times: butterflies. When vol is high, butterflies get pretty cheap because they have to account for the increased risk that price moves all the way through it to the other side. This lets you get pretty big ranges - like 20pts a side (40 wide), for what would normally be the cost of a 20-wide.
Then, if towards the end of day price is above/below the range, you can often naked call/put in between. So you do a 10x butterfly and a 1x call/put. If the price moves against your naked call/put, it then moves into a gigantic profit pool. You just have to hope it doesn't keep moving all the way through it.
Not finance advice, but something to consider
