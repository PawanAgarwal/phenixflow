In short, and over-simplified:
Options is a bet that the spot will reach the strike before the option expires.

The idea of the pricing model is based on determining the probability that the option ends up ITM.
The option premium has two components: intrinsic and extrinsic value.
The sum of this two will give you the premium.

The intrinsic value is simply the strike-spot difference. But it is always non-negative.
If your call strike is 100
and the spot is trading at 90
your intrinsic value is zero, bcs if you'd exercise the call, you would have to buy it above its spot price that is not a good deal.
But if spot is at 110,
your intrinsic value is $10, bcs in this case you can buy the underlying $10 cheaper than its market value.

everything else is the extrinsic value.
So, if your call strike is 100
the spot is 110
and you see that the call premium is $11.58
this means that the extrinsic value is 11.58 - 10 = 1.58

Extrinsic value is the time and volatility value.
It is the probability of your contract landing ITM or not.

DELTA

In math the symbol of delta is used to express "change".
Something changes relative to something.

In finance delta expresses the change of an asset price relative to another asset. it can be literally anything you want to meassure.
This is delta. Nothing more, nothing less.
If you want to compare a stock to itself it is one delta bcs a stock price is always equvivalent with itself. This is why MMs like to call them "one delta assets".

In the option pricing formula, delta is a number expressing the change of the options premium with respect to changes in the underlying asset's price.
The idea is to see how the premium will react to the spot changes.
Originally the delta is in the [0; 1] interval, but bcs a contract covers 100 options, broker platforms multiply the delta by 100, and we say i.e. "22 delta" instead of "0.22 delta"

If your delta is 1, this means that if the spot moves up (increases by a positive number), you simply add the delta to your options premium. If spot moves down (increased by a negative number), you extract the delta value from your options premium.
If you have delta of -1, you do the same, but the effect will be reversed bcs extracting a negative number is addition, and adding a negative number is extraction.

In this case you'll have positive delta.

If you bet on the opposite, so that the underlying will go down, you long a put,
so you will have negative delta.
Why? bcs your option premium will gain value if spot moves down, and lose value if spot moves up.

GAMMA

Gamma is the rate of change in the delta with respect to spot.
Basically the delta of delta.
Gamma can also be negative and positive.

Lets give you an example:
We have an XY stock currently trading at 100

Now lets bet on this that this stock will go up to 110.
I long a call with strike of 110.

Lets say I have delta of 20
If spot moves up, I add the positive 20 to my premium, so my premium will increase.
If spot moves down, I extract the positive 20 from my premium, so my premium will decrease.

But how will the delta change?
(Now, I simply this for you guys, I want to make you understand the probability factor here)
If spot moves up, the probability of my contract ending ITM will increase, so my delta should increase too. Logic.
If spot moves down, the probability of my contract ending ITM will decrease, so my delta should decrease too.
This means gamma must be a positive number. Bcs if I add it, it increases the value, and if I extract it, it decreases the value.

2025-02-05T11:18:35.710Z | Alma
Now same underlying trading at 100, but we now bet on that this will go down to 90.
We long a put, we have lets say -20 deltas.

What is gamma here?
If spot moves up, the probability of this contract ending ITM will decrease, so my delta should also goes towards zero, but MY DELTA IS A NEGATIVE NUMBER!!!
So if my delta goes from -22 to -18, it is an increase!! A decrease of its absolute value, but it is an increase, bcs you go right on the number line!! Keep this in mind. (for advanced members: "longer delta")

And if spot moves down, the probability of this contract ending ITM will increase, so my delta should goes farther from zero, but again, my delta here is a negative number,
so if my delta goes from -22 to -30, it is decrease!! bcs we moved left on the number line.

And bcs we are longer delta up, and shorter delta down, this is again positive gamma.

CHARM

As I explained earlier, as time passes the probability will change for having intrinsic value or not.
Or in otherwords: if my delta is above 50 (ITM), or lower-equals 50 (ATM/OTM)

Time passing is a positive vector, so we handle it as a positive number, there is no negative time = time doesn't goes back... sadly.
So we handle delta the same way.

If my contract is ITM, the probability will increase by the time, that it will stay ITM into expo, so my delta must increase.
If my contract is ATM or OTM, the probability will increase that it remains so as time passes, so my delta must decrease.

This means that my delta is positive for ITM contracts, and negative for ATM and OTM contracts.
Bcs if you add a positive number as time passes, you becomes longer delta,
and if you add a negative number to your premium as time passes, you becomes shorter delta.

This is charm.
(Of course, if somebody short a contract, it is again the reverse, bcs he will profit from the premium of the contract he shorted decays away. This will be an important layer later when I'll talk about dealers hedging)

VANNA

(I won't talk about it that much now, bcs vanna is crazy compex and conditional. But I want you to understand it to from this probability perspective, bcs beyond any sophisticated smartass speech, thats the goal at the end of the day)

 So as time passes and the spot is trading in OTM territory your delta decays by gamma and charm, as explained above.
However, I also said, that in this case your last savior is volatility. Bcs if the price starts to move in a wider range, and the rate of returns go higher, this is good for you, bcs it gives you hope that your contract will go ITM in time.

So if vol increases for your OTM contract, your delta will increase, bcs probability expressed by the extrinsic value, will increase.
However, if even vol goes down, sadly, the your last hope is also gone, so your delta will decrease, as the probability will decrease.

Different picture if the spot is trading in ITM territory.
With time passing, your chances will increase that it will finish ITM.
But then comes volatility.
You don't want big price swings here, bcs your spot can leave the ITM territory, and then bye-bye for intrinsic value.
(The upside move doesn't matter that vol gives you, bcs of the log scaling showed above. While the downside negative returns increasing, so they have way much bigger weight)

So if volatility increases for an ITM contract, your delta must go down a bit, bcs the odds are not in your favor.
But if vol decreases, then your chances are better that the spot won't go out of the ITM territory, so your delta can stay as it is, and even increase, as odds are in your favor now with low vol.

This is the key concept for vanna.
So for OTM contract vanna is positive: vol up/delta up, vol down/delta down
And for ITM contracts vanna is negative: vol down/delta up, vol up/delta down

One last thing I want to clarify before showing you the OptionsDepth Heatmap finally.
It is the change in gamma.

Just think about it logically:
I said, returns are scaling logarithmically.
So if the spot is already trading in the ITM territory, the further upsward movements shouldn't have the same weight as they did when the contract was OTM, right?
So the delta will increase, sure, but not that much. Less and less, as spot moves up, when the contract is already ITM.
What is the delta change to spot? It is gamma.
So what I'm talking about is that the rate of change in delta will go lower, as spot moves up in ITM territory.
In otherwords: gamma will decrease.
And this rate of change in gamma with respect to spot, is called SPEED.
And for now, you can also say that speed is negative for an ITM contract, bcs it lowers the gamma as spot moves up, and increases gamma as spot moves down.

And whats the thing with OTM contracts?
Well, think deductically here.
If we got the conclusion that gamma increases for OTMs when spot moves up, bcs the contract has increased probability to reach ITM territory, so delta goes up, and the closer it gets to ITM, the faster the delta changes,
this means that gamma value also increases when contract approaches ITM territory.
Now, if this is the case (and it is), then it also means, that if you go backwards, it is the opposite: if something increases into a way, it will decreases to the other way.
 So speed is positive for OTM contracts.
