2025-02-08T14:18:26.645Z | Riaan Lourens
Very much respect your opinion and appreciate all your posts, and I hope you don’t mind me jumping in and adding another perspective.

I use both OD and ConvexValue, and I find them to be very complimentary but it’s an apples to oranges comparison in respect to which is “better”.

OD provides a TON of value with their out of the box functionality, namely the calculation of the MM book/positions/gex as well as their visual maps of gamma and charm.  It’s very intuitive and easy to understand, and it’s a very active community.  It’s well worth the money, but it’s limited to spx (which, for me, is ideal).

ConvexValue on the other hand is effectively an application that lets the user view/graph/display/etc realtime option data across many many tickers, futures, vix, etc etc etc.  They calculate all the Greeks, volatility, buy/sell data, etc on the fly and provide access to that data and more (from memory, well over 100 parameters).  For example, a user can easily watch the buying and selling across any strike and any/many expiration in real time, and it’s visually easy to see (but the user will have a learning curve on how to do it). Or, charting volatility, etc.  You have to create your own “dashboards” based on the data that you want to see.  They also have a “map”, but it’s based on buy/sell volume vs. ODs approach (MM positioning and the potential impact of gamma/charm).

In short, OD provides targeted value and easy to interpret spx positional data and mapping of gamma and charm.  Super helpful.  ConvexValue on the other hand provides a suite of tools to interrogate, view and query many many data points related to options as it relates to Greeks/volatility, buy/selling, etc.  An important difference is that OD provides the specific positions of customers and MMs, whereas convex provides standard OI but processes data “live” and provides all the data as close to realtime as what retail can get, at least at this point.  Convex’s API is amazing, as a side.

Apologies for the long message, but wanted to make sure people understand it’s two totally different tools but highly complimentary IMO.

2025-02-09T00:37:25.272Z | Alma
in very simplified terms for now:
what is vanna? delta change to vol.
positive vanna = positive correlation (vol up/delta up, vol down/delta down)
negative vanna = negative relationship (vol up/delta down, vol down/delta up)

if something goes from positive to negative and vice versa, it must have a zero too.
thats the flip point.

if we are in positive vanna regime, it also means positive speed, where MMs short downside gamma+vega, and long upside gamma+vega
(bcs vanna is vega convexity too, vega change to spot, the delta of vega)
Now, in this regime if spot moves down, MMs are shorter vol, so they buy vol, bidding up IV
20-22delta strikes.

logical: vol up/delta up... if dealers are longer delta, they must sell futs, that increases vol.
also bcs it is shorter donwisde gamma so less resistance from gamma = more vol.

2025-02-09T00:37:26.801Z | Alma
but

2025-02-09T00:38:23.596Z | Alma
when spot reaches zero vanna line (that is not a stationary line but an oscillating territory),
it all shifts.
now vanna works reversely.
so the higher the vol, the lower the delta will be pushed

2025-02-09T00:38:40.688Z | Alma
bcs spot went to negative vanna territory

2025-02-09T00:38:54.837Z | Alma
and if MMs are shorter delta, they buy back

2025-02-09T00:39:59.763Z | Alma
this is the mechanical dynamics.
if an anomaly happens, that volatility gets dumped at the zero vanna territory, where it switches from positive to negative, then the lower the vol the higher the delta in negative vanna territory, MMs remain longer delta = selling

2025-02-09T00:40:31.980Z | Alma
opposite happens, when the vanna flips leads from negative to positive.
there vol has to be up in order to break above the vanna flip
