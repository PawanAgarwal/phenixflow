@Alma - Thank you again for taking the time to respond in such detail the other day. Your posts and comments always make me think, wonder, challenge the how/why/reasoning, bridge macro perspectives, etc.  That thread is a fantastic example of that…

Regarding your point on "whether the positioning is self fulfilling at some level", yes, this is in part what I was trying to explore myself when I read your statements of fundamentals/liquidity vs. option market positioning.

Maybe at the core of some of the points from the thread (using your analogy of the options market being a skewed galdon board):  the initial positioning of the pegs on a skewed galdon board is based on expected liquidity in the future (e.g. expectations for the next trading session as an example), but the “read” or interpretation of that board across the market by traders may indirectly or even directly create much higher or lower liquidity at certain levels (via MM hedging, traders reacting to perceived options market positioning, etc.) during trading hours.  But as you say, a large percentage (or even majority?) of the traders must believe in the dynamics of a certain area/level to truly get the liquidity that may of justified the initial expectation.

So, chicken and the egg as you said.  One of your initial points though, which I inherently agree with, is that the options market isn’t the only market/lens that people should focus on and the over rotation of the BSM model (by retail, Furus, etc.) as almost the “bible” and inherent truth is quite dangerous.  It’s both a blessing and curse for retail though — at the surface, relying heavily on the perceived “edge” of BSM as the primary lens of the market (which, worse case, often times is naive, etc.) ultimately is very detrimental.  But, at the same time, using some of this data to get a “view” of the market expectations is inherently valuable vs. just relying on price action.  The trick though is to keep grounded on thinking through it with a stochastic expectation vs. a deterministic expectation.  Just because the position is X, doesn't mean it will always lead to Y.

But more broadly my inherent gut bias has always been that we don't always have a truly efficient market in the short tenures (like, on a day to day basis vs week to week or month to month) because of general reflexivity theory but also because of things like the mutual reinforcement (vs. pure chicken and egg) of how the market reacts to known/visible positions in the market.  Maybe in the longer time periods than a day or a week, yes, the market will prove itself is close to efficient and the fair value will be found (e.g, the AI bubble will eventually pop IMO) but people in mass trading based on the distribution and current dynamics (like, high gamma area), the expected behavior at levels (lower vol in the high gamma area), etc. ultimately changes human behavior.  A completely unrelated example would be like a restaurant — an owner needs to plan for staff + ingredients + timing based on expectations for the next day.  Its human nature for customers to go check what is going on at that restaurant if there is a long line of people standing out the door at 6 PM (to which the owner likely planned for) vs. customers walking into the restaurant next door.  People will often times even stand in line and pay more without even knowing if its worth the wait + price… but sometimes, that owner’s expectations/planning goes completely out the window because there is unexpected snow (or worse, a fire outside) and therefor none of his/her planning has any effect and a lot of raw ingredients goes to waste -- and all the original customers went to a place where there is shelter.  Not quite the same, but something like that, lol :)

That all said, one of my own lessons that I need to keep myself honest with every time I engage with the market, is to effectively force my brain to separate “analysis” vs. “execution”.  When I do a post-mortem on a bad trade, the HUGE majority of time is because it is emotionally tied to a bias related to analysis (like a form of prediction based on analysis bias) vs. just keeping execution simple and bound by rules (e.g. sizing, react to price movement that validates the original analysis vs. indirectly predicting based on analysis).  Your point on randomness and the path it will take very much lands with me.
For example, I have many of my own code/scripts covering everything from vol surface, BSM modeling, BSM analysis, market analysis, yield analysis, trend lines, etc. etc.  It's important for me to do the work to at least understand or try to understand — but the over reliance on any of my + your data + OD + whatever service ultimately doesn’t change poor execution.  I tend to spend a lot of time doing that work to just reverse engineer the "why",  but for me to really make sense of it all, I would need to quit my day job to be able to focus on it all.

Sorry for the long winded answer, but part of the reason you have such a fantastic following and service is that you do not focus on trade guidance or just provide naive bullshit… but because of the combination of your willingness to share your read of the tape & market and describe the distribution/dynamics WHILE teaching/guide in way that constantly challenges each person to try to understand.  Sometimes you have to spoon feed your audience, but your approach is very effective.   If you are ever willing to take suggestions for topics to go deeper into, let me know, as I have a laundry list of things that I would like to pick your brain on — inclusive of being interested in reading your chaos theory research :).   It is likely way out of my reach in terms of potential understanding, but the problem is with any of these topics, none of it is interesting unless I try to understand.

Anyway, thanks again for the thoughts the other day.  Much appreciated.

one thing...
this type of "self-fulfilling"-ness what you refer to is very likely a misconception, if i understand you correctly.
if i take it right, you mean the "herd mentality" jumping into the same trades.
Well, this is rather momentum-anomaly, than the effect of expected dsitribution.

this "conscious well-planned self-fulfilling" positioning is an illusion of literally what it is.
so, the dispersion is big. The way the positions are dispersed in the market is very huge, and even when it looks like the market agreed on a level, you can clearly see (even on OD maps) that those are rather "areas", "nodes" rather than straight bets.
and this amount of dispersion fluctuates across the levels, and there will be areas where the market happens to be net seller or net buyer of vol, in different amount.
and this creates the illusion of self-fulfilling-ness (if theres a word like this)

but the core is that billions of people computes the expected structure of the following day.
they scrutinize the same market, same historical datas, so they will get similar or the same results.
they place their bets, that will be more than less the same or very similar... on a net basis!
bcs we still have a lot of participants who sell calls in an area where the market is net long call.

And now if we go on this road, we would arrive to the conclusion that "ok but then the structure gets manifested by the positioning, the positioning generates more participants to accept the positioning further amplifying it, and finally the dealer flow that acts as a leverage to amplify the effect of the positioning, so it is self-fulfilling".
and for this case, we have a very nice word in german: "jein" (yes/no at the same time, ja+nein :D)

bcs positioning is already a reaction of the masses to the expected path of the liquidity thats already happening.
but the key is! listen, that there is a significant amount of time when the mass does not assume the next day well.
there are days when the positioning expects something totally different than what's happened, and panic selling, or panic buying kicks in.
and it happens more than 1-5% of times, so it is bigger than a 2 sigma event.

So yes, while it definitely has a very strong reflexive effect on the market movements, this effect is currently way overestimated. Ppl will see it during the next bearmarket.
This is why I like to consistently call it a "bet"... opposite to ppl who are sweating to try to pre-simulate the market move based on positioning solely.
