Before jump into Hormuz, let me once again highlight the three main risk flashpoints for Western markets and economies:     The Iranian conflict   In recent weeks I have placed increasing emphasis on the risks stemming from the Iranian situation. In Monday’s article I showed that the market is pricing in an attack by March, and that this represents a trap for Trump — one from which he can only emerge as a loser. The events have already set in motion a process that will lead to his personal downfall and a serious challenge to American hegemony.   I also demonstrated that the United States — under Israeli pressure — repeated the exact same intelligence failure here as in 1979, the one so thoroughly analysed by Robert Jervis. This is precisely the factor that the market has failed to price in adequately. The offensive will not deliver long-term results; instead, it will destabilise and radicalise the Middle East, directly damaging supply chains and shadow trade, pushing oil prices above $90-95, deepening Western stagflation and deflationary risks, and weakening the U.S. President’s negotiating leverage.   Once economic problems intensify, China will step in to create further disruptions along the critical First Island Chain trade route, ahead of any move on Taiwan.      The AI/technology bubble   A significant portion of current AI and tech developments is unnecessary and will eventually face regulatory constraints. The overbuilding is being financed largely through debt and government subsidies. As I wrote last year and again in January, NVIDIA and TSMC will be the leading indicators of the bubble’s end.   In Wednesday’s article I showed that signs of delays and overspending are already visible at NVIDIA, while at TSMC — although the official data still look solid — Wall Street is already expecting slippage and slowdown in the second quarter. When this materialises, the AI bubble will burst.      Japanese yen carry-trade risk   Japan operates under an implicit “policy put” because it faces severe stagflationary and deflationary risks; therefore inflation will remain under strict control. The left tail of the carry trade has grown extremely fat. I flagged this risk already in December last year, well before Takaichi. And as I said then, I continue to expect that in the second half of the year the government will no longer be able to tolerate a persistently weak yen.   The market has priced this in to some extent, but it creates a synthetic short-gamma risk. Speculators are still trying to extract maximum profit from the situation in the meantime. Subscribe          Strait of Hormuz                 Currently, the most critical phase of the conflict is unfolding in the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman, primarily in the electromagnetic spectrum.   the IRGC is continuously broadcasting on open channels, declaring that “  the Strait of Hormuz is closed, no vessel may pass  .” Although this has not been officially declared, a de facto blockade is already in effect.   Large-scale military GPS jamming and widespread AIS spoofing are ongoing. Phantom vessels are appearing on ship radars, dramatically increasing the risk of collisions and the chance that commercial ships could be misidentified by naval forces and engaged.   According to my sources, more than 450 oil and gas tankers (LNG/LPG), 200 bulk carriers, and 170 container ships are currently stranded in the Gulf. Bloomberg data supports these figures.                                          Importantly, the vessels are not stopping solely because they cannot physically transit the strait. Many are being held back due to charterparty terms, banking requirements, P&I and war risk insurance conditions, as well as port and flag-state restrictions. This has created a de facto standstill in maritime traffic. Transit has become economically and legally impossible without proper insurance coverage.   However, due to radio jamming and spoofed signals, accurate real-time data is not reaching Bloomberg or other market intelligence platforms. As a result, the geo-risk premium on oil and gas products has spiked by as much as 50%.    According to leaked diplomatic reports, regional leaders have warned Washington that oil prices above $100 now represent a “clear and present danger” — despite OPEC+’s decision to increase production, because the physical supply remains locked inside the Gulf.   Additionally, Gulf states have halted put trading on exchanges until Tuesday across the GCC.     The second key flashpoint is the Saudi oil refineries in the KSA, which Iranian forces are repeatedly attempting to reach. Both are high-beta risks, but the second one in particular carries extreme danger, as it could take the Petroline pipeline out of commission entirely.   If refinery gets hit, it can slowdown the oil price increase, but generate a spike in gas prices.
