Otherwise, target is 6585    PCE    While GDP was revised down, the headline PCE came in at 0.3% month-over-month and 2.8% year-over-year, while the core PCE was 0.4% month-over-month and 3.1% year-over-year. Personal income grew by 0.4%, DPI by 0.9%, nominal consumption rose by 0.4%, but real PCE only by 0.1%; the savings rate jumped to 4.5%.                                         From the Fed’s perspective, this is important because the January CPI for the same month showed only 2.4% headline and 2.5% core, while the PCE indicates 2.8% / 3.1%: the CPI’s softer message was overly optimistic for the Fed.                                        The headline easing was mainly driven by energy, which fell 1.7% month-over-month in January and became negative by 0.8% year-over-year as well, while goods prices flattened to 0.0% m/m, but services prices rose 0.4% month-over-month and 3.5% year-over-year. Even the market-based core PCE is 0.4% m/m and 2.8% y/y, so the stickiness isn’t just coming from imputed items. Consumption is clearly services-led: nominally, services added +105.7 billion dollars, while goods subtracted -24.6 billion; in real terms, goods -0.4%, services +0.3%                                        I wouldn’t read the 0.9% jump in DPI as too strong a demand signal either. In January, social security benefits rose due to the January indexing, personal taxes fell on a monthly basis, while real personal income excluding transfers grew only by 0.2%. In other words, household cash flow improved, but the pure demand impulse was moderate. If we annualize the November–January monthly trajectory, the headline PCE ran at about 3.7%, the core PCE at about 4.1% run-rate — that’s still far from the 2% target.  However, the oil and gasoline shock after February 28 isn’t included in the PCE. As I’ve been saying since this Substack started, a new wave of inflation is coming.
