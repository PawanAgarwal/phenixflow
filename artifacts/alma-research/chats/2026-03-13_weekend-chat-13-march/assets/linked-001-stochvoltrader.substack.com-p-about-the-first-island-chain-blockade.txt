If an agreement cannot be reached in the war (and it likely won’t be), China will slowly scale up to a blockade of the First Island Chain, which is the most critical node for the Western economy in terms of supply chains. I already wrote about this last year. The frog is already boiling.    Ship movements visible on radars have drastically changed. During the recent military exercises, “Justice Mission 2025” in December 2025 and “Joint Sword 2025-B” in early 2026, China openly simulated a blockade and the cutting off of energy imports.                                          This strangulation is not carried out by the gray hulls of the Navy (PLAN) in the inner waters, but rather by a deniable, multi-layered fleet consisting of intelligence vessels disguised as civilian fishing boats (which also form physical barriers) and militarized Coast Guard ships of several thousand tons (which serve to provide a law enforcement facade for customs inspections and vessel boardings—since China has extended its domestic maritime laws to the First Island Chain). Meanwhile, Chinese warships and aircraft carriers patrol the Pacific Ocean and critical chokepoints (e.g., the Bashi Channel). If a Western cargo ship refuses to stop for the Coast Guard, the maritime militia aggressively surrounds it. If it does stop, it   de facto   acknowledges China’s jurisdiction (norm creation). Lose-lose.   In this scenario, due to the constant harassment, radio threats, and “accidental” collisions, Lloyd’s of London and global insurers would classify the region as a high-risk zone (War Risk). Freight rates would skyrocket, causing a continuous, silent, artificially generated stagflation in Europe and the US. Due to the risks, major shipping companies (e.g., Maersk, MSC) would have to avoid the Chinese “control zones,” which could force them into long detours lasting up to a week (e.g., along the eastern side of the Philippines). The latest CSIS wargame report, titled “Lights Out?”, points out that the mere slowdown of shipments would lead to power outages and industrial shutdowns in the region within weeks, toppling Western markets like dominoes.   The problem is that since officially it is Coast Guard vessels taking action under the pretext of illegal fishing or environmental protection, Western navies cannot fire at them without appearing to be the aggressors themselves. International law is ill-equipped to deal with a quarantine.    Western strategists, of course, clearly see this strangulation. The US and the Philippines are rapidly militarizing the northern part of Luzon (with BrahMos and Typhon missile systems) to keep at least the southern exit of the First Island Chain open for Western trade.
