LISTEN,
Whenever you take a longer-term position (monthly or longer), always assume your profits.
But when you calculate your expected profits, always take into account the
1) Bid/ask spread
2) Fees
3) The taxes you will pay for that profit
4) Inflation (estimate the future value of your profit)
+1) check how much % could have been earned through treassury bonds (your country, tax-free), to check the risk-free profit

So, whenever you take a trade, you already in loss. And you need to take into account these to determine your target zones for exits and size-ups

2025-07-23T21:10:31.067Z | Alma
Also don't chase tops and bottoms.
Market is a stochastic system. All we have is a probability distribution, that can be derived from the options positioning.

Catching tops and bottoms may look virtuoso, but thats not the point of trading.
It is not about to play some sort of "guess the top" games, but to win more than lose.
Loss is a part of the game.
Place your bets, if you have a read. Determine the size through your confidence level. And when the move is confirmed by the structure, size up. If it is disproved, cut the loss as soon as possible. There is no point to sit on losses if the market doesn't want to give it to you.

(You saw me catching tops and bottoms many times, but not always. My strategy was always to determine the area for the reversion, and go for it, but never all-in, but scale in, if I have conviction. These areas were very near to tops and bottoms, sure, but I do this for mor than a decade, using algos and very accurate datas, and the main thing: my motivation was NEVER to catch the tops or bottoms. As the market is stochastic, whenever you catch it, it is only by fortune, nothing more. Everything else is furu lie. Take it)

2025-07-27T14:14:48.745Z | Doc McGraw
That statement is meaningless. It’s one of those market platitudes that sounds profound but falls apart under any analytical scrutiny.

First, markets are constructs, not entities with volition. Markets don’t “know” anything or have intentions - they’re just aggregation mechanisms for information flow and participant behavior. The anthropomorphization of markets leads to lazy thinking.

Second, because of how markets are interconnected - bond markets, stock markets, FX markets, commodity markets - which market are we even talking about at any given time? When we say “the market,” are we referring to equity markets reacting to bond volatility? FX moves driving commodity flows? The reflexivity between these markets means any single narrative about price movement is incomplete.

If anything, “the market” follows liquidity, which will be on the side of “the most.” Market makers and systematic flows gravitate toward where the most participants are positioned, but this creates fragility points that Taleb discusses in Antifragile. When everyone is on the same side of a trade, you get violent unwinds - not because the market “wants” to hurt people, but because of positioning dynamics and risk management protocols.

From a gamma exposure perspective, this is especially visible around major gamma levels. The market doesn’t “choose” to pin at max gamma - it’s the natural result of delta hedging flows. When those flows reverse (like when we flip from positive to negative gamma), the resulting volatility isn’t punitive - it’s just mechanical.

The statement essentially commits the narrative fallacy - imposing intentionality on what are really just emergent properties of complex systems and participant positioning.
