Spot up / vol up in this context just gives you the market dynamics you could expect and that you should consider in your trade structure. Like telling your friend to drive slow because there is ice on the street. That does not mean you cannot drive (or short vol), but you need to consider the environment before you get into the car. So this statement reduces a three-dimensional volatility surface (time, strike, IV) to a single observation. So we could assume a parallel shift up, that means IV rises by the same amount on all strikes and expiries (your two-dimensional smile shifts up by the same distance everywhere). In practice that does not happen like this. Skew rises or falls, 3d vol is bid more than 3M vol, etc... you catch my drift.  So there is a multitude of possibilities how the surface can move and for you to structure a trade around it. So let's just make an example up. We just sold off for a few days and therefore don't expect much more short term downside generally over the next week or two. So we might have a neutral to slightly bullish bias. Puts 2 weeks out seem to be rich compared to your forecast of declining realized vol. So in the simplest example I could short some SPX puts with a high vega (let's say slightly ITM) and scalp my gamma (ie delta hedge). We can also up this one notch. Maybe AAPL IV is rich compared to index, so I might go short AAPL puts instead (if I also think that AAPL will not sell of further). Once you have that trade on, the real fun starts.  The basic idea behind short vol is that you want to isolate your vegas from the rest of the Pnl. So practically speaking you short volatility and try to be as (delta) neutral as possible. So now let's say the spot moves against you and your deltas pick up (you are long delta and have negative gamma). So you need to reduce your long delta exposure. So you could just sell AAPL stock until your deltas are back in line with your risk appetite. If we are in a mean reversion environment, that means you will usually buy the top and short the hole. So with that in mind maybe you might give your deltas more leeway and only hedge at a critical point. Now let's turn this around. We are generally in a spot up/ vol up environment. So let's say spot goes in our favor and people start buying 0, 1, 2DTEs (let's assume they exist on AAPL). So short end vol is bid and you start losing on your vega a little bit. The 2W IV moves a little as well, but not as much. So you still decide to reduce your vega exposure before it explodes in your face. So you might opt to long a call. Since you don't want to hedge your vega completely you might opt for a call a little further out (maybe 2W as well) and OTM. So if the market rallies against your expectation the vanna will pick up and compensate (some of) the short vega. But let's suppose instead of AAPL you realize that you can get cheaper vegas in GOOGL, where vol seems underpriced in relation to the market. So you might put on a relative value trade and buy a slightly OTM call on GOOGL 2 weeks out. There is a lot of discretion and in the end all of this is more an art than a science and like everything in trading comes down to risk aversion, personal preference, etc.
