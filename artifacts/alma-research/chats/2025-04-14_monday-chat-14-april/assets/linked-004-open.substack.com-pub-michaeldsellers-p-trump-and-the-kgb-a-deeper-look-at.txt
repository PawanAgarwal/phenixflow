I have recently obtained a copy of the actual text of the letter and believe it is worthwhile to go through it carefully as we analyze the likelihood of KGB manipulation of Trump from 1987 onward. KGB “Active Measures” were directly tasked to shape Western and world opinion by every means available.  The  insertion of Kremlin-approved themes and narratives into Western discourse via agents of influence was a core part of the KGB’s mission. Such an agent might be fully witting of their control by KGB and might accept formal tasking from them as part of a quid pro quo “agent” relationship — or they might simply be artfully manipulated into carrying out Kremlin objectives through a combination of persuasion, appeal to ego, and other psychological manipulations with the ‘agent’ as a willing participant but remaining less than fully aware of how he was being manipulated.  With this in mind, Trump’s unusual act of taking out a paid advertisement to publish a letter that aligns so completely with Kremlin talking points is difficult to accept as anything other than an act that was, at a minimum, Kremlin-inspired by ideas that were undoubtedly pushed with Trump during his just -concluded visit to Moscow.    Thus analysis would conclude with a high degree of probability that Trump had been influenced to write the letter by interactions with various Soviet sources during his trip — intellectuals, government officials, politicians, all of whom would have been carefully selected by KGB and who would be acting to advance Soviet interests by promoting these same talking points while at the same time flattering Trump for his ‘brilliant insight’ that allows him to see things far more clearly than other American, etc, etc …  According to defectors like Yuri Bezmenov and documents from the Mitrokhin Archive, KGB recruitment and development tactics often involved appealing to personal ego and nationalist instincts—especially with influential Westerners. A common goal was to encourage disruptive voices to promote messages aligned with Soviet interests, even if they didn’t know they were doing so.  Trump’s ad campaign fits this model: an American business celebrity voluntarily spending personal funds to broadcast Kremlin-aligned messaging in top U.S. newspapers is precisely the kind of post-contact behavior the KGB sought from what they termed “active measures.”  The “Armand Hammer Template”                 Trump’s publication fo the letter doesn’t   prove   recruitment.  But I would argue that it proves beyond reasonable doubt that the KGB successfully manipulated Trump in 1987 to adopt and articulate positions that aligned with Kremlin talking points; and that they successfully inspired him to start taking concrete steps in the direction of a political career.     Further, to understand and contextualize the KGB’s  interest in Donald Trump, it helps to consider their decades-long relationship with   Armand Hammer  , the American industrialist and CEO of Occidental Petroleum, who maintained close ties with the Soviet Union from the 1920s through the 1980s. Hammer was not just a businessman; he was a conduit for informal diplomacy, backchannel communication, and economic collaboration between the USSR and the West. Soviet leaders trusted him, and the KGB viewed him as an invaluable asset who could blend capitalism with Kremlin interests.    When Trump emerged in the 1980s as a wealthy, publicity-hungry New York developer with political ambitions and no apparent ideological commitments, it’s easy to see why the KGB might have seen him as a potential   "next Hammer"  —a Western capitalist who could be courted, cultivated, and used as a channel for influence.   Like Hammer, Trump showed:    A desire for personal grandeur and elite status.    A fascination with Soviet power and prestige.    Willingness to engage in direct dialogue with Soviet officials.    An eagerness to project himself into international affairs despite no formal diplomatic or governmental role.     From the perspective of Soviet intelligence, cultivating Trump didn’t require him to be a secret agent or ideologue. It only required that he be   ambitious, flattered by attention, and willing to echo Kremlin-friendly narratives  . In this context, Trump wasn’t the first of his kind—but potentially the most useful since Armand Hammer.
